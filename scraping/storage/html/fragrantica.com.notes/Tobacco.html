Tobacco (Lat. Nicotiana) is a genus of plants of the solanaceous family (Lat. Solanaceae). This family is represented by such apparent tobacco relatives as tomato, eggplant, bell pepper (capsicum), potato, boxthorn (better known as goji berries) as well as some very poisonous plants such as belladonna, datura and henbane.
 
 
As the majority of solanaceous plants, tobacco was brought to Europe from the American subtropics.
 
It is considered that the first Europeans to ever see tobacco were the participants of the Columbus' expedition who actually brought the very first tobacco leaves back to Europe with them. However, the seeds got to Spain with the second expedition: they were brought there in 1496 by a monk named Roman Pano.
 
Year 1560 was important for the history of tobacco distribution throughout Europe.
 Jean Nicot, a French diplomat who had been the Embassador of France in Portugal at the time, gave tobacco to Catherine Medici, the Queen of France, as a remedy against migraine. In honor of Nicot, tobacco received the name herbe nicotiniane, and later, the alkaloid, derived from the plant, was named nicotine. Dry tobacco leaves contain about 1–5% nicotine.
 
In addition to nicotine, tobacco contains other alkaloids, for example, harmine, harmaline and tetrahydroharmine which are monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs), which relates them to certain antidepressants that act in the same way.
 
The complex of these compounds gives tobacco smoke its entheogenic (which literally means 'generating the divine within') effect, which in fact allowed the indigenous tribes of America to use it in their shamanic practices in order to enter the 'mystic altered states of consciousness'. However, none of that relates to the modern fragrance industry since natural aromatic raw materials extracted from tobacco do not contain nicotine or any other alkaloids.
 
 
 
 
 
 
The fact that tobacco contains physiologically active substances was known as early as VIth century BC: there is evidence that tobacco had been already cultivated.
 
Historians think that, in the first century BC, Mayans already smoked tobacco during their not so frequent religious ceremonies, but the earliest material evidence of that are images on clay dishes from Guatemala (VII-XI centuries).
 
 

 Several kinds of tobacco have been cultivated throughout history. Primarily people grow varieties of the Nicotiana tabacum (Generic tobacco or Brightleaf tobacco). Every year, on a territory covering more than 4 million ha, about 7 million tons (!!) of tobacco are cultivated, the major part of which is then used in tobacco products manufacturing.
 

 China is the leader on the market (with over 2.5 million tons of tobacco grown yearly), followed by India and Brazil, while USA produces about 450 thousand tons of tobacco per year.
 
 
 
 
Certain varieties of tobacco, for example, jasmine/sweet or winged tobacco (Lat. Nicotiana alata, occasionally called Nicotiana affinis earlier in history), Aztec or Strong tobacco (Lat. Nicotiana rustica) or Petunia tobacco (Lat. Nicotiana petunoides; by the way, petunia is a very close relative of tobacco and aslo belongs to the solanaceous family) possess highly fragrant flowers.
 
People make home-made absolute from these flowers, which has a deep floral aroma with notes of violet, cloves, spices and dried fruit, however the material is not produced on an industrial scale.
 
 
 
 
 
The tobacco absolute is derived from dried fremented tobacco leaves and here it goes basically by the book: first, the leaves are extracted with petroleum ether or hexane (they had also used benzole for the purpose earlier) and the resulting concrete is then extracted by ethanol.
 
 
Virginia varieties are the ones most often used to produce the absolute, more rarely people use Burley, the so-called Oriental (Turkish) and Latakia varieties (grown in Syria and Cyprus; upon primary drying, this variety of tobacco is then fire-cured; looking ahead, let me just say that Christopher Sheldrake attempted to reproduce the aroma of that variety of tobacco in his Fumerie Turque, which was created under the strict guidance of Serge Lutens).
 
 
Tobacco absolute is a dark brown semisolid mass with a nearly repulsive odor, barely reminiscent of the scent of pipe tobacco. Classically made absolute is rather heavily colored which may lead to problems (coloring of clothes and skin), which is why it is often additionally decolorized.
 
There is a slightly different way to go about it: first they do the initial extraction, then they add a special high boiling solvent and conduct a codistillation resulting in a weakly colored viscous liquid.
 
When heavily diluted, tobacco extracts get their characteristic tobacco-cigar aroma: somewhat sweet, herbaceous, woody, slightly mossy, with notes of hay, tea, honey, dried fruit and hints of flowers, leather and chocolate.
 
 
Tobacco absolute is used not only in the tobacco-themed fragrances, for in small quantities it adds depth and character to aldehydic frgrances and imparts some typical dryness to fougeres and oriental compositions. Tobacco notes combine well with sandalwood, castoreum, labdanum, sage, vetiver, cedar and violet-iris.
 
An absolute that is somewhat similar to tobacco in terms of aroma is another natural material – the absolute of Blazing star (Lat. Liatris odoratissima), a plant of the aster family (Lat. Asteraceae): sweet, herbal-coumarin, with undertones of vanilla. Combined with musks, heliotropine, ionones and cinnamon, Liatris absolute creates a very interesting and dry powdery accord.
 
 
 
 
Much like tobacco absolute, liatris absolute can be successfully combined with oakmoss, labdanum, lavender, incense, salycilates, cloves, patchouli and many other materials.
 
 
 
 
 
The chemistry of the tobacco scent is quite rich and diverse.
 The biggest contributors to the shaping of its charateristic tobacco fragrance are carotenoids: ionones and their many derivatives, damascenone, edulane and its derivatives, theaspiranes (important components of the scent of tea and osmanthus), safranal, megastigmatrienone – which is also present in osmanthus, but plays one of major roles here; Symrise and certain other companies produce it under the brand Tabanon.
 
 
As any natural material, tobacco absolute contains many terpenoids: linalool, linalool oxide, limonene, geranyl acetone, farnesyl acetone and many others The rosey note of damascenone is built up by phenilacetic acid and its esters. Generally speaking, esters are present in all their diversity in the tobacco scent.
 
An important role is played by phenols (cresols, guaiacols, eugenol and its analogs), shaping a medicinal, slightly cured accord. The derivatives of pyrazine give a nutty roasted tone, while furan derivatives impart a caramel note: the effect of 2-acetyl furan (sweet, balsamic, roasted, with coconut, almond and coffee) can be seen in Hugo Boss Baldessarini.
 
 
Tobacco absolute also contains lactones: first and foremost, the peachy gamma-undecalactone and the nutty-coconut whisky lactone with undertones of tonka beans, caramelized sugar and celery.
 
Coumarin gives tobacco a somewhat sweet hay note (together with benzaldehyde, coumarin creates the very recognizable almond-cherry accord), while macrolactones, mostly pentadecanolide, impart the rather easily perceptible musky tone. Among odorants that shape the scent of tobacco we even find ambroxan and some similar compounds: they impart the amber accord with all its multiple facets to the tobacco scent.
 
 
Among synthetic tobacco scent shaping odorants, we find compounds of very different structure: many synthetic cariotenoids have a tobacco note, with veltonal, a Bedoukian product, being one of the brightest examples of that: an intense violet-ionone odorant with a distinctive tobacco tone and an interesting raspberry-pomegranate fruity accord.
 

 Many amber-woody materials possess a tobacco aspect in them: kefalis, trimofix, tobacarol (a synthetic derivative of caryophylene oxide).
 
 
Many minor components of the tobacco aroma are nitrogen-containing compounds, which is not typical for aromachemistry at all. Certain synthetic heterocycles and nitrous compounds are used in the fragrance industry in nanoquantities just in order to make the tobacco note richer, more vivid and more high-contrast.
 
Tabaxol, for instance, is an extremely intense smelling compound, but when heavily diluted it achieves a roasted, nutty, slightly meaty scent with phenolic and metallic undertones, whereas its 'big brother' mossenate is less aggressive and possesses some interesting orris and ylang-ylang notes.
 
Methyl nicotinate combines warm spicy aromatic notes with a distinct tobacco tone, while damascenine (despite its name, a close relative to anthranilates) combines tobacco and floral notes. Tonkavert may also seem quite an interesting compound: chemically speaking, it is a nitril, but one can recognize notes of tonka beans and tobacco in its scent.
 
 
 
 
 
There are a great many tobacco-themed fragrances out there, so there is no point in listing even the ones that contain some version of the word 'tobacco' in their names.
 
People often mention Caron's Tabac Blondas one of the most famous tobacco fragrances, but it is rather more leather Isobutyl Quinoline, i.e. leather in vanilla sauce with hints of cloves and iris.
 
Many remember and love Remy Latour's Cigar, which was practically a single note fragrance (pipe tobacco being the star note there), very 'damascone and dried fruit'. One of my favorite tobacco fragrances is Etat Libre d'Orange's Jasmin et Cigarette with a realistic note of an inadvertently water-drenched pack of cigarettes; Demeter's "fragrance library" offers several mono-compositions around the tobacco theme Pipe Tobacco, Whiskey Tobacco, This Is Not A Pipe and Humidor.
 
 
Tobacco Vanille, a fragrance created by Olivier Guillotin for Tom Ford in 2007, became a new benchmark when it comes to the tobacco accord in the fragrance industry: others tried to copy and recreate that damascone-vanilla-tobacco structure, heavily mixed with Iso-E-Super, countless times and there were rather decent compositions among those, too, such as: Franck Boclet Tobacco, Phaedon Tabac Rouge, Nikos Sculpture Homme God's Night. Ford also proactively continued to develop the tobacco theme, producing Tobacco Oud and Tobacco Oud Intenseafter the initial blockbuster.
 
 
There is also a great number of fantasy fragrances where the tobacco note is sometimes used in a rather unusual context: e.g. Parfum d'Empire Tabac Tabouwith its daffodil-honey accord, plus a tart and burnt note of immortelle; the aniseed-aquatic Tabac Licorii by Maison Incens. 
 
Then there is the pirate's rum, cardamom and cinnamon of Comptoir Sud Pacifique's Rhum & Tabac; the leather and lavender Cuir Tabacby David Jourquin; tobacco with mandarin and thyme in Jo Malone's Tobacco & Mandarin – Yan Vasnier performed a clever trick in this fragrance by stringing the key accords on thymol as though the former were pearls, thymol being an important part of all the three aforementioned ingredients. I think that you can easily continue this list of wonderful fragrances yourself!
 
 
FDA and Fragrantica warn you: smoking is bad for your health. Meanwhile, fragrances, including tobacco-themed ones, are absolutely harmless and they can always lift your mood.
