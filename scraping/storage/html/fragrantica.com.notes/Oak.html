Just the cactus of jealousy,
 The thistle of habit,
 And the molding moss of oblivion.
 
-Igor Severyanin, Don Juan, 1929
 
 
It's probably not going to be a surprise for many of you when I say that oakmoss is in fact no moss at all. Oakmoss, or Lat. Evernia prunastri, is a kind of lichen from the Lat. Parmeliaceae family.
 
Unlike true mosses, or bryophytes (Lat. Bryophyta) which are higher order plants, lichens, which include oakmoss, are not even truly plants, but rather symbiotic associations of fungi and microscopic green algae or cyanobacteria.
 
Oakmoss grows on oak trees all over the territories of Central and Southern Europe. The fragrance industry uses oakmosses collected in Macedonia, France, as well as, to a lesser degree, Hungary, Czech Republic, Greece and some countries of Northern Africa (e.g. Morocco, Algeria).
 
 Oakmoss (Evernia prunastri)
 
 
 
The tree moss is a type of 'lichenized' fungus from the Parmelia family, with Pseudevernia furfuracea and Usnea Barbata being the two prominent varieties. These fungi are most frequently found in the humid forests of Central and Southern Europe, but they prefer pine and fir trees.
 
Usnea Barbata
 
 
 
The two experts on the Chemistry of Natural Odorants, Steffen Arktander and Boris Vinogradov, agree on the fact that the extracts of mosses used in perfumery are hardly ever produced from just one variety of the raw material: it is almost always a blend of various fungi.
 
More than any other natural aromatic raw material, oakmoss absolute is virtually always a blend. It is 'finetuned', not so much for economic reasons, but in order to maximize the stability of its olfactory profile.
 
Pseudevernia furfuracea
 
 
 
Strictly speaking, only the material obtained from Evernia prunastri can be considered oakmoss extract. If, in addition to Evernia prunastri, other lichens are also used (Pseudevernia furfuracea (L.) Zopf, Ramalina fraxinea (L.) Ach., Usnea caucasica Vain., Usnea barbata, Parmelia nepalensis, etc.), the product should be labeled as 'tree moss' extract. There are even mentions of certain algae used in the production of these odorants.
 
The chemical composition of oakmoss extracts has been meticulously studied; it is probably only jasmine absolute that we know even more about. At present, there are about 200 substances collected and studied, among them various terpenes and their derivatives (our regular readers can't help but yawn here, finishing the phrase by themselves: linalool, geraniol, pinens, camphor, myrcene, carvone, γ-terpinene, paracymene, p-cymene, thymol, bornyl acetate, limonene, β-caryophyllene, longifolene, humulenes, β-farnesene and others).
 
The characteristic peculiar scent of moss extracts - 'mossy', woody, earthy, wet, a bit oily, with a distinct phenolic, rather cured note and some animalic accords - is achieved thanks to the so called depsides(aromatic carboxylic acids of the phenolic kind, ethers of aromatic oxycarboxylic acid) and their derivatives. It is the most important and the most interesting part of aroma chemicals derived from lichens.
 
 
Look at the the formula of evernic acid, a 'classic' depside. It is a rather huge, substantial structure that is possibly too heavy to have a scent. Yes, there is a surprise here for us: the majority of phenolic substances that determine the characteristic scent of oakmoss are not present in the lichens themselves, but are the result of the extraction process.
 
Upon collection, lichens are often dried and presoaked in warm water for a few hours right before extraction - this procedure ensures the fullest extraction of odorants.
 
During the presoaking process, both partial hydrolysis and decarboxylation of many depsides takes place, which during subsequent alcohol extraction leads to trans-esterification and other destructive processes.
 
It is widely known that when methanol is used for extraction instead of ethanol, one gets a product with different olfactory properties. It has more pronounced, somewhat sweet anise notes. So any 'natural' oakmoss extract is quite the product of chemical synthesis.
 
 
Several types of fragrance extracts are produced from oak moss: first and foremost, we have concretes, i.e. materials obtained by extraction using non-polar solvents.
 
Initially, benzene was used for the purpose: the result was an excellent product of exceptional perfumery properties. For over 35 years now, the use of benzene for these purposes has been strictly prohibited and, unfortunately, we are yet to find a suitable substitute for it, one that would result in products of similar quality.
 
Scientists are researching various binary and more complex azeotropic mixtures, but for now people mostly rely on hexane to make these concretes (output 1,5 – 2%), or petroleum ether, a mixture of hexane with polar solvents: esters (acetates) and alcohols (isopropanol). Good output is achieved by acetone extraction upon presoaking.
 
Concretes extracted by ethanol give us absolutes. Sometimes people opt for co-distillation of absolutes with high boiling solvents, usually at high pressure, in the conditions of the so called molecular distillation. That allows one to fractionate the product obtained.
 
Oftentimes another material is obtained, a resinoid: the product of hot ethanol extraction. All extraction products are subject to decolorization by activated carbon or other similar methods.
 
The composition of the raw material, the solvents, the temperature that is used during extraction, the duration, the presoaking - these are some of the parameters that may be varied in the process of oakmoss extract production for the fragrance industry. This is why the number of materials, varying greatly in their quality and value for the fragrance industry, is truly great.
 
 
Oakmoss concretes and oakmoss absolutes appeared as soon as manufacturers got access to volatile organic solvents, i.e. a very long time ago.
 
In the second half of the XIXth century, oakmoss was already one of the most popular raw materials; classic fougères contained generous amounts of it. It achieved its superstar status a little more than 100 years ago with the launch of the legendary Coty Chypre: from that moment on, the chypre accord, which always features oakmoss, has appeared in numerous fragrance bestsellers.
 
Oakmoss fits well into almost any context: it can be encountered in colognes, oriental fragrances, floral bouquets, woody compositions, modern abstract concoctions and so on. Oakmoss, with its rich and pleasant notes, imparts a certain 'naturalness' to the heart of any fragrance. It also has good longevity, which allows it be viewed as sort of a fragrance 'fixative'.
 
 
 
 
 

 The situation radically changed in 2009 with the introduction of the 43rd amendment of the IFRA. Just to be fair, let me admit that people have been aware of allergenic properties of oakmoss for quite some time. Even in 1940s, there were articles on cases of contact dermatitis among lumberjacks that had been in direct contact with the lichens. In the 1980s, the sensibilization resulting from oakmoss extracts was the subject of detailed research; at the time, every 5th case of allergy to fragrance products was connected to moss. A number of depsides and their derivatives were found to be the main cause of the trouble.
 
IFRA's 43rd amendment limits the use of oakmoss extract to 0,1% in the end product (which is about 1% in the concentrate), provided that the used material contains no more than 100 ppm (0,01%) of atranol and chloroatranol, the derivative depsides of atranorine and chloratranorine, respectively.
 
Atranol and chloratranol can be removed by various methods: chromatographic quantitative division, solvent extraction, countercurrent division, membrane division, catalytic hydrogenation and other chemical methods.
 
For example, one of those methods relies on the fact that an allergenic fraction is, in terms of its chemistry, a group of aldehydes. The blend is processed with leucine or another amino acid, which in turn results in the creation of Schiff base insolubles, which are then filtered out.
 
 
The biggest problem here is that both atranol and chloroatranol have distinct scents of their own and their presence in the mixture impacts the resulting fragrance. Once atranol/chloatranol is removed, the 'safe' moss smells differently.
 
Perfumers are being quite creative in their efforts to find the solution to the problem: for instance, Thierry Wasser once shared that when he was in the process of reformulating Guerlain Mitsouko, he had to mend the 'olfactory hole' in Robertet's oakmoss extract with the help of mastic tree absolute and a special heavy solvent that would not change the scent yet slowed down the evaporation process.
 
 
Among substances shaping the characteristic aroma of oakmoss, we have to mention certain monoaromatic compounds, phenols and ethers, mainly derivatives or orcinol.
 
 
In the first paper that mentioned methyl β-orcinol carboxylate, which came out in 1924, it was described as odorless (and I have to agree with the german scientists here, I am practically anosmic to it myself), but soon it was found out that the very compound was the main contributor to the scent of moss.
 
In the 1960s, there appeared several papers that suggested different ways to synthesize the compound. At present, the substance is sold under such trademarks as Veramoss, Evernyl, Everniate, LRG201, Atralone, Musgolide, Synthetic Oakmoss and others.
 
By the end of the same decade, the appearance of evernyl sort of 'propelled' the creation of new chypre accords, and in the 1970s the majority of chypre accords were built on the basis of evernyl. If a fragrance contained any natural oakmoss at all, it would be in trace quantities only. We are all familiar with these fragrances, some of them are shown on the photo below.
 
 
Another derivative of orcinol, the monomethyl ether, possesses a woody, mossy, phenolic scent and is produced under the trademarks Orcinyl, Orcinyl-3 and Oakmoss Phenol.
 
Ionones and irones  are also worth mentioninghere (α-ionone, β-ionone, cis-γ-irone, trans-α-irone, cis-α-irone); they were found in the composition of oakmoss absolute. If violet-powder-woody ionones are widespread, contributing to the creation of many scents including those of fruit, irones were for a long time considered to be present solely in the iris.
 
From the moment the aforementioned restrictions were put on the usage of oakmoss extracts for the fragrance industry's purposes, their production has been greatly reduced. For instance, France produces approximately three times less than 30 years ago.
 
 
Nevertheless, some modern fragrances are examples of 'working on the edge', with the natural moss raw material content in them being close to the maximum allowed: Ralph Lauren Polo Crest and Serge Lutens Gris Glair contain about 1% of oakmoss absolute, while 2% of tree moss absolute is found in Lacoste Essential, and Terre d'Hermes has about 0,5%.
