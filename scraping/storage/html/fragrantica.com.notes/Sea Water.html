The American poet Mariane Moore wrote a perceptive, refined description of the ocean side in New England in her poem “The Steeplejack”. It opens:
 
Dürer would have seen a reason for living
 in a town like this, with eight stranded whales
 to look at; with the sweet sea air coming into your house
 on a fine day, from water etched
 with waves as formal as the scales
 on a fish.
 
 
 
Wild juniper overlooking a rock ledge leading to the beach.
  
 
She goes on to discuss the kaleidoscopic details of life in a small town at the side of the water; all the flowers, the lobster traps, the fact that people often escape “up East” to get away from their troubles from somewhere else in the world. Being a New Englander myself, I understood her poetic summary, but also thought that she came very close to capturing the sense of smell that accompanies the seaside. And what a difficult task this can be, a series of smells so bold or so subtle that we rarely think them through, or stop to consider them for what they truly are. 
 
 
 
The cycle of life and scent at the sea: A dying tree trunk that has become imbued with salt, and is the support for Common Elderberry (Sambucus canadenis), Spice Bush (Lindern benzoin), and Carolina Rose (Rosa carolina).
 

 I recently took two separate trips to Conanicut Island in Narragansett Bay, just at the tip where it faces the Atlantic Ocean in Rhode Island. For those unfamiliar with this region, it is a craggy, jagged bay that empties into the sea, not very far from Long Island, New York. My first trip was somewhat somber, as it was a memorial, but the second was for research. The reason I came back a second time, besides the immense beauty that I found at Jamestown and Beavertail, was because of a chance encounter that occurred during the first visit. I’d been swimming in the salt water and laying in the sun for an hour or so when I became acutely aware of the smells around me. I’m not sure I would have had my nose so “wide open” if it weren’t for the bit of meditation I was in… I wasn't saying much, with closed eyes, ears overwhelmed by the roar of the tides as they flooded into the cove. Instinctively, I raised my hand to my nose and inhaled - what was I smelling? My skin had a smell, and it wasn’t any fragrance I’d put on that day.
 
 
 
A beach cypress, bent by the constant struggle of growing against the force of the coastal winds.
 

 First, there was the unmistakable smell of salt. I’ve thought a lot about what makes this smell of salt, and how we perceive it, and I know it is accentuated by our sense of the sea. It is more of a sensation. Being on the rocks of a cove and surrounded by rushing tides, you feel, hear, and smell salt all at the same time. The sun also plays a part as it bakes this smell into your skin, like a patina on metal. So on that day, I started to summarize this first smell: salt on skin. It came from the air, the waves - it clung to my skin, so there were remnants of sunscreen catching some of it on me. What was the smell? Something aquatic and green, dusty and metallic? I was searching for adjectives when I knew that I needed to come back and do much more research because this was a complex smell, a very beautiful smell, but one made up from a range of small elements grouped together, shifting with the winds as it swirls crazily along the coasts. And it shifted with time, as the sun traveled overhead, and the tide rose and fell, leaving bare minerals, shells, seaweed and a whole world of loud, decaying, sea-logged smells verging on the utterly unpleasant, but also nostalgically marine. I grew up not so far from the water, so the smell of low tide was never something that bothered me much, it is similar to the smell of a marsh or other wetlands - it’s the smell of things undergoing a life cycle of decay and regeneration.
 
 
 
Sweet and green scented sea grasses on rock ledges near the shore.
 

 I came back to Conanicut armed with a camera and a lot of time to investigate. There are three distinct but interdependent realms of smells at the ocean side that impact each other. The flora in the landscape, the watery ecosystem, and the weather. I begin with some of the striking features of the landscape and how they "set" our nose, but also prepare us for the way that life in this particular region faces startling obstacles. These challenges that the organic world at the seaside undergo has a profound impact on what everything looks like, smells like, and feels.
 
 
 
Clockwise from top left: Wild Tansy (Tanacetum vulgare), Beach Pea (Lathyrus maritimus), and Globe Thistle (Echinops ritro).
 

 Some people are surprised by the beauty and intensity of botanical life at the water’s edge. We’ve grown used to “manicured” beaches, where clean sand has been shipped in, the ground has been cleared of plants, and virtually no insects can be found flying anywhere. But that is a highly unnatural state for the tidewaters. Many plants find their way to the shore and thrive there. Others have adjusted to the harsh climate and find ways to overcome the difficulties of living in constant roaring wind, high levels of salt in the air and soil, and in a space where the dirt is sparse, rocky, and crowded. Plants grow in abundance where they are able, and will still find their seeds settled into the smallest, most impossible of spaces to thrive and grow. As you go further south along the island, the land narrows, and you notice that the species that are growing the most become fewer - clearly a case of survival of the fittest. And yet the abundance, and the smell! 
 
 
 
The wild and deeply fragrant Virginia Rose (Rosa virginia).
 

 Perhaps the biggest surprise of all are the the unexpectedly rambling hedge roses. Some gardeners and botanists will tell you (and I tend to agree) that simpler roses have a stronger scent. Three varieties of rose that grow wild on the New England coastline are Swamp Rose (Rosa palustris), Carolina Rose (Rosa carolina), and Virginia Rose (Rosa virginiana). They vary in shade from white to white with slight yellow markings, to pale pink, to deeper magenta. Unlike hybrid or garden roses, these are single blossoms that have five petals, which open up to a bold star formation. Their leaves are abundant, soft, bright green, and fragrant. But nothing compares to the smell of these roses.
 
Walking up to a hedgerow of Virginia Rose in a gust of coastal wind is a shock: Oh, the sea! You’re met with the most wonderful and peculiar rose smell - bright, fruity, and full of spice. This rose is simple, linear, but startlingly poetic. Imagine, if you will, what the ocean water can do to the smell of a mulled wine and spiced Moroccan rose, and there you have the wild Virginia Rose as it falls over itself in huge masses over weathered wood fences and dying cedar tree trunks.
 
 
 
The enormous, juicy, and brightly fragrant rose hips of the Swamp Rose (Rosa palustris).
 

 Another startling feature of the seaside roses is the bright red color of their rose hips (seed pods) as they mature. The Virginia Rose has modest-sized, round rose hips, whereas the Swamp Rose produces rose hips as large as small tomatoes, and nearly the same color - remaining on the branches well into the fall, providing color to the landscape, and food for birds and animals. They're also used as a healthful base for rose hip jelly.
 
The smell of rose hips isn’t noticeable until you pierce them - and then a clean, clear, slightly acidic scent arises, smelling like a combination of rose, grapes, tomato, and lemon. The taste is sweet, a bit like pomegranate or persimmon, but without any bitterness. The leaves that surround these rose hips also have a characteristic green leaf smell imbued with a drop of rosewater, and tinged with saline. Like most smells that are found near the sea, this too has a sharpness and vivacity that appears when it’s coaxed out of its shell.
 
 
 
A white Virgina Rose growing in a shallow ledge of sand in the rock face at Bevertail park, Jamestown, Rhode Island.
 

 The flora is numerous and takes many forms, often providing nourishment in the way of berries and fruits. Bayberries and their beautiful smell (something that every New Englander has deep in their scent memory) is something akin to eau de cologne - it resembles Laurel leaves or Bay Laurel. Huckleberries and their fragrant leaves can be found crawling near the ground not far from wild grape vines, Common Elderberry, and Spice Bush, the latter being perhaps the most fragrant of all the leafy shrubs on the island. Queen Anne’s Lace, a version of wild carrot, has a rooty, pungent smell like carrots and turnips mixed together when its roots are exposed, the flowers a dusty kind of deep evergreen. Some flowers have shy, green smells like the Beach Pea or the Swamp Azalea. 
 
 
 
Wild and fragrant: clockwise from upper left: Japanese Honeysuckle (Lonicera japonica), Wild Grape vine (Vitis labrusca), Northern Bayberry (Myrica pensylvanica), Queen Anne's Lace (Daucus carota), and Blue Huckleberry (Gaylussacia fromdosa).
 

 The smell that pervades much of the day when you walk from the green fields to the rock ledges is the faint smell of wood - the saturated wood of cypress, juniper, oak, and cedar, varieties that have grown used to the harsh growing conditions of a ferocious coast. Some trees are curiously bent by the wind, listing to one side as the strong breezes and moist air have painstakingly shaped the trunks into determined angles. You’ll find the small gray-blue berries of juniper in many varieties everywhere, crush one in your hand and that piney, woody smell feels completely at home in this salty world.
  
 
Beach fence with vines and wild flowers, Jamestown, Rhode Island.
 
We might stay for days in the abundant greenery of the island, but the horizon beckons, and the sound of the waves calls us to the water. While walking down to the shore, notice the rocky coastline, one which exposes a wide range of sights and smells. The tide is lowering, and you see the evidence of shifting plates from over millennia. Rock violently scraping against rock, with flakes of mica still glistening everywhere. This mineral sensation glints in your eyes, and asks you to cover your forehead with your hand as you walk your way to the first pool of water in the cove. The abundance of sea vegetation comes full into view and you smell - smell deeply - that beautiful fresh smell of seaweed.
 
 
 
The rocks and blue sea facing southwest, out toward the Atlantic.
 

 There is something eternally clean and innocent about seaweed. It’s greenery born in water - always being washed by the water, limp because it’s never had to stand on its own. The smell, especially when you have it in your hand, is fascinating. What makes up this smell? Any sea smell is complex; we’re smelling all the cumulative smells of things that are alive in the water - the vegetation, the fish and creatures, the cycles of life and death in that vast watery world. New England waters have enormous amounts of Bladderwrack (Focus vesiculosus), the seaweed of capsules, a greenish brown in color, often found in Irish water as well. This, mixed with Kelp, and green sea algae make up the majority of the plants you’ll find around Rhode Island. Pulling some fresh seaweed in your hand, a rush comes to the mind and senses - the smell of clean fresh fish, the smell of the ocean, that nearly impossible smell to describe of worn, calcified seashells; a wet, grassy odor, something you only smell when water is evaporating off the surface of something organic. It's sweet, mildly animalic, peaceful, impossible to disconnect from air or water.
 
 
 
BladderWrack (Focus vesiculosus) and dried green algae and seaweed on the shore.
 

 It is this particular cycle, water and evaporation, that constitutes the majority of smells of the ocean here. You’re reminded of it as the water rushes loudly past your legs and hits aggressively against the rocks behind you. In an hour, that water will be part of the air again, and you’ll smell it as part of your experience in the sun, with the sand under your feet. As I dive in the water for a swim, I realize I’ve not picked the best cove (the tide has gone out too far, and it’s shallow.) But the smell! It’s an overwhelming, decaying, wet wood, fishy smell, yet in it is something optimistic. Turn around for a moment, and the sun burns the smell off again and another wave of water passes over you and the smell is agitated again. 
 
This is the smell that oyster lovers crave - pure saline freshness. Could this be the reason that some of us get unusually strong cravings for food from the sea, because we know that captured within those shells is a bit of a time capsule of smell; a memory of the seaside?
 
 
 
The full sensory experience of the coast - seaweed underfoot, water rushing around.
 

 Eventually I chose a deeper cove and found the water refreshingly cool, but not too cold. Swimming in the cold Atlantic water is considered a "tonic" for many New Englanders, something you do to cool off and somehow purify the body. The smell of the saltwater is at its most beautiful when you dive in, surface, and shake the water from your head.
 
At this stage in the day, when you've swum a few times and spent time under the sun overhead, the weather and environment of the island reveal themselves as part of the ocean smell experience. The deep heat (as the sun starts to descend from the sky) adds a level of intensity to the smells everywhere.The roses from the Southern tip of the island have been releasing their perfume slowly, swept along by the winds and seagulls' wings. It sharpens the saline and sun-baked stones nearby. Little puddles of splashed water from bathers is evaporating in small steamy columns. Salt is everywhere. It's late afternoon and all these small parts, from the crushed sweet grasses to the drying kelp on the rocks contributes to this shifting but persistent scented environment.
 
 
 
One of the coves at Beavertail Park, Conanicut Island: where sea, mineral, sky, and foliage meet.
 

 After swimming, I’m wrapped in a towel and vaguely make out a scent of the laundry, but I’m so much more intrigued by the scent of the trampled Queen Anne’s Lace nearby, the whiff of algae, and that smell (yes, I do believe there is a smell) that accompanies the setting of the sun. Is it a smell, a feeling, or perhaps just our reaction to the sun being lower on the horizon, facing us more directly? It can be powerful.
 
I make a visit to yet another beach as I wander my way back up the island to the mainland, and find that more wildness in the landscape has taken over a narrow strip which connects two sections of the island together. Here you’ll find massive sweet grasses and cat-o-nine-tails. This feels almost prehistoric, where dinosaurs could have roamed. Some of the plants, like Sea Holly (Eryngium maritinum) are spiny, sharp and spike-tipped. Self-protected and guarded, but sparsely beautiful.
 
 
 
The beach facing east: plants, sand, stone, air and seawater fill the air with the palpable smells of summer. 
 

 Many of the plants have massive thorns, grow tightly together, are almost impenetrable. This is a landscape of bravery - plants and weather that know nothing mild or gentle. Yet somehow, in it, we derive a sense of the sublime, subtle, and clean smells from this world. A distilled, clear gem from massive pressure. Looking back at the water, and the fading sun above, everything seems to contribute to the smell of the sea - even the paradoxical relationship that we have with this environment that can sometimes feel alien to us, but still echoes many of the patterns of our own lives, like tides rushing in and out, powered by higher forces like the pull of the moon.
