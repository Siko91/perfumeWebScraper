Serguey Borisov: Dear Pierre! Glad you have enough time to continue our interview series about natural raw materials. Let me suggest to you a new theme: Tuberose! I remember that you had been creating a new Tuberose soliflore—I have seen and smelled it in Esxence Milano. Let`s begin with that. What are the main scent features of Tuberose absolute? What does it smell like? Green, flowery, lactonic, narcotic and…? Are there any other flowers that smell similar?
 
 
 
Pierre Benard, photo by Sophie Thouvenin
 
 
 
Pierre Benard: Dear Sergey, Tuberose! I am glad that you remember this creation. Its spectral sillage walked by the corridors of Esxence showcase last year. If rose is the queen of flowers in perfumery and jasmine the jewel of the perfumer, tuberose is my muse—a subject that I sculpt again after a conference dedicated to the Gardens of the International Museum of Perfumery, writings, photographs and conversations with farmers in the countryside of Grasse. The flower and its fragrance are difficult to tame. Let us draw near to her spirit, to her absolute. Her smell is rich, precious. Her rarity comes with its price. Her floral scent is powerful, even heady for delicate nose—you must be strong. Her olfactive spectrum is large and kaleidoscopic.
 
photo by sleepyneko
 
 
 
The smell of tuberose absolute is like an explosive bouquet of white flowers. Its extreme complexity gives the olfactive illusion of different flowers and their extracts. With green and narcotic notes, she approaches the scent of Narcissus species. With its milky notes, from its profile emerge exotic flowers like gardenia, frangipani and tiare, whose petals are mixed with coconut oil for the traditional Monoi, the scent of skin under the sunlight.
 
photo by coconut wireless
 
 
 
These exotic notes will partner with cold spices. The tuberose absolute contains fatty acids (palmitic, linoleic and linolenic), odorless molecules that makes up 20% of the material. Butyric acid gives the scent a fatty note, really buttery. Its sillage is sweet, honeyed, like beeswax or genet absolute, and it does suggest gingerbread.
 
tuberose ready for transportation, photo by mmmavocado
 
 
 
 
 
Serguey: Could you describe the main constituents of Tuberose absolute and the most simple way to mimic the note in perfume (I believe that your perfume was far more complicated, so let's make your example minimalistic, with the least amount of components).
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: My soliflore perfume is slightly more complex, but it finds his inspiration in the olfactive architecture of the flower. The headspace of this flower analyzed by nose or by chromatography releases very volatile molecules.
 
The famous limonene, which features in essential oils of citrus, is the main constituent of orange essential oil. There is a typical and medicinal odor, pharmaceutical indeed, easily identifiable, such as eucalyptol (cineol) and especially methyl salicylate which makes the smell of wintergreen essential oil, as it contains more than 90% of this molecule.
 
The eugenol and its metallic-smelling derivatives, such as methyl eugenol and methyl iso eugenol, whose spicy emanations are like a bouquet of lilies that could fill all the room by its scent. The methyl benzoate reiterates the importance of this flower among flowers, a molecule presented in the essential oil of ylang-ylang.
 
photo by jayeshp912
 
 
 
Lactones, like delta nonalactone and jasmine lactone, enrich the tuberose palette with creamy notes of coconut milk. In the basenotes, methyl anthranilate and indole emerges. Molecules of skin, they are the link between the flower and animal. At twilight, they will manifest more deeply in the colorless flowers that target the insects of the night. Fertilization of this lady flower is nocturnal. It’s done by Moro Sphinx, and some hummingbird species (specially dedicated to Fragrantica), which holds its proboscis to reach the juice at the bottom of the floral tubes.
 
 
 
 
Serguey: Let`s return to history. What is the story of Tuberose? Is it true that Tuberose was brought to the Old World from South America with potatoes and tobacco? Where is the native area of Tuberosa polianthes, and what are the main regions that grow Tuberose now?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: Tuberose is endemic to Central America and southern Mexico. The flower landed in India via the Philippines. From this migration, two other routes were traced to Europe. One was through Spain to the Languedoc in southwestern France and the other, through Persia, traveling through Liguria to Provence. Tuberose was introduced in 1632 in Provence by RP Théophile Minuti.
 
tuberose field, photo by mmmavocado
 
 
 
Long before the botanical name Tuberosa polianthes, it was named Hyacinthus indicus tuberosa radice in 1601, literally "hyacinth of India." Today, literally it means "flower of towns." The term "polianthes” comes from Greek polis meaning "city" and anthos, "flower." The name of its genus Tuberosa characterizes the fleshy tuberous root. Tuberosa is from the agave family, the genus comprises a total of 13 species and each is more or less fragrant. Nowadays, tuberose is mainly cultivated in Morocco, The Comoros, India and China for processing.
 
photo by mmmavocado
 
 
 
 
 
Serguey: I have read that unmarried girls were not allowed to go near flowering Tuberose fields in India, as doing so could bring “unchaste thoughts.” Do you know any other legends about Tuberose?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: Very fragrant tuberose has been in many legends and stories that reveal its bewitching nature. The best-known story that I recall is during the Italian Renaissance, where it was forbidden for unmarried girls to walk through the gardens where tuberose exercised her erotic and intoxicating power, so they would not succumb to drunkenness and men maddened by the erotic smell. If jasmine absolute reveals the joy on the faces of the people who smell it, it was said that a woman who exudes the scent of tuberose cause mimicry recalling orgasm.
 
Far from romantic eroticism, I know another story written by Belgian cartoonist Peyo (Pierre Culliford), in the comic The Purple Smurfs. To save his people from the curse of the fly called Bzz, Papa Smurf concocts in his laboratory a lot of preparations before finding the ultimate antidote: pollen of tuberose.
 
 
 
 
 
Serguey: When did people begin to cultivate tuberose?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: In 1530, a French missionary imported from Mexico the first tuberose bulbs, and secretly grew them in the garden of a monastery near Toulon. The Tuberose was developed then in Languedoc, in Italian Liguria and Provence, through the second half of the seventeenth century, for glovemakers, merchants and apothecaries.
 
photo by Gary Soup
 
 
 
 
 
Serguey: In what other areas have the plant and its parts been used? Is it useful in perfumery only, or does it have any other usage, like in medicine, aromatherapy, rituals, religious ceremonies and agriculture?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: The tuberose flower is toxic and inedible like the lily, lily of the valley or narcissus. Two varieties of Polianthes tuberosa are used. “Usual tuberose” is used in perfumery as it provides better organoleptic characteristics. Another flower variety, neatly called “pearl tuberose” is used for floral decorations. In Latin America, pearl tuberose is a prominent part of bridal bouquets and is deposited at the entrance of the bride's house to distinguish it. It is grown in indoor gardens and patios to welcome with its fragrance.
 
Some stories say that the Aztecs cultivated tuberose for their chocolate, the drink of the gods—an aphrodisiac blend of cocoa beans, vanilla bean and chilli. Today, flavorists use it with parsimony to reveal fruity notes like strawberry aroma. In India, women make wreaths and necklaces of this flower called Maalai for temples as offerings. Tuberose concrete is part of the composition in many incense mixtures and cosmetics.
 
 
 
Serguey: What are the different production technologies of Tuberose absolute?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: At one time it was the practice to use the cold enfleurage technique, which is capable of extracting the fragrant oil out of the most delicate flowers that do not tolerate heat, such as jasmine, jonquil and our precious tuberose. In the process tuberose flowers were kept on a mixture of animal fats for 72 hours (comparee with 48 hours for the jasmine, whose flowers are more tender and die so quickly). This traditional technique was long and tedious.
 
Nowadays, the extraction methods have evolved while preserving the fragile olfactive profile of the plant. Concrete is obtained by hexane extraction from freshly harvested flowers. From this concrete, absolute is extracted by ethanol or processed by supercritical CO2 extraction.
 
 
 
 
 
Serguey: Does anybody still make enfleurage of Tuberose—in France, India or in America?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: To my knowledge, there is an independent producer in Grasse who still achieves enfleurage, but in confidence and in an experimental way. This technique was specific to the city. I don’t know if it has been exported to other countries.
 
 
 
 
 
Serguey: What are the natural properties of the flower and its absolute?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: The natural properties of the flower and its absolute would be anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic. The pleasant fragrance and various chemicals have relaxing effects on the brain, nerves and muscles. Tuberose would calm the soul and relieve stress, tension, anxiety, depression, anger, etc. The flower is favorable to developing intuition and solving problems.
  
   
 
 
 
Serguey: Could you tell us the story of Tuberose in perfumery? When did its use in perfumery begin, especially French perfumery?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: Tuberose absolute and synthetic tuberose compounds have been used in the past in fine fragrances. L’Oeillet du Roys by Houbigant, a carnation-type perfume of the early part of 20th Century, contained a large percentage of this raw material. Among other older fragrances containing varying amounts of tuberose are Tabu by Dana and Je Reviens by Worth, both created in 1932.
 
 
 
 
Serguey: What are the best examples of Tuberose perfumes, in your opinion? As everyone knows about Fracas by Robert Piguet, were there any other great Tuberoses in past years?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: I acquired as a memory code, engraved in my olfactory memories, Carnal Flower by Editions de Parfums Frederic Malle. Its composer, Dominique ROPION, whose style of composing is very rich, has combined eucalyptus, often used in technical perfumery, in an overdose of tuberose absolute. Serge Lutens and Christopher Sheldrake accentuate methyl salicylate in their Tubereuse Criminelle. Fracas by Robert Piguet remains the reference in the collective memory of perfumery. This archetype created by Germaine Cellier was able to play its marketing to make us forget the first soliflore with the name is the flower name: Tubereuse by Le Galion, that was composed in 1939 by Paul Vacher who later signed Miss Dior. This perfumer exacerbated the camphor and menthol facets of tuberose extract with a green freshness of eucalyptus and hyacinth. Nothing comes from nothing, what exists now has always existed, everything is transformed. There are no good or bad smells ... there is odor. The scatological civet, a wild cat, can reveal jasmine. It's all about dosage ... it's all about harmony.
 
 
 
 
Serguey: Could you tell us a short formula for a Tuberose perfume to try at home?
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: Here is a short formula that you can find in Flower Oils and Floral Compounds in Perfumery by Pajaujis Anonis—Perfumer & Flavorist Allured Publishing. It is called Tuberose # 2, the formula is a total of 528 units [of measurement]. I prefer formulas that lead to a common domininator, say 1000 units, but this is not my formula.
 
 
 300 Aurantiol
 80 Benzyl Alcohol
 35 Benzyl Salicylate
 25 Phenyl Ethyl Alcohol
 25 Linalol
 15 Cinnamic Alcohol
 15 Methyl Salicylate
 10 Benzyl Acetate
 10 Alpha Amyl Cinnamic Aldehyde
 8 Iso Eugenol
 2 Vanillin
 2 Aldehyde C12 MNA
 1 Sweet Orange Oil terpeneless 10% 
 
 
 
Serguey: Dear Pierre, thank you for your time and for the knowledge you shared with Fragrantica readers!
 
 
 
 
 
Pierre: Dear Sergey, thank you for your request. This emblematic flower still reveals many mysteries. Fragrantica readers can send me their odorless mail to this virtual address: osmoart@gmail.com
 

 By Sergey Borisov
