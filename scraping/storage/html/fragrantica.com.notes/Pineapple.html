 There are perfumes that torture my brain when I try to understand them. And those are the ones that boost my love for perfumery. Sometimes they are very complicated. Sometimes they are challenging or extremely original. And then there are ones that have conflicting effects on me.
   
 

 Like Hacivat, from the Turkish house ofNishane. Why is that? Because it gathers love and hate inside me, but, what's worse, it does that in a masterful way and because of that it forces me to smell again and again something that I would never re-smell it if it hadn't been so well constructed.
 
Hacivat was recently presented for the first time during the Esxence event in Milan and I shall not elongate my presentation of the fragrance, since my colleague Julietta already did this part in her article HERE.
 
This is more of an afterthought, a personal interpretation that I wanted to add for discussion. What makes me hesitate so much regarding Hacivat? The answer comes in just one word: pineapple! What makes me accept it and even enjoy it? The magnificent structure it has and also the fact that hot weather really boosts its beauty, and summer seems to have come earlier where I live.
 
 
 

 Pineapple is probably one of the worst things a fragrance can have for me. There are very, very few perfumes with a pineapple note that I enjoy and for now I can only think of Scherrer 2 and Detaille's Miles (I am sure that there are a couple more). Pineapple in masculine perfumery has been associated with so many functional perfumery products, like shower gel, deodorant, and shampoo, that I have a hard time considering it beautiful.
 
It comes in so many low-quality fragrances that I immediately associate it with junk. If pineapple is put inside a fougère composition, I will be running from it like the devil from a crucifix (yes Jimmy Choo, I am talking about you).
  
 

 
 
 This introduction is supposed to justify why Hacivat is such a difficult fragrance for my personal taste. But it also has a chypre structure that makes an extremely interesting contrast of high and low, a poor material in a rich context. And that is a cool paradox and also a modern idea. What the strong note of oakmoss inside Hacivat does to the intense pineapple (along with the extra sour bite of grapefruit) is turning it into a bitter fruit.
 
 
And that plays with your brain, because you usually see pineapple as sweet and juicy, not particularly bitter. Bergamot comes into play to assert the chypre character which at the end of the development is disturbed by the adding of ambergris, imparting a slight salty touch. It's a complicated fragrance disguised as a falsely cheap thing in the opening. The untrained nose might not discern the truth inside.
 
 
Hacivat is part of the Shadow Play collection
 
 
 
Taking something that I perceive as lowbrow (pineapple) and juxtaposing it with something that I perceive as highbrow (oakmoss): that's a clash of conflicting interests that can have good results and this is a perfect example, even though it took me a few months and the onset of hot weather to finally come to this conclusion. You can take it the intellectual way or just enjoy the freshness.
