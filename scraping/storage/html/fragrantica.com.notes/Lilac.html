The lilac (Lat. Syrínga) is a genus of flowering woody plants that belongs to the olive family (Lat. Oleaceae). It has about 10 wild varieties which mainly grow on the territories of South-Eastern Europe and China. The most popular variety of lilacs is the common lilac (Lat. Syrínga vulgáris), and almost two thirds of all cultured varieties (over 2300 in total) have been bred on its base.
 
In some languages (e.g. Russian) the name of the plant ("сирень") stems from the Ancient Greek word σῦριγξ – syringa, which was a variety of horizontal flutes that looked like a hollow tube. This is due to the specific structure of the core of the lilac shoots, which is easy to remove. The same root is responsible for the appearance of the English word 'syringe'.
 
 
I am certain that practically everybody knows that neither natural essential oil nor lilac absolute has ever existed, and perfumers have always used synthetic materials to convey the fragrance of lilac flowers. This is true for a great many other flowers as well, for there are no natural raw materials obtained from lily-of-the-valley, lily, gardenia, linden, freesia, cyclamen and many more. I should, however, note here that lilac was one of the first flowers whose scent perfumers had figured out how to imitate, and that it happened more than 100 years ago.
 
It is quite hard to give a satisfactory answer to the question 'Why can people get essential oil from one plant but not another?' with the only suitable one being, 'Such is the nature of things!' Certain plants contain great quantities of essential oil in them, for example citrus fruits, from which the oil can be obtained simply by pressing the rinds.
 
Most essential oils are obtained by distillation, most frequently via steam distillation. The third most common way to get aromatic materials is extraction by various solvents (and here we talk about obtaining resinoids, concretes, as well as about enfleurage and the rather modern method of extraction by supercritical carbon dioxide).
 
Those who are truly familiar with essential oils and absolutes know very well that the natural aromatic material almost never smells like the original plant (possibly with the exception of the aforementioned citrus essential oils obtained by pressing).
 
Rose essential oil smells different from rose absolute and they both smell decidedly different than the living flower itself. In the dusty, leathery and animalic narcissus absolute it is extremely difficult to recognize the original flower. Certain plants don't budge when it comes to attempts at any kind of extraction or the process is extremely laborious, making the end product prohibitively expensive (about 100 years ago, violet absolute was still produced, and to get 1 kilo of that absolute, people needed to manually collect about 33 tons of the flower, so it cost 82,500 golden German marks, which is close to a million US dollars in modern currency equivalents). Another case is when the substance obtained is completely devoid of any properties required for perfumery, and that is the case of lilac and many other flowers.
 
 
Let me also note that the process of extraction of odorant substances from natural objects is always associated with certain unavoidable chemical processes like oxidation or inter-reaction with a solvent. In the case of distillation, which is always conducted at high temperatures, the spectrum of such metamorphosis is particularly wide.
 
As a result, practically any natural essential oil contains certain substances that had not been part of the original natural source, and moreover, sometimes some of those compounds per se do not exist in nature at all (!). We can say that the first people who made an infusion, decoction or distillate of any kind were essentially the first perfumery synthetic chemists, creating new synthetic odorants.
 
First attempts to recreate the fragrance of lilac were made a very long time ago, with the appearance of the first synthetic odorants. The first perfumery lilac bases, 'semi-finished' products or 'building blocks', mainly consisted of α-terpineol (this substance is part of many essential oils, but in industrial quantities it is sourced by hydration of pinene which is extracted from turpentine, respectively), heliotropin (a synthetic substance with a scent that simultaneously reminds one of vanilla, bitter almond and cinnamon), and cinnamic alcohol.
 
Notes of lilac can already be encountered in many classic fragrances of the first half of the XX century: Fleurs de Rocaille, L'Air du Temps, Madame Rochas, Femme Rochas, Anaïs Anaïs and many others.
 
 
Nearly all young Soviet chemists knew that α-terpineol smelled like lilacs: in the translated East German book 'Chemistry for the Curious' (Grosse, Weismantel,1985-1978) several chapters were devoted to odorants. One of them included a description of a method of obtaining terpineol from turpentine, which can be basically conducted at home. The book also covers methods of obtaining esters (with the most important ones described in detail) and even aliphatic aldehydes, the so-called 'higher alkanals.' In the end, the authors even provide the reader with a real 'formula' for a perfumery composition with the fragrance of lilac!

The aromatic properties of α-terpineol are very much dependent on its purity and presence of certain additives, which is why perfumery grade terpineol often costs quite a bit and is sold under special trademarks, like lindenol, tilianol, tilianol super etc.
 
Today, besides the compounds I mentioned above, the lilac accord is created on the base of liral, lilial and hydroxycitronellal which recreate the lily-of-the-valley undernote, anisic aldehyde for a light touch of powder, hexyl cinnamic aldehyde and other jasmine compounds, as well as ylang-ylang benzyl acetate, linalool, plus benzyl-, pheny ethyl- and phenyl propyl alcohols as floral fillers/thinners. The fragrance of lilac is harmoniously complemented by vanillin, musky substances, basil oil, coriander oil, citrus oils, rose oil, benzoin and other balsamic materials.
 
 
In my opinion, Jean-Claude Ellena was the one who came up with one of the best versions of the lilac accord, even though his idea may even seem a bit strange at first. He suggested to use a very simple combination of just two components, phenyl ethyl alcohol with the aroma of roses, and helional, a substance that has an aquatic-fresh scent with cyclamen undertones. In order to give more body and a certain animalic tone to the accord, one needs to add traces of indole, whereas adding a small amount of clove essential oil will turn your white lilacs into purple ones.
 
With the appearance of gas chromatography and the head-space technology, we got the chance to unveil the mystery behind the fragrance of many flowers, as well as to study the volatile components of their aromas. The analysis of lilac aroma showed that there was no terpineol there, with the key components being ocimene and a few quite specific compounds called lilac alcohols and aldehydes. Various studies also found traces of methyl anthranilate, anisic aldehyde, benzaldehyde, certain ethers and indole.
 
Nevertheless, nothing beats the smell of natural lilacs in bloom, with perfumes simply being able to mentally transport us to our favorite lilac garden when the actual flowers are nowhere to be found.
 
 
 
By Mat Yudov
