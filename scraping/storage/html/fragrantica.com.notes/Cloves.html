At first glance, carnation and clove have nothing in common, and they just happen to be homonyms in my native language. Many languages have two completely different words for the two, like carnation and clove in English, Götterblume and Gewürznelke in Germain, œillet and girofle in French. At the same time, Germans can call both the flower and the spice Nelke, while Italians, for example, may say garofano and chiodo di garofano ("carnation bud").
 
 
Let's start with the flower. Carnation (Lat. Dianthus) is a species of perennial plants from the Caryophyllaceae family that comprses over three hundred varieties. The Latin word Dianthus comes from the Greek words Διός and ἀνθός ("the flower of God"), while Caryophyllaceae is also easily broken down into: κάρυον‎ ("nut") + φύλλον‎ ("leaf").
 
 
And now watch my hands closely, so to speak: some time long ago the first botanists called clove, i.e. Lat. Caryophyllus aromaticum, the "carnation tree", the very plant in the family Myrtaceae, the dried unopened flower buds of which we use as a spice today. Another Latin botanical synonym for the plant is Eugenia aromatica L., and for now please just pay attention to the Eugenia part which we will get to a bit later in our article.
 
The funniest thing in all of that clove-carnation story is that ancient botanists, at least in some countries, transferred the name from clove to carnation solely for the reason that both plants possess a rather similar scent.
 
 
The scent of carnation has been studied in detail.
 
There are natural raw materials obtained from carnation: from common carnation , Lat. Dianthus Caryophyllus, via petroleum ether extraction with 0,2 - 0,3% output, chemists obtain concrete - a light green colored waxy mass that possesses a green herbal aroma uncharacteristic of the flower. When performing extraction from the concrete using ethanol, with an output of up to 20%, we get absolute - a viscous thick liquid of olive green or brown color. It is a very interesting material with a deep intense and longlasting fragrance: woody, sweetish-honeyed, vivid-spicy, where one can detect minty-herbal nuances. When diluted to 5% or less, the absolute gains the traits of the carnation fragrance. The price for carnation absolute currently runs about 5000 Euro per kilogram.
 
 
Benzenoid compounds comprise most of the absolute: benzyl and phenylethyl alcohols, their esters, benzoates, salicylates, benzaldehyde and eugenol. Floral character of carnation is created by α-terpineol, linalool, cis-jasmone and methyl jasmonate, while the green herbal aspect is formed by cis-3-hexenol and its esters, with the larger percentage falling on the nearly odorless heavy hydrocarbons, such as tricosane, pentacosane, heptacosane and linear saturated alkanes C15 and higher. You can also find various terpenes, including ocimene and caryophyllene (I am sure by now you can already guess where the latter substance gets its name from).
 
 
Alas, many of the substances comprising carnation absolute are now considered unsafe: that certainly concerns benzyl alcohol, benzyl benzoate, benzyl salicyllate and eugenol –  today IFRA limits the use of carnation absolute depending on the percentage of those compounds. At present, carnation absolute is practically abandoned in commercial perfumery (according to Arctander, at the time when his book came out, which was about 60 years ago, the production of carnation absolute was limited to just about 30 kg per year), but it is easy to purchase, if you would like to experiment on your own.
 
 
Modern decorative carnation varieties, like so may other cultivated plants, are unfortunately practically or completely odorless. A light medicinal scent of such plants is often explained by the presence of methyl benzoate, which we had mentioned in earlier articles when talking about ylang-ylang and feijoa (aka sellowiana).
 
Usage of eugenol, iso-eugenol and the majority of their derivatives is restricted by current IFRA guidelines, which complicates things for a perfumer wishing to create a carnation accord. But new materials continue to appear: thus, Givaudan, for instance, produces Methyl Diantilis – a substance, the scent of which is similar to isoeugenol, but possessing powdery nuances, making it an even better choice when working on the carnation aroma.
 
 
Clove is native to the Maluku Islands in Indonesia. Today it is cultivated in Tanzania (about 5% of the market), Indonesia (75%), Malaysia, India, Ceylon, Madagascar (about 10%) and a few other places. It is not as difficult to cultivate clove as it is, for example, to grow vanilla: the plant proactively blooms twice a year, bringing abundant harvests. The buds are gathered and dried out in the open air, with no special fermentation procedures necessary. The contents of essential oil in the "cloves"  reaches 20%, which is a lot and can be compared to the essential oil content of citrus fruit rind that, as we know, can be obtained by simple pressing.
 
 
Natural materials obtained from cloves are more varied and their use goes well beyond the framework of perfumery.  The essential oil from branches is obtained by distillation (steam distillation, resulting in the cheapest oil of the lowest quality), leaves, buds (hydrodistillation, the most expensive and the most interesting oil for perfumers); even the plant's roots are occasionally used.
 
Unlike the leaves and the stems, the essential oil of which consists almost entirely of eugenol (its contents reaches up to 96%), cloves' buds contain 10-15% acetyl eugenol (eugenyl acetate) that contributes a great deal to the smell of the spice. When steam distilled, that substance hydrolyzes, so a more gentle method is used instead. In the process of rough hydrodistillation there appears a certain amount of caryophyllene and some other sesquiterpenes - and no such compounds were found in the living plant.
 
Light aliphatic ketones impart a rather weird fruity note to the aroma of clove essential oil.
 
 
Low-quality clove essential oil is often used to extract pure eugenol, which was formerly used to obtain vanillin: it is also the practice today, albeit on a much smaller scale.
 
People also obtain concrete, absolute and oleoresin (the latter is obviously mainly used for culinary purposes). Since the extraction normally takes place at rather low temperatures, caryophyllene is not present in the resulting products.
 
 
Eugenol, eugenyl acetate and caryophyllene are, by and large, the main components of any essential oil obtained from the clove plant. Everything else, i.e. isoeugenol, heavy terpenes (humulene, cadinene), salicylates, benzoates and some other substances are present there in trace quantities only.
 

 
 Modern botanists attribute the genus Syzygium that comprises clove to the genus Lat. Eugenia, meaning "noble". It received its name in honor of the Prince Eugene of Savoy-Carignano, a field marshal in the army of the Holy Roman Empire. Essential oils of tropical plants pertaining to the genus Eugenia were the first ones used to extract eugenol, one of our story's main protagonists.
 
   
 
 
 
Carnation and clove notes are very popular in perfumery. No matter how long a list I attempt to provide for you here it will still be incomplete, so I suggest that you write about own favorite carnation and clove-themed fragrances in the comment section below. 
 
 
Eugenol, one of the main substances shaping the scents of both clove and carnation, can be found everywhere in nature: there is plenty of it in basil, bay leaf, cananga, rose, cinnamon (especially in the essential oil obtained from leaves), citronella, jasmine, osmanthus, allspice. Eugenol is also important when creating many other accords: floral, fruity (e.g. banana), woody.
 
 
Eugenol plays an important part in one of last century's most popular and widely replicated accords, the so called mellis accord, that I had mentioned in my article concerning the aroma of honey in perfumery. The said accord is comprised of eugenol and benzyl salicylate, most typically combined with patchouli, hydroxycitronellal  (the scent of lily of the valley), woody notes, spices, and coumarin.
 
 
The combination can be found in some of the classic fragrances of years past: Youth Dew, Opium, Coco, L’Air du Temps, Moment Supreme, Tabu, Teatro alla Scala. That kind of fragrances are not as easy to find today since the accord seems somewhat old-fashioned now, and several of the ingredients comprising it are restricted by IFRA.
 

 By Mat Yudov
