The first organically synthesized fragrant substances that appeared on the chemists' shelves in the 19th century changed the face of perfumery once and for good. At that time it was important to diligently copy Mother Nature and create "nature-identical" compounds (like vanillin, coumarin, benzaldehyde, heliotropin, ionones, etc), but at the turn of the century, the first man-made materials had seen the light of day, and these materials could not be found in the real, organic world outside the lab.
 
 
The further advancement of chemical science ushered in numerous new fragrant materials from research labs, and now the number of them is estimated to be in the thousands. Chemists continue to work relentlessly, and they have their own reasons for it. And it seems that we already have invented everything one could possibly need for modeling each and every olfactory profile one can ever imagine, but still - what inspires the conquest for new molecules?
 
A new substance can find industrial application when it fits into the Procrustean measure of at least one of the following criteria:
 
Firstly, and obviously, it is significantly cheaper than its predecessor. This concerns the development of alternative ways of synthesis because this measure is useless for comparison of two different materials: there are no two different substances that smell identical.
 
Our textbook example is about vanillin synthesis: the first protocols permitted us to get a product that cost almost as much as a natural isolate, but new methods have been invented and nowadays synthetic vanillin is quite inexpensive (just check your nearby grocery store).
 
Secondly, a substance that is easier to produce is usually preferred (for example, the one that requires less production steps and where the resulting yield is higher eliminates extra expenditure on exotic machinery and reactive chemicals, etc).
 
 
Thirdly, one would most certainly prefer a substance that possesses a smell that is more intense: for example, the scent of synthetic ethyl vanillin is four times more saturated than the scent of "organic" vanillin (even though they smell differently and hence are both widely used).
 
I would also mention that the research in the field of high intensity fragrant compounds is a task of the highest priority for Symrise, a German producer of flavors and fragrances. The same is valid for stereoselective synthesis and other methods of production that permit us to create a mixture enriched with isomers of interest (for example, compare the "usual" hedione, Hedione HC, and Paradisone). Perfumers are always happy to work with more stable ingredients with a greater lasting power, that is why development of new "everlasting" materials with hesperidic, green, and flowery profiles, that are usually considered most readily volatile, is one of the key challenges for contemporary synthetic chemists who work with odorants.
 
Fourthly, the question of safety is notedly important, too. At the laboratory and development stage new and efficient compounds, that can be potentially harmful to human beings and cause environmental damage, are obliterated; ecological concerns are quite vindicable, because all the fragrant compounds sooner or later evaporate to precipitate and infiltrate into the soil and groundwater back again. It's not only about "clean" production with reduced toxic and hazardous waste (the so-called "green chemistry" is gaining traction lately), but also about chemically stable entities that are classified as persistent, hardly biotically or abiotically degradable and thus bioaccumulative: this is exactly the case of some polycyclic musk odorants. Some companies (with Firmenich among them) renounce the use of such chemicals, while certain cosmetic manufacturers indicate that aromatic compositions for their products should be devoid of ingredients with mentioned structural disadvantages.
 
 
Fifthly, the general rule is that odoriferous substances are quite chemically reactive, this is why scientists are looking for more stable compounds, boding for a longer shelf life of a whole composition. Chemically stable to a certain degree, of course - see the previous point. The compound that decomposes under 50°С will also hardly suit the perfumers' purposes.
 
The last, but not the least point to be made – a compound that possesses an unusual and unique scent, or creatively different olfactory profile, is also a great piece of luck. Remember the drastic change in the routes of perfumery in the 90's, when calone and other "aquatic" materials were introduced? Now you understand.
 
A brief starred remark: some additional functions and characteristics give a new compound a better hand as well. It can possess a bactericidal property or provide some benefit as an insect-repellent - for example, nootkatone, a compound with a citric smell and prominent grapefruit bitterness, can even work as an insecticide when used with care and moderation (it could be used in the production of bugsprays, or mosquito repellent lotions and candles, if it weren't for its astronomical costs).
 
 
 
 
 
The search for new odorants is a labor-intensive task. New structures are generally suggested on the basis of already known substances, when certain evidence-based predicted patterns of molecular architecture are deduced - the so-called osmophoric groups (potential hydrogen bond accepting groups in an odorant, chemical entities which confer odor on an otherwise odorless compound) and other structure-specific means of odor "transcription" prove to be quite useful. Analysis of facts at hand gives ground to the analogous structure suggestion; possible means of synthesis are usually described as well.
 
In Luca Turin's book “The Secret Of Scent” one can find a photo of a page from the lab journal of Jacques Vaillant, synthetic chemist: he was looking for substances with the scent of sandalwood. That happened in the 70's, and there are 45 structural formulas of molecular entities that could possess the sought-for scent.
 
One can imagine it's not the only page, but one formula required at least a week of work: source substance purification, synthesis, work-up, structure evaluation, and analysis of properties. One page illustrates at least a year of everyday work of a chemist - but only one substance of 45 really possesses the scent of sandalwood.
 
 
With advancement of computing methodologies, travail of synthetic chemists eased a bit, as some tasks in the process of research and matching are farmed to specialized software. Computers analyze existing structures and calculate molecular descriptors, i.e. spatial position, chiral center configuration, conformational parameters, dipole moments, oxidation-reduction potentials, molecular polarization, ionization potentials, geometry, etc.
 
With reference to this information, a computer concludes whether a potential structure can possess a certain scent and suggests a way of synthesis if a molecule would be necessary for a study in vitro. Such calculations noticeably speed up the search for a substance with desired properties. One should keep in mind though, that this method is almost inoperative when an entity with a unique olfactory profile is sought.
 
 
The journey of a synthetic odorant from the lab journal to the perfume bottle is long and irksome: usually such research is conducted by some of the major companies with expenditures amounting to tens of millions of dollars.
 
The aforementioned procedure of so-called screening, or enumeration of potential structures (computer-aided or theoretical), is the first step in a long, hard journey. At this stage, objectives and desired properties of a sought-for entity are described. A great team of scientists is involved in the course of structure development of a new odorant: synthetic chemists, biochemists (hence many source substances derive from the raw organic matter), IT-specialists, etc.
 
They choose and develop laboratory procedures of target compound and intermediate compound synthesis. They also suggest the closest structural analogues: it is this step, where a substance is determined, which is easier and safer to produce, with higher potential yield and lower energy costs, etc. It is this moment, when a preliminary estimation of economic efficiency is made.
 
 
After laboratory synthesis, the scent of a substance is studied - one may guess that the majority of compounds are deselected at this point. Odorless substances, substances with an odor that is far from the initial concept, barely scented and outright malodorous substances are consigned to the dustbin of history, while advanced development experimental specimens proceed further. First, toxicology tests are conducted: acutely toxic substances or specimens with other nasty properties are discarded immediately.
 
Selected substances undergo another thorough odor-testing: it helps to determine the quantitative characteristics of scent they possess and to confirm their odoriferous value. Specific tests require a lot of time, hence the possibilities of long-term adverse effects on human health need to be examined, the so-called risks of subchronic toxicity (allergenicity, carcinogenicity, teratogenicity, mutagenicity, etc); that part requires 20-30% of the total budget. It is one of the longest stages: toxicity of a new lab specimen is evaluated over three generations of rats, at least, and it takes between 2-3 years, being one of several stages that gradually grow more complex. Tens of specimens shrink to only a handful, and bureaucratic procedures begin: the substances undergo registration, licensing, and certification.
 
During final certification, a new substance undergoes obligatory state acceptance testing. In the case of experts' positive response, the substance is officially registered and acquires clear legal status, after which the technology of its industrial synthesis is developed: scientists "upscale" the laboratory machine, test it, and finally assemble an industrial full-scale facility. This stage of industrial synthesis development is the costliest, thus chemists, technicians, physicists, cost estimators, engineers, and other specialists need to perform as a perfectly-attuned orchestra.
 
 
When batch production of an odoriferous substance is officially launched, the substance undergoes a process of state validation and certification again: compounds should meet up-to-date standards, hence qualification tests of products or substances for human use are conducted. Only after that is a new substance certified to be used in perfumery and cosmetics.
 
Inventors of new compounds try to eliminate all the unnecessary issues that could possibly emerge: new substances are patented for an extended period of time (as long as 30 years), and exclusive compounds remain captive, meaning they are not commercially available for the non-insiders of a manufacturing company: only the best of the in-house perfumers bearing the title of Master Perfumer gain access to them, while such compounds remain available for the project use only.
 
 
Actually, now we approach the most exciting chapter in the history of contemporary niche perfumery. All the best and most innovative creations are "top-secret, locked up in the vaults of major companies, being inaccessible to the layperson. Manufacturing companies keep their secret ingredients undercover, and even though the creme de la creme of perfumers' stratum can gain access to the captive compounds, it is hardly imaginable that a commercial fragrance is composed with two different captives manufactured by two different companies. Independent perfumers that can acquire only commercially available ingredients in fact use a gamut from 30 years ago, and believe me - quite a lot has changed in the meantime.
 
At the biennial Simppar show that takes place in Paris, all major manufacturers demonstrate their best flavor and fragrant compounds. International majors like IFF, Givaudan, Firmenich, Symrise, Takasago, etc, are not really keen to demonstrate their secret ingredients - they mostly show their commercially available substances and bring something new and extraordinary quite infrequently. It would be no exaggeration to say that organic materials are demonstrated more eagerly than their synthetic counterparts - mostly because it is much easier to certify and validate organic substances in most parts of the EU and non-EU countries.
 
Finally, I should admit that there is a huge lot of pending issues and interesting tasks in the field of flavor and fragrance chemistry, which is why this fascinating discipline is still waiting for its impassioned admirers.
 
by Mat Yudov
