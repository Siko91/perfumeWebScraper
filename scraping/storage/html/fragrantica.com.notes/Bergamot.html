Bergamot orange (Citrus aurantium var. bergamia) is a small, roughly per-shaped citrus fruit, which grows on small trees known as bergamots. It is a cross between pear lemon and Seville orange or grapefruit. Production of bergamot is mostly limited to the Ioanion, coastal region of the province of Reggio Calabria, in South Italy, where the soil and climatic conditions are very favourable for its cultivation. It is also cultivated in Ivory Coast, Argentina and Brazil, but in no other part of the world does it fructify with the same yield and quality of essence. Bergamot is named after Italian city of Bergamo, in which its oil was first sold, and it has become a symbol of the entire region and city. The fruit is not edible and is cultivated for the production of its essential oil. The essential oil of bergamot is expressed from the ripe fruit peel and is used extensively in perfumery for its sweet freshness. Bergamot oil is also used for flavouring purposes, e.g. Earl Grey tea and the so called althea drops, in candy-making, and indeed most people know it from Earl Grey tea. But it is also very popular among perfumers, who use for many fragrances, whatever their olfactory style: fresh, oriental, chypre....
 
 
 
OLFACTORY PROFILE
 
Bergamot is sometimes called “the finest flower of citrus”, because of its very subtle and complex scent, much more than other citruses. An acidulous and fresh scent, obviously, but also bitter, fruity, and subtly floral with a mild spicy tone, recalling lavender.
 HISTORY
 
There is no conclusive evidence on the origin of bergamot, the result of crossing bitter orange with lemon for some, or crossing bitter orange with lime for others. What is certain is that bergamot trees have been growing for 600 years in Calabria, a small region between Italy and Sicily. Bergamot looks like a lime at first, but when it grows to its full extent it looks like a yellow orange. The flesh isn’t edible, but we can extract from its rind a beautiful essential oil, with acidulous, bitter, fresh, fruity and floral tones. 
 
Etymologically, the name “bergamot” is named after the Italian city of Bergamo where it has been cultivated a for long and where its oil was first sold. It has therefore become a symbol of the city and region of Bergamot.The Arab language, whose expression “beg-armûdi” means “lord’s pear”, might also be a link to its history, given that the Moors might have been the ones to introduce bergamot in Europe. 
 

 CHEMICAL STRUCTURE
 
Bergamot is composed, among others, of acetate de lynalyle and linalool, two molecules that provide its lavender-like fresh smell. Indeed, linalool is a molecule that is naturally present in bergamot, as well as in lavender, neroli, petit-grain, or coriander seed for instance. It has a fresh, floral, bittersweet scent.
 EXTRACTION METHOD
 
Like with all citruses, we use “cold expression of the rind “ to obtain bergamot essential oil. Indeed,  to capture the citrus smell, a specific technique is used, applying to apply an intense pressure on the peel to obtain the essential oil. The peel is removed from the fruit, pierced all over obtaining numerous small holes and mechanically pressed to produce the essential oil. 
 

 OLFACTORY ROLE
 
One might argue that bergamot is almost everywhere. We can smell it in a lot of fragrances, whatever their style. It is an essential note to the Cologne accord, among with orange, lemon, petit-grain, mandarin, neroli, lavender, thyme, rosemary... Its refreshing smell is also very frequent in fresh fragrances in general.
 But perfumers also use bergamot for more powerful perfumes, whatever their style. Bergamot is a “top note” so we can find it floral, chypre, woody, and even oriental fragrances. Moreover, bergamot is one of the essential notes of the Chypre accord, amidst rose, jasmine, oakmoss, patchouli and labdanum. And perfumers like to use bergamot in the top notes to contrast with ambery or vanilla dry-down, to bring the lift to heavier and rounder compositions.
 ICONIC PERFUMES FEATURING BERGAMOT
 
Bergamot is prominent in many citrus fragrances, such as L’Eau d’Hadrien by Annick Goutal, 1981. It's also the protagonist in a cooling "eau" in the Aqua Allegoria Collection from Guerlain, eponymously named Bergamote   Calabria.
 Bergamot fits well with aromatic fresh notes, as evidenced in the classic Eau Sauvage by Dior (1966).
 More recently, bergamot was one the main notes of the cooling, green and floral Eau Parfumée au Thé Vert by Bulgari (1992), where it is used to recreate the illusion of a green tea note in the perfume.
 The same perfumer, Jean-Claude Ellena, has also sublimed bergamot in the fragrance Bergamote for the niche brand The Different Company in 2003, alongside orange blossom and white musk.
 
Because of its essential place in the chypre accord, bergamot is also one note that we can smell in many a chypre fragrance, even if it remains subtle in this kind of fragrances, such as in Mitsouko by Guerlain from 1919.
 Similarly bergamot is used to refresh the round, sensual and “dark” ambery evolution of oriental fragrances, creating contrast with the balsamic, sweet and resinous drydown. The main and  most famous example   remains Guerlain's Shalimar,   which contains a staggering 30% of bergamot in the formula. 
 

 AROMATHERAPEUTICAL PROPERTIES
 
Bergamot essential oil is known for its relaxing, soothing and purifying benefits. Moreover, it said to induce good humour. Bergamot is also a beneficial element for oily skins, and last but not least,  it is   also used as an   antispamodic natural treatment.
 
SAFETY
 
Bergamot used to be present in sunscreen and has been proven to cause darkened pigmentation marks on the skin exposed to sunlight. This is why the industry has been refining the process of production, removing the terpenes and bergaptène, molecules that have photosensitising properties, from the essential oil used.
