Fresh and juicy green fragrances have become a significant perfume trend. From a supporting role, the green note has become a star following the great public demand of naturalness and freshness. There are a great number of green materials, so it is impossible to mention all of them, however, we will try to look into the most popular. Let's start from the beginning.
  
 

  
 GALBANUM 
 
 
 
Perhaps, Pierre Balmain 'Vent Vert' is the most famous green fragrance of the XX century. This "Green Wind" created by Germaine Celler has been relaunched many times, and it is rightfully considered a superstar in modern perfumery. Its dominant bitter green note is created from an overdose of galbanum essential oil. Before synthetics, it had been one of the few green materials available to perfumers at the time. Two years later, Miss Dior was launched, with galbanum, in a more modest amount, infused into its chypre accord.
 
Chanel №19 is another textbook example of a galbanum fragrance, in which it is placed in a floral-woody environment.
 
 
Galbanum's story continued in the 60s when allyl amyl glycolate was synthesized. It has a penetrating green odor with a characteristic galbanum nuance. It also has a cool metallic facet and an expressed fruity character, reminiscent of pineapple. 
 
Very often, the smell of allyl amyl glycolate is compared with the specific smell of tinned pineapple. This is a rather accurate description. The material has been known to perfumers for more than 70 years, but it got real recognition when Davidoff Cool Water was launched (about 3% of the substance).
 
 
Later, a lot of materials with a similar odor profile were synthesized: galbascone, cyclogalbanate, pharaone, spirogalbanone, and other compounds commonly containing a 1-substitute pent-4-en-1-one fragment; we already reviewed them in my article Pineapple Water for Marilyn Monroe: New from A Lab On Fire.
 
 
When instrumental methods allowed to break down the chemical composition of galbanum, it turned out that undecatrienes (specifically (3E,5Z)-undeca-1,3,5-triene, which Firmenich branded as Galbanolene Super), as well as 2-isobutyl-3-methoxypyrazine aka Galbazine, are responsible for its green scent.
 
 
 

  
 FRESHLY CUT GRASS 
 
 
 
We all know well the smell of a freshly cut lawn. Perhaps, it is the greenest smell of them all––the uplifting scent of fresh green grass, without any other distracting components. This smell is defined by С6 wound compounds: almost any plant, if you tear off its leaf and crush it with your fingers, begins to emit them. 
 
As you have probably guessed, these are substances with six carbon atoms: cis-3-hexenal and trans-2-hexenal, cis- and trans-3-hexenols, and esters of these alcohols.
 
 
It is quite difficult to extract them from green plants by the usual extracting methods, but, fortunately, they can be easily synthesized. Cis-3-hexenol aka leaf alcohol is a very popular green perfume material with a fresh grassy smell. In its pure form, it smells intense and even acrid. Its isomer, trans-3-hexenol smells more bitter and earthy, tenacious, with floral facets. Trans-2-hexenol is somewhat reminiscent of an unripe banana peel – leafy, fruity, waxy.
 
 
Esters of leaf alcohol are very popular; there are many of them at the perfumer's disposal. Cis-3-hexenyl acetate is fruitier than its alcohol, it has distinct pear-apple notes and some banana, melon, and tropical fruits facets.
 
Cis-3-hexenyl tiglate in a high concentration smells like wild mushrooms, in small amounts; it is indispensable in creating a gardenia accord.
 
Cis-3-hexenyl isovalerate has a fresh green smell, reminiscent of green apple, but it is also useful in creating accords of some wild berries, in particular blueberries.
 
 
Cis-3-hexenyl salicylate combines green aspects with floral balsamic notes, common to salicylates. Considering that it also has a strange metallic hue and a great ability to create unusual "concrete" accords, it is clear why this substance is so popular in the current trend for mineral scents. Karmaflor, a captive by Givaudan, structurally is very close to cis-3-hexenyl salicylate. Karmaflor is an important part of Tom Ford's Soleil Neige.
 
Cyclohexene derivatives are similar, particularly triplal (trivertal, zestover), isocyclocitral, cyclovertal (vertoliff). These are extremely strong-smelling materials with an aldehydic character and nuances of unripe citrus zest. To me personally, these substances are rather green with a slight blue tint, if I need to define them in color.
 
 
There is often a certain confusion with coumarin, whose scent is often described as the smell of freshly mown hay. In reality, the smell of coumarin is as far as possible from green: it is sweet (especially in high concentration), almost confectionery, like marzipan, with nuances of vanilla, almond, and heliotrope. Highly diluted, coumarin partly loses its sweetness and acquires the herbaceous, spicy, slightly tobacco character of dried leaves and semi-fresh hay.
 

  
 MINT 
 
 
 
For a long period of time, perfumers tried to avoid a bright mint solo in perfumes, allegedly due to the strong association with toothpaste. But the green trend of recent years has changed this stereotype, and now you can smell many interesting fragrances with a blazing mint scent in the foreground.
 
Different species of mint have vastly different olfactory profiles. We talked about the chemistry of mint's scent in this article. We also have spoken about perilla (shiso), a close relative to mint which is now an extremely sought-after perfume ingredient.
 
 
In many substances, minty green tones coexist with slightly medical camphor nuances.
 

 
  
 VIOLET LEAF 
 
 
 
We mentioned the smell of violet leaf when we were on the topic of the scent of violet flower. 2,6-nonadienol ("violet alcohol") and 2,6-nonadienal ("violet aldehyde") contribute the most to the scent of violet leaf and its absolute. Violet aldehyde is an important part of the legendary perfume bases of the Parmantheme family, developed by Firmenich.
 
 
One of the most important compounds with the scent of violet leaf is methyl heptine carbonate aka folione, it has been known to perfumers for nearly 120 years, however, it became popular only when Dior Fahrenheit was released, the original formula of which contained as much as 0.6% of folione (given its extremely intense character – that's a lot).
 
Folione has a dry piercing green scent, recalling violet leaf and sliced cucumber. It smells very natural, although it is not found in nature. Folione also has a specific acetylenic aspect, which occurs only in compounds with a triple bond. I can describe it as mushroomy, metallic, gasoline-like.
 
Methyl octine carbonate has a character similar to folione, it can be distinguished by its more pronounced mimosa, strawberry and melon nuances.
 
 
Due to its chemical instability and the unpleasant property of causing skin irritation when used in high concentrations, folione and its analogs were practically banned from perfumery already in the 60s. However, folione had its last big success at the end of 80s and very early 90s – after its launch, 1.400.000 bottles of Dior Fahrenheit sold in the first three months only in Europe! Nevertheless, very soon esters with triple bonds were severely limited, and the Fahrenheit formula had to be heavily modified.
 
Folione has been replaced by neofolione, undecavertol, parmavert and violettyne (the latter being a substance with a triple bond, but unlike folione, lacking an ester group), vionil (violet nitrile), violiff (the main component of the IFF MOC replacer base), liffarome, and - to a certain extent - cis-5-octenol-1.
 
 
 

  
 VEGETAL GREEN 
 
 
 
Fresh green vegetables (bell peppers, green peas and beans, asparagus, etc.) have their own characteristic smell due to the presence of pyrazines (in particular alkoxy-substituted ones) – nitrogen-containing compounds with an extremely strong odor.
 
 
Trace amounts of 2-isobutyl thiazole can give a composition a particular tomato leaf smell. Another very useful nitrogen-containing compound with a green profile is phenyl propyl pyridine aka Corps Racine, which is an extremely powerful and fairly stable compound (alas, not very common in green odorants). It has a clean, fresh green profile with bitter, vegetale (green pepper) and metallic (nasturtium) undertones.
 
 
 
 FIG LEAF 
 
 
 
Another nitrogen-containing compound which deserves a special mentioning is stemone. Olivia Giacobetti used stemone for the fig leaf accord in Diptyque Philosykos, and it can be found in many other fragrances.
 
However, in her very first green fig fragrance, L'Artisan Parfumeur Premier Figuier, Olivia introduced a different material – glycolierral, which was also used by Bertrand Duchaufour in his Nomenclature Shi-sõ.
 
 
From a chemical point of view, glycolierral is an acetal of aldehyde called maceal (ivyal). Typically, most acetals have a fairly similar smell to the original aldehyde, but slightly softer and more streamlined. The case with the maceal / glycolierral pair is quite unique: the maceal is much more intense, bitter, fresh, recalling nutmeg. Glycolierral is very soft, with rosey and lactonic nuances, slightly woody. Both compounds are endowed with the bitter aspect of ivy leaves and can be used in creating such accord.
 
Maceal and glycolierral are isolated from phellandrene (which, in its turn, is isolated from eucalyptus essential oil). Another green odorant with aldehyde-metallic and citrus-waxy nuances is vernaldehyde, which is synthesized from myrcene, which is extracted from sweet orange essential oil.
  
 

  
 FLORAL GREEN 
 
 
 
Some compounds, like for example styrallyl acetate (gardenol), combine their basic green character with a floral facet. Besides obvious floral nuances, gardenol has a grapefruit-rhubarb aspect, which is now very popular.
 
The substance had been in great demand in the middle of the last century (Nina Ricci L'Air du Temps, Jean Couturier Coriandre, Carven Ma Griffe, Miss Dior, Gres Cabochard), and has become popular again with the launch of Mugler Aura, CdG Chlorophyll Gardenia.
 
 
Green notes are essential in so many floral profiles since they infuse a certain liveliness and realism into the perfume. In some cases, they are an inseparable part of a flower scent itself, like hyacinth.
 
A number of substances have the bitter green profile characteristic of hyacinth. Phenyl acetaldehyde is one of them. It determines the natural scent of hyacinth. Among others, I can name acetals of phenyl acetaldehyde, phenylacetic acid esters; ethers (phenafleur, pandanol, anther / "green ether"), esters (ethyl hydrocinnamate), aldehydes (trifernal), and alcohols (benzyl ethyl carbinol), usually containing a phenylethyl moiety.
 
Many green-smelling substances have a somewhat abstract floral aspect: rose oxide, methyl cyclocitrone, 2-heptyl pyridine, hexalon (alpha-allyl ionone), and terpineol esters for instance.
 
 
 

  
 GREEN+ 
 
 
 
In most substances, the green scent is paired with something else:
 
Isofreshal (Innospec) is aldehydic green, slightly metallic; Alicate (Givaudan) combines green nuances with rose, rhubarb, lily and banana; Clarycet (IFF) is grassy green, with sage, saffron and tea; Gyrane (Givaudan) smells minty-spicy-vegetal-green, with a nuance of geranium; Myroxide (Firmenich) offers a sweetish lavender-metallic green with some sage and pear nuances; Mintonat (Symrise) is a spicy-sweet, minty lavender; Neoproxen (IFF) smells like tart green citrus; Romilat (Henkel/KAO) is chamomile-greens with rose nuances; Centifolether (Firmenich) is waxy rosy green.
 
 
Syvertal (IFF) is metallic green with some floral nuances of mums and gardenia, and radish. Citrowanil B (Symrise) is fresh aldehydic green, grassy-citrus, with sweet spicy nuances, like anis. Cortex Aldehyde (IFF) is ozonic green plus hyacinth and leather. Cyclacet (IFF) and Jasmacyclene (Givaudan) are woody green, combining fir needles with licorice. Verdalia A (Givaudan) is grassy green with a surprising note of watermelon.
 
 
All these and many other similar compounds can make a perfect link between the green block of the perfume composition and other accords.
 
 
 
 
 
Personally, I prefer fragrances with an expressive, convex green accord. It is interesting to observe how perfumers employ different materials and their  imagination to invent yet another unusual green fragrance, since there are so many ingredients to choose from! There are 3,182 "green perfumes" listed in the  Fragrantica database, and another thousand of fragrances with all sorts of green notes (green grass, green apple, green tangerine, etc.) And then there are 7,616 fragrances with the word "green" in its title, and 5,217 with the word "vert." It is not easy to choose from all this splendor, so I will focus on just a few of my favorites.
 
 
I adore green fragrances created by Antoine Lee. For example, the summer cottage of CoSTUME NATIONAL Cyber Garden, a classic fougère depicting a collection of vinyl records amidst freshly cut grass. In Le Cercle des Parfumeurs Createurs Vague de Folie Verte, bitter galbanum and wormwood are softened by sweet anise. Recently, Lee created Eris Parfums Green Spell, in which you can smell green in all its multifaceted glory: galbanum, violet leaf, fig leaf, tomato leaf, etc.
 
Ulrich Lang Apsu is an example of a beautiful green aquatic. LM Parfums Infinite Definitive is a meticulously crafted summer forest fragrance. The Comme des Garçons brand loves green scents no less; you can smell a toxic, psychedelic greenness made with galbanum in their Copper, and a weird vegetal greenness among the floral notes in Amazingreen. The new Clash trio is also about all sorts of green.
 
Nomenclature Shi-sõ by Bertrand Duchaufour is amazingly good in the summer heat. It has won my heart over my all-time favorites Givenchy Greenergy by Alberto Morillas and Ilias Erminidis, and the minty green lily-of-the-valley Guerlain Aqua Allegoria Herba Fresca, created by Jean-Paul Guerlain and Mathilde Laurent.
 
 
I greatly appreciate the minty start of 27 87 Wanderlust / Wandervogel and the way it transforms into a mineral aquatic accord. You can find a strikingly beautiful mint accord in the A*Men flanker Mugler A*Men Kryptomint.
 
Chanel Bel Respiro shows off its dramatic mint among flowers. Diptyque Oyedo, with its delightful green citrus, was among the first to establish the modern yuzu trend. Last year's Lolita Lempicka Green Lover sets a juicy mint-citrus green accord against a musky vanilla background.
 
 
 
Surely you have your own favorite green perfumes! Let us know!
 

 by Mat Yudov
