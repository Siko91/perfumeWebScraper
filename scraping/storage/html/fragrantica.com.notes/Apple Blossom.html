Fall is harvest time, and even though some fruits are no longer "in season," their fragrances ring beautifully in autumnal air, augmenting the spicy scent of falling leaves with their juicy ripeness.
 
 
There are only slightly fewer fruity fragrances in perfumery than there are floral ones, so it is utterly impossible to review them all at once. An entire novel-sized book would be needed for that.
 
What about the newest fruity fragrances? Well, many of them are not interesting enough and not truly worthy of attention.
 
How about the best then? It is according to the subjective opinion of the reviewer, of course, but it is easier to reach a certain degree of objectivity when you can compare the perfume with something that exists in real life.
 
Well, there is a trap there as well. For example, there are many fortunate fragrances with solo plum or orange notes, but very few with solo pomegranate or quince. One will never be able to pay equal amounts of attention to all fruits without making one of them into the proverbial "Queen of the Harvest."
 
 
 
***
  
 
 
Let me just start with the most typical fruit for my latitude, i.e. the apple. It is a very difficult topic for me, but I did a review of the recent apple Angel Eau de Toilette (2019) Mugler and Nina Rouge by Nina Ricci. Yet there are so many interesting perfumes containing a prominent apple note that you can always find two or more other interesting options.
 
Let's take Caprice De Sophie by Chabaud Maison de Parfum for instance. It is a very sweet and very juicy apple that is not the most fragrant variety: The aroma of such apples only bursts out with the juice when you bite into the fruit's skin. It softly moves to the fragrance of baked apples with raspberry jam and boiled sweetened condensed milk. It is cozy, consoling, and perfect for the fall and winter seasons.
 
Or try Daytona 10 by Panama 1924, which is a very sour green apple – wild, tart, yet fragrant. It is adorned by the fresh stormy scent of ozone, the sharp scent of lemon, and the fragrance of magnolia that got wet in the rain. The perfume is daring and gamine – or bad boy/tomboy girl, if you prefer – in terms of style, yet (s)he is entirely unafraid of bad weather and bites into those firm apples with visible pleasure.
 
 
Out of the entire series of Acqua Colonia 4711 colognes, Red Apple & Chili 4711 is farthest from the original, yet it is the brightest and the sweetest. Yes, it does smell like apples, and rather natural at that. It smells like sweet apple juice with hot red chili pepper. I also find that it smells like mulled wine, too. Or an apple that survived some frostbite but turned a bit winey in taste as a result. Strangely, it is a beautiful combination: sweet apples and chili pepper. It is especially good for wintertime.
 
A very non-trivial apple is offered by an Arabian perfumery company called OmanLuxury Oud Aquilaria — an apple tree weighed down by loads of juicy sweet-and-sour fruits, yet shedding them into brambles of juniper, rosemary, and rosehip bushes, all of the above being placed on the background of the sensual aromas of leather, spices, and oud. You get a sensation that this nook of a rather forlorn orchard will soon be engulfed in a wave of pure oriental luxury… And yet at the moment the thick and dark wave is just threatening it, so the apples are redolent with freshness in such a furious way that it seems that they try to rebel against the inevitable flood.
 
From the fragrances that I had mentioned in my past articles, I would like to remind you here of the intense note of green apple in Sirio by Mendittorosa: It is a basket of ripe fruits, apple cider, the scent of green grass and roses, a warm, sweetish breeze… Like heaven before the original sin.
 
I find Molodilnye yabloki (Apples of Youth) by Ladanika to be really good, too. Those are ripe apples covered in morning dew, surrounded by apple blossoms and fragrant lilacs. They are not just any fruits, but those of a mythical apple tree that is simultaneously blooming and giving fruit, like apples from Avalon, the isle of fairies.
 
 
The most beautiful fragrance of apples can be found in Lann-Ael by Lostmarch. I personally truly regret that Lostmarch has discontinued their business, so their delicate, crystal, lacy, and pollen-like fragrances can now only be found at auctions and perfumery flea markets. But Lann-Ael is worth it.
 
Lann-Ael combines aromas of gingerbread houses and sea breeze, buckwheat pancakes and tarred fishing boats, milk with honey and smoke, fresh cold cream and immortelle – that dry, somewhat sweet, spicy fragrance of immortelle that the coasts of Brittany and Normandy smell like. Then there is the scent of apples: a delicate scent of green apples and the appetizing scent of baked apples.
 
The apple in Lann-Ael is the most truthful apple in all of perfumery, in my opinion.
 
 
 
***
 
 
 
Pear is a mysterious note to me, even though you seem to often come across it in fragrance pyramids and its scent cannot be confused with anything else. However it is the scent of pear that manages to really merge with other fragrances that it is combined with and dissolve there in a way that it is the very note that you cannot feel. Let me suggest a few of my favorite pear fragrances for this fall.
 
 
A truly charming pear has ripened next to a rose garden imagined by Nicolai Parfumeur Createur in a perfume that scares me off with its name: Musc Intense. Musks are normally not for me, but Nicolai Parfumeur Createur has managed to tame musk into a gentle touch of flower petals and truthul moisture on plant leaves. A warm evening brings along some sweet fragrances, but the strongest of them all are pears and roses — lots and lots of roses.
 
A true classic of the pear fragrance subgenre is Petite Cherie by Annick Goutal. It is a fragrance created for a young girl: Seemingly very sweet yet too transparent to ever become cloying, the fragrance of ripe pears combines here with the aromas of fresh morning roses and milk. At least I distinctively feel that lactone kind of softness.
 
Another classic fragrance — yet in this case a luxurious classic for adults — is La Belle Helene by MDCI Parfums. This is a pear that has been simmered in sweet syrup and wine, presented with peach liqueur and ice cream, and garnished simply with a fresh pear cut in half. In La Belle Helene the dessert is presented with hawthorn flowers, osmanthus flower pollen with a linden blossom honey fragrance, and the silky coolness of irises complete with aldehyde notes and a subtle chypre bitterness. It is a multifacted, intoxicating fragrance, where in the heart of it all, the pear aroma reigns supreme.
 
 
A worthy rival of La Belle Helene is another beauty called Belle De Jour by Christian Dior. This fragrance is not as complex, but it is understandable and seductive instead. Here we have ripe pear and white peach combined with pear ice cream, a rose bouquet, and feminine powdery notes. Even though in real life peaches smell stronger than pears, it is pear that performs the solo in Belle De Jour, and the combo of its aroma together with the scent of rose powder makes the fragrance utterly enticing. I am utterly charmed by it, and I cannot hide it.
 
There is another very sweet and glamorous pear in perfumery: Ananda by M. Micallef. This is a huge, ripe pear filled to the brim with juice, topped with whipped cream, and placed inside a springtime ring-around-a-rosy type of dance performed by violets, mimosa, and white flowers, all powdered in the manner of a court lady from the 18th century.
 
 
Speaking in general, the pear dessert theme, or pears in syrup, aerated with full-fat ice cream and/or whipped cream, has caught the fancy of perfumers more than once. The most gourmand variation on the theme is Wild Pears by Montale. Just as in La Belle Helene, the pear was cooked in simple syrup with added cognac. The pastry chef of perfumery Montale is excessively generous because his dessert has it all: vanilla ice cream, whipped cream, candied oranges, white chocolate, and what not! It is intolerably sweet to my personal taste, yet the fragrance is very popular, and many people find their gourmand happiness in its embrace.
 
English Pear & Freesia by Jo Malone London is a fresh, cold pear that was picked early in the morning. It's a sweet-and-sour fragrance with notes of freesia and rhubarb. As the fragrance is developing, the scent of pear and a garden in the morning turns into the sparkling fragrance of pear cider. Being perfectly balanced and somewhat sharp, English Pear & Freesia is undoubtedly a refreshing and life-affirming fragrance.
 
A lesser-known scent but also worthy of our mention is Flanerie Dans La Verger by Jeanne en Provence. Here we have pear, apple, ice-cold lemonade, and luscious green grass. It is a family picnic in the tree shade found in a lovely fruit orchard — light and cool. A pear that someone had already taken a bite of and some effervescent bubbles of carbonated beverages.
 
 
The most unusual pear fragrance is Ciel d'Airain by Pierre Guillaume Paris: a thunderstorm in a pear orchard, with lots of wet greenery, lots of fresh air, the scent of ozone and water, a sweet scent of pears that have just been shaken by the wind, some of them torn off the tree, some broken and bursting with juice… They are not yet ripe; some pears are still very firm, but they are all disheveled by the storm and thus very fragrant.
 
 
 
***
 
 
 
Probably no other orchard fruit has been as lucky as plum in the world of perfumery. We can find it in different stages and states: from sweet pulp to its somewhat acidic skin, as well as in the form of plum wine or simply a plum color, and let's not forget the cozy aroma of plum Christmas pudding. Blue and yellow plums are easy to distinguish in perfumery, and the mirabelle variety (aka cherry plum) is different from both of them.
 
 
It was mirabelle, by the way, that was featured in the latest plum-themed perfume that has fascinated me: Naughty Fruity by Mugler. A popular variety in France, a variety that green thumb websites call "something between a plum and a cherry plum," mirabelle's sweet-and-sour, piquant fragrance is different from the scent of other plums. It seems that it's not just the baby that was the result of the union between regular and cherry plums, but something even more exotic, like a mango. There is more tartness and freshness in its scent and taste than sweet plum varieties.
 
In Naughty Fruity, mirabelle is supplemented by the rather cloying redolence of blooming tobacco, as well as the effervescent aroma of cider. However the fragrance gets thicker, and the flowers are outshined by the spicy scent of pipe tobacco, the soft aroma of cognac that adds nobility to the entire composition, and the note of suede that makes the fragrance of Naughty Fruity not in the least "naughty" but quite elegant — albeit with a dose of flirtatiousness.
 
 
Among fragrances that can still be found out there, even though it becomes increasingly hard to do so, I cannot help but mention Femme Rochas by Rochas. It is a charming, sensual, and flighty fruity chypre — more fruity than chypre because the bitter-cool note of oakmoss is basically sunk to the bottom by the honey sweetness of white plum, the aroma of prunes steeped in cognac, and the redolence of peach that is perfectly ripe, velvety, and firm.
 
Femme Rochas is long-lasting, and when its sweetness and juiciness subside, the fragrance of flowers starts shining through. We get jasmine and rose, which are not particularly sensual here, but rather modest and delicate. Plus there appears to be an utterly fiery carnation. That is when the chypre quality of the fragrance becomes scruffier and more intense.
 
The fragrance is playful, light, and refined — much like a parisienne from some 1960s French New Wave movie. In order to be truly aristocratic, Femme Rochas probably lacks sufficient coolness and enough sharp edges. The fragrance is rounded like a fruit, like flesh, like the sensation of a first sip of expensive red wine… Look for it so you can at least test it!
 
 
Overall, I think that the best plum fragrances are those created by Serge Lutens.
 
In Feminite du Bois, cedarwood is combined with plum, plum blossom, and plum wine.
 
In sumptuous Boxeuses by Serge Lutens, the juice of ripe plums, the bittersweet scent of licorice, and the aroma of soft, slightly smoky leather make for an enticing perfumery bouquet. However, Boxeuses is more leathery than fruity, even though its plum note is one of the best in perfumery.
 
Serge Lutens also has the gorgeous, plentiful Bois et Fruits. It is much like Feminite du Bois, but as much as Feminite du Bois is gentle and silky, Bois et Fruits is dense and almost tactile. Its cedarwood note is less pronounced, while the aroma of plums is intense, thick, and sweet. Once warmed up on the skin, Bois et Fruits becomes deeper and even juicier, and other fruit aromas reveal themselves. e.g. peach pulp and the gentle sweetness of ripe figs.
 
Bois et Fruits's fruit bounty is something truly intoxicating, and yet it does not make you think of how appetizing those fruits are. No, you only think of how beautiful they are and how gorgeous their aromas truly seem. Much like a skillful jeweler that has finished polishing a precious stone reveals the latter's beauty to others, this perfumer is capable of showing fruit aromas in their true glory. The background for the fruit feast of Bois et Fruits is spicy and sweet resinous notes which are sensed at their most vivid during the cold months of the year.
 
 
Plum Japonais from Atelier d'Orient by Tom Ford is a thick, sweet, and resinous perfume that smells like ripe plums and plum marmalade, cinnamon and amber, saffron and benzoin, immortelle and an entire trinket box filled with all kinds of spices. It is very beautiful, but the plum note is rather just a part in the general decorative weave. Plum Japonais can be equally considered fruity and oriental-spicy. Nevertheless it is astonishingly beautiful, especially in the fall when mulled wine notes reveal themselves. For some reason, cold rainy weather truly brings out the beauty of Plum Japonais. Now is the perfect time for the fragrance!
 
Flower Bouquet Extreme by Mimmina is the pulp of sweet plum, a juicy tangerine, some candied rose petals, and the thick sweetness of jasmine. All are presented on a sweet amber-patchouli-woody base, with a light touch of chypre. In addition, you get musk, but not a killer of a musk here, but a rather harsh kind.
 
Flower Bouquet Extreme is truly extreme. It is almost furious in its longing to be noticed. It is loud and saturated, with a sillage worthy of a coronation dress. The perfume itself is like a big formal gown, something velvet and made for the evening yet worn in casual circumstances. However, plum performs the solo in this fragrance, so plum fragrance lovers must at least test this perfume.
 
Dark Purple by Montale features ripe plums, candied plums, prunes, roses, loukhoum, and lots and lots of musk. When the perfume just came out, I enjoyed it. However, I think they used to put in less musk. Or was I simply not as sensitive to musk back then?
 
If you are not afraid of musk, and you still have not tried Dark Purple, you should consider testing it. Yes, it may seem piercing to the point of shrieking, like a crazy saxophone, but it may also reveal itself with some glossy stained finish and coolness, with a noble hue of sweetish woods, bitter orange, and cranberry.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
For those of you who crave something more austere and streamlined, there is V. Intense by Robert Piguet, a warm and velvety fragrance. In it you will find sweet-and-sour plums, maroon-colored highly fragrant roses, milky notes of sandalwood and tonka bean that simultaneously create the illusion of plum-in-brandy filled chocolates, and rather dry vanilla-scented powder. V. Intense is a perfume that is cozy like a throw and yet as stunning as a formal gown.
 
However, there is a fragrance that is even more noble and more complex, and I'm talking about Chambre Noire by Olfactive Studio. At some point, it attracted me just by its name alone: "Dark Room." It sounds like it has something Gothic to it, and yet something cozy, something straight out of scary bedtime stories, but also it is a room that each of us has in their souls, a dark room where we store our unpleasant memories and shameful desires. Despite the "law" according to which all perfumes with interesting names turn out to be disappointing, Chambre Noire is a wonderful fall-winter fragrance: complex, refined, and rich thanks to the bounty of its notes.
 
Chambre Noire smells like ripe plums, plum wine, and violets; but also leather and sandalwood; dry, non-sweet vanilla; pink pepper; cinnamom; and incense. Chambre Noire is mutable. Sometimes it primarily smells like books — old books in genuine leather bindings. Yet who placed a violet in between the pages of "Sonnets from the Portuguese"? And why has it not yet wilted? Or is it only still fragrant in your imagination?
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Sometimes Chambre Noire smells like Christmas pudding with extra brandy and less sugar than usual. I strongly associate the perfume with "Brideshead Revisited" by Evelyn Waugh: Luxury and beauty that are taken for granted, that people no longer know how to truly appreciate. Aristocratic manners and refinement. The spirit of the olden days. The incense aroma of a Catholic chapel, and the search for forbidden pleasures.
 
Still, you can always escape to your very own kitchen, to its warmth and its pudding, or to a library where you find poetry and delicate emotions, a place where you can forget all about the bad things happening out there. Chambre Noire can help you with that... I mean the task of forgetting the bad things!
 

 By Elena Prokofeva
