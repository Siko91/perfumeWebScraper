Castoreum is a specific fragrant secretion from the castor sacs of the beaver, one of the biggest rodents who live in Europe, Asia and North America.
 
Bevears, males and females, use castoreum in combination with urine to mark their territory. The yellowish liquid secretion includes a waxy substance and helps beaver fur remain waterproof.
 
The glad that produces castoreum is a small ovoid sac about 5 to 17 cm long and 2.5-5 cm wide. In order to collect it, you have to kill the beaver.
 
It is believed that a specific "smoked" aspect of castoreum could be obtained after drying it above the open fire or after "aging". For perfume purposes, alcoholic tinctures, resinoids and absolutes are prepared from sacs dried and crushed into small pieces.
 
 

 Bevears have been hunted since ancient times for their beautiful fur and castoreum. In the 18th century, their population started to drastically decrease, and in the beginning of the 20th century beavers were at the edge of extinction.
 
 With protection in the early 20th century, the beaver population has rebounded to an estimated 10 to 15 million, still much less than it was before the fur trade.
   
 
 
 
From ancient times, castoreum has been used as a medicine to boost the immune system reaction on different harmful factors. Although, it was recognized that many plants (rhodiola rosea, ginseng, eleuterrococcus, sea buckthorn) are more effective.
 
Castoreum has some anti-inflammatory and antipyretic effects due to salicylic acid and its derivatives, but aspirin, paracetamol or ibuprofen cope much better with these problems.
 
 
Castoreum is mainly produced for perfumery. It has a strong animalic, warm and sweet smell, with leathery nuances, and is mainly used in leather, animalic and chypre perfumes.
 
Besides perfumery, castoreum is used in flavoring tobacco, and in beekeeping to increase the honey production.
 
It is also sometimes added to food, like vanilla ice-cream: a microquantity of castoreum significantly enriches the flavor of vanilla.
 
 
The scent of castoreum depends on the beaver diet. Bevears are vegans, they eat tree bark and tree shootings. European beavers prefer birch, aspen, willow, poplar, and some herbaceous plants, like water lilies, cattail, reed.
 
Canadian beavers do not mind eating coniferous trees. Partly for this reason, castoreum from Russia always has a tar-creosote aspect, and castoreum from Canada has galbanum-coniferous nuances.
 
 
At the moment, we know about 60 compounds forming the smell of castoreum. Phenols play an important role in it, imparting a characteristic smoky facet which is sometimes described as medicinal. A smell of phenol or carbolic acid is known to everyone who has at least once opened a jar with a gouache paint. Gouache smells like phenol, it is added to the paint as an aseptic component to protect it from bacteria (the base of gouache paint is a wonderfully nutrient medium for many microorganisms).
 
Methylphenols, or cresols, in addition to their main smoky-medical smell, have an interesting mimosa and narcissus-like nuance, and this is not surprising, because it is the derivatives of cresol that give the narcissus its characteristic animalistic aspect.
 
So the presence of castoreum in some flowers is quite obvious; most often castoreum can be found in the smell of carnation, mimosa, and orchids.
 
 
Besides phenols, acetophenone, benzyl alcohol, benzoic acid, borneol and traces of ammonia are important components of the castoreum odor, all these substances were discovered in the first half of the 20th century.
 
A little later, nitrogenous compounds were discovered, they make a significant contribution to the total smell: first of all, pyrazine derivatives together with lactones give castoreum a slightly roasted, nutty nuance.
 
The only substance that is not a pyrazine derivative, shown in the picture on the lower right, was found in some varieties of tobacco. Among the nitrogenous compounds of castoreum, it was a substance discovered that is structurally very similar to muscopyridine, first found in the deer musk.
 
 
In addition to these fairly simple compounds, a number of nupharidinederivatives were found in castoreum, their total content is about 0.16%, including one specific compound named castoramine. It is believed that the presence of these substances is directly related to the diet of beavers: they are alkaloids' metabolites contained in yellow water lily (lat. Nuphar).
 
 
100 years ago, when beavers were seriously endangered, perfumers tried to find a synthetic replacement to natural castoreum. Dihydrocarveol (a green smell) and its derivatives together with a birch tar were among the most often solutions.
 
In 1930-es, one synthetic perfume base with only 2,5% of castoreum was popular, the rest of the formula was composed of anisyl acetate, bornyl acetate, para-cresol, methyl acetate, birch tar essential oil and other compounds.
 
Very often castoreum is blended with styrax, guaiacol, Peruvian balsam, and cedar essential oil. A usual companion is also isobutyl quinoline (a leather smell).
 
 
As I have already mentioned, a smell of castoreum is usually associated with classic chypre perfumes like Chanel Cuir de Russie by Ernest Beaux, or Pierre Balmain Jolie Madame by Germaine Cellier.
 
Later, we can also find it in much more modest quantities in Cabochar Gres, Aramis, Knize Ten, YSL Kouros, YSL Opium, Caron Yatagan, Paloma Picasso, Chanel Antaeus.
 
Nowadays, vivid animalic perfumes are not as often, but castoreum is stil an important part of oud accords, you can smell it in Arabian style perfumery: Kilian Cruel Intensions, L’Artisan Parfumeur Al Oudh.
 
Sometimes, castoreum is used in an abstract fantasy accords, like, for example the smell of circus, wooden filings at the arena and a popsicle stick in L’Artisan Parfumeur Dzing!, or a "smell of genitals of a freshly bathed animal", as they put it, in S-Perfume S-eX.
 
Castoreum used to be a common note in luxe fragrances, but now we smell it only in niche: Amouage Memoir, Beloved and Fate, Mona di Orio Cuir, Nishane Afrika Olifant, Mark Buxton Sexual Healing, Kinski, Serge Lutens Baptême du Feu, One of Those Sulphur [16S], Les Liquides Imaginaires Saltus.
 
 
Separately I would like to mention the fragrance Beaver by the Canadian brand Zoologist. Each perfume of this brand is dedicated to a particular animal, none of them contains ingredients of an animal origin (except for the last Hyrax fragrance, but getting a hiraceum, or an African stone, does not do any harm to the animals).
 

 Victor Wong, the founder of the brand, clearly expresses his statement:
 
"We do not want to harm animals just to smell good, our goal is to reflect their wonderful, beautiful, joyful and shocking world in our perfumes."
 
The recently re-formulated perfume Beaver is really more about a beaver's life and habitat than their castoreum; there is a lot of water, linden blossom, watery leaves, bitter shootings, tree juices, and just a little bit of leather notes.
  
 
By Mat Yudov
