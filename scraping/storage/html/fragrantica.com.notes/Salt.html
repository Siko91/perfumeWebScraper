Elena Vosnaki, Fragrantica's Greek editor, recently brought us Trendy Notes that Smell Tasty: Rhubarb and Ginger. And Miguel Matos, Fragrantica's Portuguese editor, just this week discussed his discovery of the salty Olympea. This got me to wondering, "When did salty notes become a thing?" Because considering gourmand (or edible) notes that are having their moment in the spotlight, one cannot overlook the current ubiquity of salt!
 
On Fragrantica's notes page for salt, we explain, "Odor profile: A savory note that can render interesting a marine, woody or - more intriguingly - a gourmand composition. Pairs great with vetiver and some fruits or sweet notes (chocolate, cream, biscuit) to provide an unusual and arresting effect in modern compositions. Can also be due to a high ambergris (natural or synthetic) content."
 
Salt crystallizing along the shore of the Dead Sea
 
 
 
I think we can clearly perceive salt when we taste it, but when we smell salt, what is that we are smelling? The reality of science tells us that salt, or sodium chloride, actually has no aroma due to its low volatility. Could it be the iodine that is so often included in our table salt? Interestingly, recent science reports that the smell that we most often associate with the ocean or the beach is not so much salt, or even seaweed, but dimethyl sulfide, a gas produced by bacteria consuming decay products2!
 
But why should we want to smell like salt when so often we bathe in order to rid our bodies of their own salt? Is it the lure of the oceans, a primal aroma that we long to perceive no matter where we might be? Are we attempting to keep the sea with us? Is it our physiological craving for a mineral that makes up roughly .4% of our bodies1? Or is it perhaps simply the way salt accentuates other notes that makes it so attractive in modern perfumery, like dashing crystals onto watermelon slices to bring out their sweetness?
 
The use of salt as a fragrance note had a bit of a slow start. It first appeared as a component in a fragrance composition back in 1989, a unique concept from the inimitable Olivia Giacobetti (Best in Show: The Fragrances of Olivia Giacobetti (2017)) in the short-lived Le B by Agnes B, a fragrance inspired by the scents of nature. It wasn't until 1993 that salt would show up again, this time in the ill-fated launch of the polarizing Horizon (created by Alain Astori; pictured below left) from Guy Laroche of Drakkar Noir fame. It was part of a "marine accord" that imparted an aquatic facet, and it would be used in such a manner for years to come.
 
 
 
 
 
 
However, it was Olivier Creedwho hit a home run with the inclusion of salty notes in a successful fragrance with 1995's Creed Imperial Millesime, creating a realistic marine balance to Impérial's fruit and citrus accords. Two other fragrances in the 90s also utilized salt to craft sea accords, Beth Terry's unique ginger lily and avocado themed Mare (1997) and Quicksilver's Hula (1998). Again, salt as a note was used to create the aroma of sea water.
 
 
The early 2000s saw the salt note take off in a big way with its use in fragrances from brands as diverse as Avon, Annick Goutal, and Comptoir Sud Pacifique. Similar to the preceding fragrances, in almost all cases, salt continued to most often represent the sea. Some of the standouts from this period include the curious Comme des Garcons Odeur 71, where salt was used to create a contrast to the aromas of inorganic materials, while The Different Company Sel de Vetiver and James Heeley Sel Marin once again focus on salt water.
 
 
     
 
 
 
But the decade in which the concept of salt would crystallize into a major fragrance note is the 2010s when well over 100 fragrances list salt as part of the olfactory pyramid! And nowadays, it's not always about a marine accord either. Here are just a few successful salty scents:
 
One of my favorite non-marine accords where salt plays a role is Calvin Klein Reveal (for Women) (above right), a warm, peaceful blend of cashmeran, ambergris, iris, and sandalwood. The salty notes balance the sweetness and powder of the vanilla, floral, and woody notes adding a unique edge that lasts throughout the aroma.
 
Thierry Mugler's Womanity earned quite a bit of attention due to its infamous salty caviar note. While salt itself is not listed in the composition, the almost metallic sodium tang of fish eggs was certainly an unexpected juxtaposition against sweet fig, fig leaf, and fig tree notes.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Jo Malone's Wood Sage and Sea Salt makes good use of a salt note to create the effect of marine driftwood and sea air. It's fascinating how perfumer Christine Nagel managed to capture the aroma of the taste of salt. Again, perhaps it‘s the iodine and other sea minerals that routinely give salt its olfactory definition?
 
 
The use of salt in modern fragrance presents an interesting contrast to the countless sweet, candied scents that have long dominated the market, no doubt a welcome shift for folks who prefer savory to sweet. However, who can forget the mouthwatering interpretations of sweet AND savory in fragrances such as Shay & Blue Salt Caramel?
 
 
L'Artisan perfumers Karine Vinchon and Elizabeth Maier even took us to Brazil in the cocktail-inspired Batucada, a blend of lime, cachaça, mint leaves, tiare flower, ylang ylang, coconut water, and you guessed it, marine notes of sea salt.
 
 
Whether aquatic, mineral, sweet, or savory, one thing seems to be certain, salt as a scent is here to stay!
