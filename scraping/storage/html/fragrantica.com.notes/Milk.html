Lactones are probably not the most popular class of organic compounds, but when it comes to the chemistry of fragrances, they get mentioned quite a bit. The scent of lactones is often defined as creamy and the word itself partly hints at its meaning (Lat. lac, lactis – milk). That said, lactones' aromatic spectrum is much wider, but let us begin at the beginning.
 
 
 
 
 
When you're studying chemistry in high school, fragrant substances are rarely mentioned. Esters are an example of the few exceptions, and they are traditionally viewed as products of the interaction between carboxylic acid and alcohol. Esters, a mandatory part of all compounds of that class, are circled in purple on the image below.
 
Even though organic acids and alcohols usually smell rather disgusting, their 'joint ventures' often possess a rather pleasant aroma. The textbook tells us about the aroma of fruit and that is generally true, if we talk about aliphatic saturated esters. On their own, nearly all of them smell like nail polish remover or shoe glue, but when heavily diluted they get a pleasant, abstractly fruity character. It is a combo of esters that shapes the specific gustatory and olfactory characteristics of many fruits..
 
Isoamylacetate basically smells like pears and bananas, while butyl isobutyrate is a veritable chaos of tropical fruit and overripe strawberries.
 
 
 
 
An ester double bond ensures additional hues to the palette: ethers of cis-3-hexenylacetate predictably have some freshly mown grass accords; folione (Givaudan) smells like violet leaves and watermelon; ethyl-2,4-decadienoate, aka pear ether, smells like pears and apples, while ethyl crotonate has a cognac and whiskey scent. The fragrance of allyl alcohol ethers often reminds us of pineapple.
 
A triple bond in the fragrant substances used by chemists today is a rare thing, and yet one of those few compounds that features it is folione, a very intense violet-aluminum cucumber weirdo used in the original Fahrenheit, which, alas, is very restricted in terms of its usage nowadays.
 
 
The name of an ester is typically created from its 'parent' acid and alcohol, e.g. crocodile alcohol and mosquito acid would give you a crocodile ether of mosquito acid or Crocodyl Mosquitate.
 
 
 
 
Aromatic esters ('aromatic' is used in its chemical sense here, in other words, if we don't go into details, esters with benzene rings in the formula) are a slightly different territory. Salicylates and benzoates have a floral-balsamic scent while phenylethyl alcoholic esters have a honey-rose one. One can find various olfactory colors among esters: Methyl anthranilate smells like wild strawberries, grapes and orange blossom, while certain modern musks, e.g. helvetolide, are also esters in the chemical sense.
 
Imagine a molecule that has both carboxyl (acid) and alcohol elements, i.e. an alcohol/acid that is its own Mom and Dad at the same time. An example of such a subtance is lactic acid (please refer to its formula below). It was first discovered in sour milk and it's the product of sugar fermentation.
 
The cyclical product of such intramolecular esterification (i.e. ester formation from alcohol and acid as a chemical reaction) is the very lactone we're looking into today. This class of compounds derives its name from lactide, which is formed out of two molecules of lactic acid. In a purely geometrical sense, the angle between three atoms of carbon equals roughly 109°, which is why the formation of 5- and 6-unit cycles is esterically the most convenient; they are called γ- and δ-lactones, respectively. Lactones often have the '-olide' suffix in their names, e.g. decanolide, habanolide.
 
 
One of the first lactones that attracted the attention of perfumers was γ-undecalactone (coumarin being the very first one and, as it turns out, in its chemical sense, a veritable lactone), which was independently synthesized by two groups of scientists, French and Russian. The latter procrastinated with getting their paper published, so their article finally came three years later, which is why the French are usually considered to be the pioneers of the molecule. 'Gamma' denotes the 5-unit cycle, while 'undeca', which means 11 in Latin, stands for the number of atoms in its molecule.
 
 
The industrial synthesis of γ-undecalactone was made possible quite quickly and the trade name given was 'aldehyde C-14'; at the time, perfumers were just discovering the unusual features, strength and power of fatty aldehydes, so any new 'aldehydes with numbers' were very much in demand. By naming the new raw material an 'aldehyde', and thus intentionally misleading people, producers were also partly fighting competition: the actual C-14 aldehyde (tetradecanal aka Myristyl aldehyde) has a rather weak waxy scent and is not really favored by perfumers, whereas γ-undecalactone was an instant hit, thanks to its unique scent that immediately got its 'ripe peach skin heated by the sun' label stuck to it, think legendary Guerlain Mitsouko here.
 
 
 
 
 
 
100 years later, γ-undecalactone has not lost its popularity and despite the fact that modern perfumers have many an interesting alternative to it, 'Aldehyde С-14' is proactively used to this day.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
If we remove 2 atoms of carbon from γ-undecalactone's side chain, we get another fragrance star and working horse – γ-nonalactone, aka 'aldehyde С-18' or 'coconut aldehyde'. This substance has a rather sweet, waxy coconuts'n'cream scent and it is certainly the go-to ingredient when one is creating a coconut scent or a tropical fruit fantasy type of composition. It is also an important part of many floral and fruity accords, especially those of tuberose and fig. Another one, δ-nonalactone, is coconuts'n'cream with a coumarin character and a light minty accord.
 
Decalactones combine fruity apricot and peach notes with coconut ones: γ-decalactone is more refreshing, waxy and slightly 'plastic', while, δ-decalactone is sweeter and creamier.
 
 
The so-called wine lactone has a rather unusual olfactory profile: its main theme of rather sweet coconut is intertwined with a peculiar spicy dill-and-veggies accord. However, the compound is best known for its other trait; this is a substance that the human sense of smell is most sensitive to. We can sense its scent even when its concentration is just 0,00000000000001 g per 1L of air.
 
The so-called whisky lactone (tonka bean, coconut, roasted nuts, celery) has an additional methyl substitute and its side chain has 4 atoms of carbon. By extending the side chain by 1 atom, we get methyl tuberate (waxy, floral, slightly metallic, with a touch of plum and wine), which is sometimes called 'tuberolide'. If a chain has 6 atoms, then it's apriflorene by Symrise (аpricot-peach, floral, green).
 
 
Spirolactone methyl laitone (Givaudan), apart from its coumarin-milk-coconut theme, has a tropical fruit and coconut accord, and, on top of everything, works really well in woody accords, especially when one needs to enhance the 'creaminess' of sandalwood. Its closest relative, the stronger ethyl laitone, has been patented for a long time, but the patent has recently expired, so it is likely to become widely available soon. There are many lactones possessing unique characteristics among captive odorants. Mane perfumers, for example, have tropicalone, figolide and other exotic substances.
 
Lactoscatone (Symrise) combines a rather sweet lactone accord with a distinctly animalic character that is reminiscent of civet and ambergris. It is often used to impart an animalic accord, while trace amounts of it work wonders in white floral notes.
 
Certain substances with a sweet caramel scent are also lactones, e.g. levistamel, sotolone and pantolactone.
 
 
The majority of natural plant-derived musks are macrocyclic lactones: exaltolide has a 16-unit cycle, while ambrettolide has 17. One of the most in-demand synthetic macrocyclic musks ethylene brassylate or Musk T is a 'double lactone': a cyclical ester of 2-atom ethylene glycol alcohol and dicarboxylic brassilic acid. Among other macrocyclic lactones with a musk scent, we currently have habanolide, nirvanolide, Musk R1 and certain others.
 
The trick with changing certain lactones' names into aldehydes was later reversed, since certain substances called lactones are in fact no lactones at all. For instance, maple lactone, or methyl cyclopentenolonе, a substance that smells like maple syrup, is chemically not a lactone, but a ketone, while milk lactone is a mix of unsaturated carboxylic acids. Due to the fact that many of the essential musk odorants are macrocyclic lactones, the suffix '-olide', typically denoting a lactone, has been attached to certain musks of other chemical structure, e.g. galaxolide, helvetolide, silkolide, celestolide, traseolide, romandolide and many other musks which are not lactones at all.
 
 
If one changes an atom of oxygen included in the cycle into an atom of carbon within a lactone, the resulting cyclical ketone will have a similar olfactory profile. For example, velutone (Firmenich) possesses a fruity peaches-and-apricots profile very typical for lactones, however, like many other derivatives of cyclopentanone (e.g. cis-jasmone, methyl jasmonate, hedione) it also has a celery-jasmine accord. Unlike plant-derived ones, macrocyclic animal musks are in fact ketones and not lactones.
 
 
 
 
 
Esters and lactones can be encountered in nearly any fragrance. They are basically indispensable in most floral and fruity accords. If we keep in mind that coumarin and many musks are also in that same compound class, it is hardly a surprise that the chance of meeting an ester or a lactone in a fragrance is close to 100%.
 
By Mat Yudov
