The umbellifers (Lat. Umbelliferae, aka celery family, Lat. Apiaceae) is one of the largest plant families in all of nature (over 400 genera and about 3500 varieties), and it is certainly incredibly important when it comes to agriculture. Apart from a few plants that have a rather notorious reputation (such as cowbane, aka Northern water hemlock), many plants are edible and/or used for culinary purposes: carrot, lovage, celery, parsley, parsnip, dill, fennel, cilantro, and others. 
 
 
The plants of the family are easy to recognize thanks to their characteristic umbrella-like inflorescence with small, typically white flowers. The fruit (seeds) of many umbellifers (officially their fruit is called a bifid seed) are used as a seasoning, e.g. coriander (cilantro) seeds, caraway seeds, cumin, zira, fennel, dill, ajwain (ajowan caraway), aniseed. 
 
 
There are very "perfumery friendly" plants among umbellifers, too. Of course we have angelica and galbanum, but today, carrot seed essential oil is also gaining popularity thanks to certain similarities it has with iris. Close relatives of galbanum are asafoetida (a spice reminiscent of garlic in terms of its scent is obtained from the plant's latex), and the now extinct ancient superfood silphium.
 
 
The mix-up between caraway and cumin is purely linguistic in nature in many languages (like my native Russian), since it is basically the same word in many languages. Philologists tend to think that the word appeared in Russian, Ukrainian and Belorussian via Polish and Czech from the Latin word cuminum, which in turn stems from the Greek κύμῑνον, where it appeared from the Semitic languages. Despite the fact that the plants are quite close to each other botanically and their seeds look similar, their aroma and taste will not be confused by any person who has tried them at least once.
 
 
Let us start with caraway. For culinary purposes, people almost always use varieties of the most widespread caraway known simply as caraway and Persian cumin (Lat. Carum carvi), and occasionally black (Northern) and white caraway (an annual variety known as Carum carvi var. annuum), with the white being more delicate. The main components of its essential oil are limonene and D-carvone, the latter can be considered the characteristic odorant of caraway. D-carvone possesses a spicy, aromatic, herbaceous aroma somewhat reminiscent of rye bread, possibly simply due to people's direct association (rye bread is often baked with caraway, e.g. traditional Latvian rye bread). When it comes to taste, caraway is bittersweet  (somewhat reminiscent of fennel and anise), warm, spicy, and a bit hot. Over time, caraway essential oil matures, and some of the carvone turns into carvacrol.
 
 

 Here is a perfect opportunity to remember that we live in a 3D world and optical isomers can be radically different in terms of scent. A textbook example here would be the "mirror reflection" of D-carvone, L-carvone, which possesses a sweet minty aroma. Wrigley’s Spearmint gum owes its characteristic aroma and taste exactly to L-carvone.
 
 
 
 
Caraway is a rather self-sufficient spice which does not combine with other seasonings very well, except for those that are generally somewhat similar in nature, such as dill, fennel, aniseed, and coriander. Caraway seeds are mainly used for salt brining, in cabbage dishes; it is also added to breads and used when making some types of liqueurs, for example the famous Kummel (Kümmel simply means caraway in German). 
 
 
Caraway is not a particularly popular perfumery profile and it is hard to find fragrances in which it plays a significant role. It can, however, be experienced in the magnificent Serge Lutens' Chypre Rouge, which is now available in the bell shaped flacon. Caraway notes can also be recognized in Kenzo Jungle L'Elephant, Diptyque Virgilio, Dior Hypnotic Poison and Escale a Portofino, Hermès Poivre Samarcande, YSL Yvresse, and Lancome Magnifique. Caraway is a rather polarizing ingredient: you are likely to enjoy fragrances featuring a caraway note if you also love aniseed, star anise and licorice. Others may be quite repelled by the vegetable and dill like  character of the spice.
 
 
I also probably have to mention another plant, the so-called "black caraway" (known as black cumin, nigella, kalojeera, kalonji, or kalanji, Lat. Nigella sativa), for the spice certainly deserves a separate detailed mention. The plant has no relation to either umbellifers or caraway, since it is part of the Ranunculaceae (buttercup or crowfoot) family. I personally do not understand how people equate black caraway and actual caraway: the plants are not similar by either the shape of their seeds or their aroma and taste. The characteristic scent of black caraway is shaped by such substances as thymoquinone and thymohydraquinone, so, roughly, the scent of the spice and the essential oil can be described as phenolic, reminiscent of thyme and fennel, with berry nuances of raspberry and ambrette seeds (aka abelmosk). Black caraway is almost never mentioned as a fragrance note, but you can experience it in  Christophe Laudamiel's Community The Zoo.
 
 
 
 
 
 
Cumin (Lat. Cuminum cyminum) is a plant that comes from South East Asia. Two main varieties can be encountered on the market, namely the Indian and the Middle Eastern. The main component of cumin essential oil that determines its flavor and aroma is cumin aldehyde, additionally, the oil contains pinenes, gamma-terpinene, para-cymene, and other terpenes. Indian cumin contains more cumin aldehyde, so it has a stronger aroma. The aromatic properties of caraway and cumin are radically different: the constant mix-ups concerning the two in culinary recipes leads to the use of caraway instead of cumin, which in the case of Indian, Thai, or even Mexican cuisines will simply ruin your dish.
 
 
Cumin has a warm, spicy-nutty, resinous, strong or even somewhat harsh aroma and a bittersweet flavor. Cumin also possesses a characteristic, animalic aspect which is often called "sweaty" on fragrance forums: indeed, cumin notes, especially when combined with "animalic" musks, often create a warm, corporeal connotation, as in Serge Lutens Muscs Kublaï Khän, which is "a bit too much" for many (In her review, Tanya Sanchez said that it smelled like the armpits of a cameleer who hasn't bathed in a week).
 
A detectable cumin note nearly always creates the effect of a strange kind of almost intimacy, which often divides people into two camps - the fans and those who hate it. Perfumers often go for that trick: from a rather careful and delicate use (Cartier Déclaration, where the warm cumin is juxtaposed with the cold cardamom, or the Oriental Amouage Epic, or even the musky fougère Jean Paul Gaulier Le Male) to rather daring statements such as Alexander McQueen Kingdom or Serge Lutens El Attarine.
 
 
Very often people equate regular cumin with zira, which is also wrong. Zira, or zira cumin, aka elwendia persica (Lat. Bunium persicum), and its relative bunium cylindricum (Lat. Bunium cylindricum), are close to cumin, however they are different plants. If we talk about Middle Eastern cuisine, and such dishes as pilaf, zirvak or even lamb dishes, then we are most likely dealing with zira. Zira most often has a stronger aroma, with some smoked undertones.
 
 
There is also a second level of confusion here, since ajwain (aka ajowan caraway, Lat.Trachyspermum ammi), a rather rare, exotic and seldomly used kind of spice, is often confused with zira as well. Ajowan seeds are more rounded, something halfway between caraway and coriander.
 
 
The scent of ajowan is not at all like either caraway's or cumin's; its essential oil contains up to 60% thymol, familiar to us thanks to the phenolic-medicinal scent of thyme.
 
There is another variety that looks very similar, and that is radhouni (Lat. Trachyspermum roxburghianum), popular in traditional Chinese medicine. Its scent is largely determined by apiole, which makes radhouni somewhat reminiscent of parsley.
 
 
 

 I am sure your personal collection features fragrances that contain notes of cumin and other materials obtained from umbellifers. Please tell us about your favorites in the comment section below. And if you have any beloved family recipes that feature the aforementioned spices, please share them as well!
