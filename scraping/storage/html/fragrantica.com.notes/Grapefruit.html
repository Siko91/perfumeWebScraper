Grapefruit was born far from the historical homeland of citrus fruits – Northern China. Its "mom" is pomelo (lat. Citrus maxima.) Presumably, pomelo seeds were brought to the Caribbean by an English captain Philip Shaddock, a participant of the British campaign to the West Indies, which happened sometime in the mid 17th century.
 
Pomelo bears large round fruits that are slightly pointed one end, reminiscent of pear, and the fruit has a thick skin. It is the largest citrus.
 
One of grapefruits names is pamplemousse deriving from Tamil pampa limāsu ("big citrus",) which Portuguese sailors captured as pomposos limões, and then it turned into pompelmousse and then pompelmoes in Dutch. In English it transformed into pomelo, pummelo or pumelo.
 
 
The "father" of grapefruit is the well-known sweet orange (lat. Citrus x sinensis,) or rather its variety naturalized in Jamaica. Apparently, the hybrid occurred by accident. Grapefruit was first described by the Welsh cleric and naturalist Griffith Hughes.
 
 
In his book The Natural History Of Barbados (1790,) which is a monumental work of nearly four hundred pages, he calls grapefruit a forbidden fruit and claims that the locals consider it one of the seven wonders of Barbados. The book was highly appreciated by Carl Linnaeus, the Swedish naturalist who is known as the "father of modern taxonomy," but still others considered it unreliable and fraudulent. 
 
Grapefruit got its modern name because of its appearance – grapefruit fruits grow in clusters, like grapes. For a long time, grapefruit and pomelo had not been distinguished, they were considered the same species called "shaddock" in honor of the above-mentioned British sailor. Botanists began to acknowledge their difference only in the 1830s, and a century later, after 1940, the origin of the grapefruit was established and it was given an official Latin name – Citrus x paradisi.
 
 
Grapefruit made its way to America in the 1820s and quickly gained popularity. At the beginning of the 20th century, pink varieties appeared (they are usually sweeter.) Grapefruit essential oil is obtained by pressing or distillation, mainly from the peel of fruits of the Duncan variety–light yellow fruits with very light flesh. Now in Florida and Texas, they grow many grapefruit varieties such as Oro Blanco, Ruby Red, Pink, Rio Star, Thompson, White Marsh, Flame, Star Ruby, Duncan, Pummelo HB, etc.
 
As a result of breeding, grapefruit has become sweeter and sweeter, losing the bitterness and astringency of the original fruit; the peel has become thinner, the seeds – smaller. It has reflected in the essential oil of the peel which has become softer, of a "lesser" character.
 
 
The scent of grapefruit essential oil is quite similar to the same of sweet and bitter oranges, as well as lemon. In his book The Diary of a Nose: A Year in the Life of a Parfumeur, Jean-Claude Ellena expresses regret that natural grapefruit essential oil smells too similar to orange, lacking in the character of fresh grapefruit.
 
The main ingredient of grapefruit essential oil is limonene. Limonene is the core ingredient of basically any citrus essential oil, with, possibly, the only exception of bergamot. Limonene is certainly responsible for juicy uplifting freshness in citruses; nevertheless, it hardly plays a significant role in creating a citrus fragrance.
 
Frankly speaking, in order to create a brighter and more convincing smelling citrus ingredient, producers reduce the amount of limonene in the oil. It is usually made by distilling it off under pressure at a low temperature, although advanced fractioning methods are much more sophisticated for a simple explanation. These kinds of oils, as you probably know, are called "folded": for example, the designation "5x fold" or "5-fold" means that such oil must be diluted 5 times with limonene to return to its original unfractionated version.
 
 
Aldehydes make an important part of any citrus essential oil. It would be a mistake to consider them as something exclusively synthetic–the amount of aldehydes in some citrus oils is significantly higher than in Chanel No 5. In the grapefruit essential oil, there is a usual complex of saturated aldehydes in which substances with an even number of carbon atoms predominate: mainly octanal (0.3-0.8%), decanal (0.3-0.6%) and dodecanal (about 0.15%.)
 
 
Aldehydes with double bonds play an important role in the smell of grapefruit: isomeric nonenals, decenesals, decadienals, sinensals, and even trans-4,5-epoxy-cis-2-decenal, widely known for its metallic "bloody" smell.
 
 
An important feature of the grapefruit smell is its sulfurous "tropical" character, and the substance making it is para-menth-1-en-8-thiol aka grapefruit mercaptan. It has an extremely intense smell – its content in the grapefruit essential oil is less than one part in a billion; however, it is responsible for the very characteristic exotic fruity note in grapefruit.
 
 
Although grapefruit mercaptan is a fairly low molecular weight sulfur-containing compound, surprisingly, in its pure form, it does not smell like the sulfuric miasmas of hell or rotting garlic, but has a quite recognizable pleasant grapefruit character. The more observant of you have probably already noticed that grapefruit mercaptan is a mercapto analog of terpineol, which makes about 0.2% of grapefruit essential oil, and about 8% of all aromatic ingredients in grapefruit juice.
 
 
The smell of grapefruit has another very important characteristic that distinguishes grapefruit from a majority of other citruses. It is a particularly bitter herbaceous woody aspect, reminiscent of vetiver. It is mainly determined by nootkatone, the amount of which in the oil can vary from 0.5 up to 2%.
 
 
Nootkatone got its name from the Nootka cypress (Alaskan cedar), from which essential oil it was isolated. You can smell nootkatone in Corps Volatils Synthetized Citrus, Sergey Borisov recently wrote about this brand.
 
Cyclic esters, in particular furan derivatives, make an important contribution to the grapefruit scent. Linalool oxide makes 12% of the aromatic composition of grapefruit, it is the second largest component after limonene. Linalool oxide brings in a floral herbaceous green nuance.
 
Another important component is 3,6-dimethyl benzofuran: it has a fairly low odor threshold, thus its participation in the general grapefruit scent is quite significant – it adds a minty-resinous nuance to grapefruit.
 
The color of grapefruit is in a direct correlation with its scent composition: white grapefruit has a higher amount of nootkatone and other minor components, while a more intensely colored one contains less of these substances, but a higher content of aldehydes instead.
 
There are quite a few synthetic materials that can help perfumer create a grapefruit profile. Often, there is a certain familiar pattern in their structure – Nootkatone / Vetivone / Valencene:
 
 
Grapefruit odorants have many facets: citrus, sulfurous blackcurrant, fruity rhubarb, woody, green. Each of them has its own spectrum of nuances: dimethylcathinone and fructalate smell very fresh, citrusy; floropal and rhubafuran have rhubarb, green scent, with a touch of marijuana; khusinil, vigoflor and rhubofix share a distinct woody character, reminiscent of vetiver.
 
 
An important tropical nuance of grapefruit is often conveyed by adding sulfur compounds, but there are other options, for example, paradisamide. It has an amazingly light ester character, luminous and exotic, while devoid of an unpleasant "feline" effect. Its scent is also quite persistent, which is a plus.
 
Perhaps, grapefruit is not among the most popular citrus notes in perfumery. In the 1990s, grapefruit was largely applied to the top citrus notes of masculine fragrances to avoid a direct reference to a typical eau de cologne.
 
Nootkatone in grapefruit already suggests an obvious grapefruit-vetiver rhyme. However, due to its very high price, nootkatone had been often imitated by rhubofix and other similar materials. 
 
 
And yet, there are perfumes created to purposely highlight the wonderful smell of grapefruit, or the very close pomelo. Among my personal favorites I can name Estee Lauder Pleasures For Men, Gritti Pomelo Sorrento, Brocard Ethno, Mugler Cologne Fly Away, Guerlain Aqua Allegoria Pamplelune.
