There is a special category of perfume notes, which is often tagged in the perfume pyramid in order to stress its exotic character, or naturalness. These notes are usually called selling notes.
 
Even if there is only ethyl vanillin in the formula (and it is not at all bad), the perfumer better call them vanilla beans, Bourbon, of course.
 
If a brand markets itself as a 100% natural, and its perfumes – as made according to your grandma's recipe you accidently found in the attic – then there should be something rare with a healthy image, like some mosquitolia graveolens absolute and it does not at all matter that first of all, it smells like rotten hay, and second, that there is actually no such ingredient in the actual perfume.
 
If your brand belongs to an avant garde niche, you can make known that your new perfume contains some captive material (a patented material by some producer, not publicly released on the market), like some Dark horse Super – no one could ever check.
 
And for your summer edition, you can raise the degree of your customers' curiosity by mentioning something exotic and sultry, like a nectar of leopard orchid, or variegated Galapagos osmanthus. They are merely words, but you already feel a hot embrace of exotic culture.
  
 
 
 
Frangipani is another name for plumeria (lat. Plumeria), tropical flowering plants in the dogbane family, Apocynaceae. Plumeria is a medium height tree, about 5-6 meters tall, and is usually twice as wide. It does not need much care, even as a house plant; all you need is bright light and good fertilizer, as well as enough space.
 
Plumeria Alba
 
Plumeria originates from Southeast Asia, and it is the national symbol of Thailand, Laos and Bali. Maya attributed it a sexual desire and fertility. Plumeria is loved in Hawaii, and although it is not an endemic Hawaian plant, it has found here a second home. Plumeria symbolizes a good spirit, and is used for making leis.
 
Plumeria flower has 5 dense petals holding on as a spiral. The smell is usually described as citrusy with a hint of gardenia, lily-of-the-valley, jasmine and spice.
 
The name plumeria was given in honor to the French botanist Charles Plumier (from XVII century), who described several tropical species of the plant. The name frangipani derives from the name of Mucio Fragipani, who made a perfume for scenting gloves.
 
Plumeria Rubra
 
Botanists distinguish 10 non-hybrid species of plumeria, and the ones of the most interest would be white plumeria (Plumeria Alba L.), red plumeria (Plumeria Rubra), and a close relative to the latter – Plumeria Acutifolia, which grows in India and is not as fragrant as rubra and alba.
 
There is a possibility of production of essential oil and absolute from plumeria flowers, but their extraction is undertaken on a small scale, mostly in India. Main ingredients which contribute to the smell of plumeria are benzyl benzoate, farnesol, farnesene, linalool, geraniol, geranyl acetone, geranyl valerate, nerolidol, benzyl salicylate, citral, (E,E)-2,4-decadienal, phenethyl alcohol, phenylacetaldehyde.
 
 
We have to keep in mind, that a scent of plumeria is usually re-created with a certain portion of fantasy. It is usually an abstract perfume accord about the smell and the look of plumeria. The scent created for gloves was, for example, quite musky animalic (with civetone).

Tiare is a species of gardenia from Tahiti (lat. Gardenia Taitensis), one of many kinds of gardenia (according to one classification, 134 species). The flower was named after Alexander Garden, a Scottish-born American physician and naturalist.
 
 
 
Henri Matisse, Fenêtre à Tahiti II (Window in Tahiti), 1936
 
Tiare is a very common flower in French Polynesia, and it is a flower symbol of Tahiti.
 
Gardenia Taitensis
 
Tiare is a shrub with dark green glossy leaves that can reach 6 meters in height. Like most gardenias, tiare has white flowers (up to 10 sm in diameter) with a heady sweet aroma.
 
Polynesian people prepare a special mixture from tiare flowers, its bark, and a coconut oil. It is called monoi, and is used for cosmetic purposes for hair and skin.
 
Paul Gauguin, Vahine no te tiare (Woman with a Flower), 1891
 

 Some very special materials define the smell of tiare, for example, dihydroconiferyl alcohol, and its various derivatives, and phenylacetaldehyde oxime methyl ester.
 
Very important in tiare's aroma are also linalool, methyl salicylate, phenyl ethyl salicylate, benzyl benzoate, cis-3-hexenyl benzoate, cis-3-hexenyl salicylate, geranyl benzoate, and other compounds (more than 150 materials have been described).
 
 
Some types of gardenia, including tiare, are used for producing perfume materials, but on a modest scale. Tiare absolute is produced among others by Firmenich and Diffusions Aromatiques.
 
Tiare absolute has a heady, very intense floral aroma with tropical aspects and characteristic watery freshness.
 
The founder of Hermitage Oils (a natural perfume oils supplier which is soon going to celebrate its 40th anniversary), Adam Michael describes tiare absolute:
 
“Aromatically tiare absolute is packed with white floral, rich heady and unique exotically charged, refreshing wetness. The opening notes showcase this intense, powerful, refreshing green water rain note that instantly makes me think of walking in a green jungle moments after the aftermath of a windswept tropical rain storm." (hermitageoils.com)

Champaca is a kind of magnolia, Magnolia Champaca, from a Magnoliaceae family. Champaca originates from Southeast Asia, it is a big evergreen tree that can reach 60 feet. There is a tip to distinguish white and yellow champaca trees if they are not blooming – yellow champaca has fuzzy leaves. While white champaca blooms the first year, it takes at least 3 years for yellow champaca to bloom.
 
 
 
Magnolia Champaca
 

 The major producer of champaca absolute is Nosy Be, an island off the northwest coast of Madagascar. Only several hundred kilos of champaca absolute are annually produced.
 
Some companies, like Robertet, produce champaca absolute in India (there is also a small quantity of champaca essential oil produced).
 
The smell of champaca is unique, reminiscent of orange blossom, ylang-ylang, cloves and tea rose, with some hints of sage, spice and guaiac wood.
 
 
 
 
Main compounds, defining the smell of champaca, first of all would be methyl benzoate (ylang-ylang, feijoa), phenethyl alcohol, phenyl acetonitrile, indole, β-ionone, methyl anthranilate, and different terpenes (farnesene, ocimene, caryophyllene).
 
 
There is also a material from Magnolia Alba (not Magnolia Champaca) first produced in China in the 90-es, which Maurice Roucel used for the first time in his Rochas Tocade, and contributed to its production. (He explained it in his interview.)
 
Chinese producers also offer an essential oil of magnolia leaves – White Champa Leaf Essential Oil, which is a very interesting and underestimated fragrant material. Its smell is etheral floral, with some oily green nuances reminiscent of juicy tulip stems and shiso leaves, with some tea-like, sage-like and coumarin nuances.

Osmanthus, or sweet olive, or fragrant tea olive, is a plant from the olive family (lat. Oleaceae, jasmine and lilac are close relatives to osmanthus). The genus counts about 36 species, mostly shrubs and small trees.
 
Osmanthus flowers have been used in china for scenting tea for centuries. For perfumery purposes, Osmanthus fragrans is usually used.
 
Osmanthus fragrans
 
Osmanthus concrete and absolute are produced in China, it takes more than 3 tons of tiny flowers to make 1 kilo of absolute. Concrete is obtained by a direct extraction, and by an enfleurage method (with fat). Sometimes osmanthus absolute by its extraction from concrete is produced in France.
 
The most important role in the osmanthus scent belongs to carotenoids: we know them from yellow and red colored fruits, like carrots and apricots, it is mostly beta-ionone and beta-damascenone, and their derivatives. Besides carotenoids, there is cis-jasmon, γ-decalactone, and a group of δ-lactones, as well as terpene alcohols (linalool, geraniol, nerolidol) and their oxide derivatives.
 
Theaspirane and theaspirone share with osmanthus their tea scent.
