 Guerlain Jicky

Perusing the aisles of modern department stores in the last couple of years we come up with an interesting phenomenon. Lavender focused fragrances aimed at women. Cast your mind back a decade ago and lavender was only considered fit for cleaning products, pot pourri, male shaving foams in a can, and for aromatherapy - said to be  a relaxing, sleep-inducing smell, a claim I always found most paradoxical since camphorous smells, such as real lavender, tend to prick the nostrils therefore keeping you awake, but I will come to that later.
 
Now we have a major trend, with major pillar fragrances such as 2019 YSL Libre (the name inspired by the little known former vintage called Eau Libre, which fascinatingly launched as a shared fragrance) and Guerlain'sMon Guerlain (2017) which was so successful - or designed so from the start - that it already boasts four variations of the original Eau de Parfum formula: the Eau de Toilette, the Eau de Parfum Intense, Mon Guerlain Florale and Mon Guerlain Bloom of Rose, plus a couple of limited editions. Wow! 
 
 
picture by Elena Knezevich
 
No wonder Mon Guerlain has such a successful course, as it was designed to be an almost identical relaunch of the already well received Mon Exclusif, a fragrance originally meant to become your very own by personalizing the bottle and the name label, but which became so very own to enough women that the company decided to sell it to all women, rendering it less...exclusive in the process. 
 
Actually, all these are synchronous with one commercial designer brand, Calvin Klein Obsessed for Women, a reworking of the Obsession concept for modern sensibilities of political correctness, and at least two unisex exclusives by a prestige brand, Chanel's Jersey (2011) and Boy (2016) from their Les Exclusifs range. The  latter would reference a masculine component, surely, since it evokes a man in Gabrielle Chanel's own life, Arthur "Boy" Capel, and is the more masculine leaning of the two smell-wise. In Jersey, however, a more feminine approach in the formula, the aromatic lavender notes are surrounded by vanilla, tonka beans, grass accords and roses, blended to provide a warm, comfortable and creamy fragrance as flexible as the fabric it was inspired by. 
 
Burberry Brit Rythm for women (2014) complements them with its claim to English lavender growing in Kent (a truly British referent), but smothered under the neroli, the Givaudan molecule Petalia, reminiscent of peony, and musk notes. It smells of those abstract sweet and sour scents of woody floral musks that Narciso has progenited for over a decade. Where is that lavender? 
 
There is one constant in the marketing of those fragrances, they're "designed for strong, bold and free women who experiment with their freedom." In the era of #metoo and a renewed interest in feminism there is a need for such verbiage, because seduction could only take a brand so far; one needs to cater to a sense of dignity too! Guerlain speaks of choices, when they back up their fragrant launch, with a concept of "the choices, emotions and dreams that embody modern femininity." 
 
Then again, this freedom, strength and feminine boldness comes wrapped in creamy billowy underscores of mille feuille in the actual scent in the bottle, so are those noble properties really a matter for our taste buds? Or is the need for alluding to the popular, if dubious, claim of vanilla acting as an aphrodisiac a perennial fall-back-upon tool in the perfume industry? One thing is certain, one cannot blame Yves Saint Laurent the man for this rather contradictory - dare I say, pharisaic? - attitude. The famous fashion designer himself was truly revolutionary by dressing women in suits and safari jackets, and he was surrounded by women who fought for their freedom and position in society.
 
The modern YSL Libre is, as our editor-in-chief Elena Knezevich says, "a very soft, diffuse powdery fragrance dressed in pastels that lacks any special distinguishing features. It comprises all of the winning market trends, aiming to be fair to each one: There is creamy powder of the most neutral shade, there are gossamer fruity musks and, while there is no lavender shaving cream per se, they did ask a man who had just used it to stand nearby and lend some of that gorgeous fragrance. We have the impression that it was not a real perfumer but A.I. that worked on Libre – not just because the ingredients are measured with razor-sharp precision here, but more thanks to how perfect a match it is in terms of what people expect from a fragrance today."
 
The former is by no means a put down. As Patricia de Nicolai wisely mentioned once, "I think that while working on original fragrances, it is interesting to give just a little hint of something that the consumer already knows." 
 
We could therefore trace the trend of lavender in feminine fragrances back to Guerlain. 
 
 
Nowadays, lavender is almost code-name for men's cologne, the backbone of a typical fougère fragrance for men, especially since archetypes such as the originally-aimed-at-women-then-usurped-by-men Guerlain Jicky cemented this notion. And it's therefore fitting that Guerlain revisited their roots tentatively when launching Mon Guerlain in 2017 with their merging of lavender (more prominently than in the Yves Saint Laurent fragrance) with their trademark orientalized vanilla. From it, a spawn of feminine fragrances with lavender came forth like mushrooms after the rain.
 
As Guerlain states, "In 1889, Aimé Guerlain created his first masterpiece, Jicky. It was classified straightaway as “the first modern perfume”. This complex creation was also the first unisex fragrance in perfumery. It marked a beginning of “emotive” perfumery, which no longer tried to copy the odour of flowers but instead aimed to stir the emotions. Jicky is a magical fragrance that blends sparkling top notes with warm and delicate base notes. At the time of its creation, women may have found Jicky puzzling, but men adopted it immediately. Today, this fresh and vigorous fragrance is the delight of women and men alike. Why the name Jicky? From his student days, Aimé Guerlain kept the enamoured memory of a young woman named Jicky to whom he paid a beautiful homage with one of his most wonderful olfactory creations. By pure coincidence, the diminutive of his nephew Jacques was also Jicky. Jicky initiated the creation of "abstract perfumery" as opposed to "figurative perfumery". With its interplay of unique facets, it was the first in the perfume world to have such a trail and tenacity. This forerunner of new modern olfactory creations is the founding father of the legendaryShalimar."
 
Or so the story goes...
 
Mon Guerlain does not dare veer into the animalic, like its forerunners Jickyand Shalimar do. The propensities of today do not allow hints of bodily odor emerging, nor would they allow the weird opening of Jicky which sometimes comes across as vaporized petrol. Instead, Mon Guerlain veers into the caramelic, with a rum & hay ambience, a "toffee" accord, which embraces the Carla lavender flower from Provence and renders it soft and pliable. After all, the starting point for Thierry Wasser was the vanillic accord, not the other way around. Was it a genius idea prompted by him? In a way. But not entirely.
 
There are several nuances lavender can take. A decade back, when I was describing the main odor constituents of Fragrantica's vast notes database one by one, I came up with the short form of "an aromatic clean note, medicinal on one end and licorice-like on the other end", and I stand by my description to this day. Hermès was the first to exploit this duality fully, thanks to Jean Claude Ellena's idea of a quintessential blend of exquisite lavender wrapped in licorice in Brin de Reglisse (in the boutique exclusives line), therefore the mischievous reconstruction of an olfactory symbol of the South of France reinvented by the gourmet touch of a ribbon of licorice. “Dressed in mat black, a magnified lavender, memory and landscape…”  
 
 
But lavender contains enough molecules and varietals to veer into various directions at once. 
 
As our Lavender database section states: "Depending on the sort, lavender essential oil can have very sweet or distinctively sharp aroma. French lavender (Lavandula angustifolia), for example, has a sweet floral aroma while Dutch lavender (Lavandula intermedia) contains higher levels of camphor and other terpenes, having very strong aromatic and sharp odor. Essential oil extracted from Lavandula intermedia is named Lavandin. This hybrid lavender has completely different chemical and therapeutic properties, even though it finds its use in the perfume industry for its refreshing notes."
 
 
It is exactly lavandin which we tend to associate with aromatherapy products, pot pourri and candles because it's cheaper to use and easier to come by. And this is the fact which makes me question the claims to sleep inducting and relaxation because of -exactly- its much higher content in stimulating camphor and terpenic molecules!
 
In fact there's solid science to back up the claim that lavender actually has a complex effect: encephalography research in human subjects in 2016 has proven that lavender oil increases beta waves in the brain, which are responsible for alertness, problem solving and decision making, while also -oddly- increasing drowsiness, by decreasing alpha waves, responsible for cognitive performance and mental co-ordination; bad for when working with machinery, then. "The lavender oil inhalation significantly decreased alpha 1 (8–10 Hz) activity at parietal and posterior temporal regions", the Masumoto study (1998) mentions. One also wonders what format and constituent of lavender oils subjects were administered this time, since most studies correlating sleep and lavender are made with the angustofolia variety on mice, please note, but I found no mention of it per se as to provenance of the materials; was it lavandin or "real" lavender? 
 
In the same study, foody smells were found to reduce level of attention, so maybe it's a focused drowsiness? I'm only joking.
 
The 2016 study mentions clearly that "Lavender odorants were associated with reduced mental stress and increased arousal rate", which takes us back to that infamous Alan Hirsch M.D. old paper, which mentioned an increase of penile blood flow in men smelling lavender (as well as pumpkin pie, apparently) which sent hoards of women searching for lavender scents, only to be met with masculine fragrances and Demeter experimental mono-themes. Maybe it does come down to good ol' seduction after all. Maybe it's just stress reducing which does the trick. 
 
 
Nowadays, it is the vanillic and licorice nuanced lavenders which are featured in any product that aims to appeal to women or women buying for babies and children. To wit, most children's products for the bath and body which claim "relaxing lavender" are mostly featuring a blend of musk with vanilla and abstract notes of extraterrestrial flowers grown on the moon; lily of the valley, jasmine and delicate, unreal roses. And this explains the divide that lavender produces; some people love the medicinal properties they have come to know from real lavender, some people abhor them, confused by the artificial construct that is advertised as "lavender" but really isn't. 
 
But back in the beginning of the 20th century, when those mental references were not already made, even herbal lavender was considered appropriate for women who wanted to exude an easy aura of elegance! Even night-time glamour in opera gloves, as some of the 1910 German cologne Uralt Lavendel (Lohse) would have us see in their glorious vintage advertising. Tracing its history, as I did in my historical piece HERE, I was fascinated to see a scent of medicinal cleanliness – with the connotations of ladylike propriety that lavender and other herbal smelling essences automatically spark in our minds, conditioned as we have been by aromatherapy parlance – being advertised as an evening scent for grand occasions!
 
 
The British equivalent, where lavender grows but achingly pushed to its botanical extremities, Yardley's English Lavender (introduced in 1913), never dared to imply it was a scent for evening, even though it does exhibit a sip of bedtime cocoa. The British reinvigorated this Edwardian concept, by the way, in Lavandula by Penhaligon's in 2002, a still-traditional lavender fragrance, but in a more soothing-to-the-ear British accent, as compared to the harsher tonalities of their Teutonic relative. These both serve as a retro-smelling reference which serves as to remind us how lavender as a concept has changed through the years. 
 
Caron's Pour un Homme (1934) gave lavender a distinctly fluffy, vanillic underpinning, like silk boxers under a suit, for men to be sure, as claimed by the name, but suitable for women too. Although not officially aimed at women, yet it's the continuing cult of this particular scent, well into the 2000s, with several online critics praising it profusely, which prompted the popularity of the combination of lavender with vanilla and amber or musk, to appeal to women. 
 
There's something sexy about making one's self feel good about themselves, giving them the confidence to be themselves, to inhabit their skin, and maybe that's the root of the concept of comfy notes producing claims to sexiness and attraction. 
 
 
Further examining the contemporary take on lavender aimed mainly at women, such as Chanel's Jersey from their Les Exclusifs range, one can see how decades of musk use in fragrance has spoiled us into mistaking the scent of "clean" for something else entirely. They smell clean and comfy, but not because of the lavender per se, it's because of the musks and the sweeter elements! 
 
If we insist with Guerlain's Mon Guerlain, and in direct juxtaposition with their above-mentioned Jicky, one can certainly see how over a century of fragrance production has seismically shifted the notion of lavender in general. From an animal-laced beast into a vanillic comforting cocoon for women who love their desserts, but don't want to be seen stuffing their mouths with it. They're well made scents, crafted expertly, to appeal to a very contemporary sensibility. It's a trend that will continue to grow. After all, there's never a lack of want for divesting guilt. Perfume is just an indulgence that divests guilt from other places.
