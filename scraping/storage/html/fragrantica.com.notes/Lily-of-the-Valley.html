So, the year 2017 has started. Those in the aromachemical industry were a bit afraid of this date at first, then they were preparing for it for quite a long time. There is even a special term for 2017 - "The Big Ban". Today we will talk about what is being banned, what will change and how it will influence us all.
 
 
There are new rules of use for certain aromatic ingredients applied from the beginning of 2017 in the European Commission: a number of allergens that producers must indicate when labeling has grown from 26 to 89; there is also a full prohibition of atranol, chloroatranol and Hydroxyisohexyl-3-cyclohexene carboxaldehyde (HICC). Atranol and Chloroatranol are the compounds of a long-suffering oak moss. But no panic at all — atranol and oak moss are not quite the same thing.
 
 
This last material I've mentioned, HICC, is more known among perfumery lovers as lyral; it possesses a gentle floral scent (that recalls lily of the valley, cyclamen and a bit of lilac) with subtle aldehydic and woody shades. There is one more lily of the valley odorant that fell into disfavour by the European Commission — butylphenyl methylpropional (BMHCA), or lilial. In September 2015, SCCS (Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety) published a report which stipulated that after three years all compositions with lilial must be reformulated.
 
Over the last few years nearly everyone in the perfume industry was occupied with identifying a substitution for lyral and lilial. What is not surprising is lyral and lilial are the ingredients of a huge number of fragrances that can be easily checked once you have a glance at any package. If a fragrance was produced several years ago, there is nearly a 100% chance that you will find among its components at least one of these materials.
 
 
Before discussing what will replace the banned lyral and lilial, let me say a couple of words about the history of lily of the valley odorants.
 
The story of the fragrant materials with the smell of lily of the valley began more than one hundred years ago. Though there were many attempts to obtain natural essential oil and essences of lily of the valley, all of these efforts led to getting a substance with a smell that didn't have anything to do with lily of the valley; that's why the value of such "essential oils" and "absolutes" is next to none. Unfortunately, not every flower can undergo traditional methods to catch its aroma, and lily of the valley is just one of them. So, as Ernest Beaux used to say, all the hope was for chemists.
 
 
The first material that recalled lily of the valley was Hydroxycitronellal. Perfumers often tenderly call this material just Hydroxy. It was synthetized for the first time in 1905 by German chemist Herman Knoll (Knoll & Co. company). Shortly thereafter, the analog was offered by the leading producers of fragrant materials: For example, in 1906 Givaudan launched Laurine, and in 1908 Firmenich's catalogue featured Cyclosia (these two materials differed by their impurity profile, by co-products). Hydroxycitronellal became the very first material with such an aromatic profile: it offers a bright, floral lily of the valley aroma with lily shades; it possesses green and watery aspects, close to watermelon; and also offers a light, tropical note. This material became famous thanks to Houbigant Quelques Fleurs, created in 1913 (the fragrance is also known as one of the first aldehydic perfumes). Another famous ode to Hydroxycitronellal and its glory hour is, of course, an oeuvre by Edmond Roudnitska - Diorissimo (1956).
 
 
Another similar material, Cyclamen Aldehyde, was obtained occasionally in 1919 and starting from 1929 it was produced by Winthrop Chemical Corporation. Cyclamen Aldehyde's production by the Swiss company Givaudan started in the 1950s. Cyclamen Aldehyde has a powerfully (unlike Hydroxycitronellal) bright, floral smell, actually recalling cyclamen - green, sweet, with lily of the valley, linden and subtle rhubarb shades.
 
 
For quite a long time these two materials, Hydroxycitronellal and Cyclamen Aldehyde, presented the whole range of lily of the valley odorants, but the situation totally changed in the second half of the 20th century. So, let me start with the heroes of this article.
 
 
In 1952, Vladimir Rodionov's study group of the All-Union Research Institute of Synthetic and Natural Fragrant Materials obtained a compound they called лилиальальдегид (lilial aldehyde). But Stanislav Voytkevitch explained, "Moscow perfumers didn't do justice to lilial aldehyde," thus I can't say how much Soviet scientists influenced national cosmetics and the perfume industry with this discovery. What is well known is the fact that on June 11, 1956 Givaudan was granted a patent for this combination, giving it a shorter name — lilial. And this became the starting point in a new history of lily of the valley in perfumery. To name some of the key trademarks of this material: lilestralis, lilyall, lysmeral, mefloral, aldehyde MBDC. Lilial differs from the above mentioned ones due to its characteristic powdery side and spicy cumin aspects.
 
 
Our second protagonist, lyral, appeared in 1960; American company IFF synthesized and started manufacturing it. Other companies produce it under the following names: cyclohexal, kovyral, landolal, lanyral, leerall, lydoucal, lysinal, mugonal, kovanol.
 
Step by step, in the new fragrances, these two materials started to complement or substitute Hydroxycitronellal. Of course, lyral and lilial are used not only in lily of the valley-centered compositions. Being a universal and rather neutral flowery "filler" they are used nearly in every perfume genre including oriental and woody-balmy families, For example, appearing in Idole de Lubin (2005 version by Olivia Giacobetti).
 
 
You have certainly noticed that all above mentioned materials are aldehydes from the chemical point of view (not to be conflated with "Perfume aldehydes" found in fragrances like Chanel #5). For a long time it was supposed that to have a lily of the valley smell, the molecule should feature an aldehyde group. Further investigations came to the fact that there are many other materials with similar odor profiles.
 
 
 
 
 

 I'd say that lily of the valley odorants present about 20% of all fragrant materials and that's why any changes or bans in this field are taken so seriously. But up to a certain moment, hydroxycitronellal, lyral and lilial were the most important lily of the valley odorants.
 
 
The first lily of the valley alcohols — materials with no aldehyde group — were discovered in the second half of the 20th century. In perfumery, alcohols are always more preferable than aldehydes as they possess higher stability. But not all the lily of the valley alcohols are "lily of the valley" ones — they often have bright rosy characters. For example, coranol is often described as very close to linalool and it is being offered as a more persistent substitute. The quest for an alcohol with a powerful and clear lily of the valley profile is still one of the key tasks for perfume chemists. In 2001 Givaudan presented Super Muguet — one of the results of this work.
 
 
From the moment when the first fragrance safety organizations appeared, lily of the valley odorants experienced trouble. Hydroxycitronellal was accused in sensitization and its content was restricted to 1% in a final product. Lilial also causes sensitization; IFRA restricts its use at 1.9% maximum. SCCS considered this conclusion to be rather mild and announced lilial precarious for use in perfumes. SCCS suspected lilial of causing mutagenic activity, though there was no proof shown — but who knows? Lyral (IFRA — 1.5%) was called by SCCS one of the main contact sensitizers, whose restriction failed and it was finally totally banned.
 
 
But every cloud has a silver lining — many respected efforts were punt into the search for alternatives and that, apparently, produced results. Now, let's see how the key corporations offer to resolve the problem of lyral and lilial substitution.
 
 
American company IFF produces quite a lot materials with a lily of the valley aroma, for example, Starfleur 40.
 
 
 
 
 

 In case you need to replace lyral and lilial in an existing fragrance, there are some base "replacers" elaborated by the company. These bases differ for fragrances, washing agents, cosmetics and hair-care products. If you are interested in specific recipes, you can easily find them over the internet — IFF doesn't hide them, which is rather surprising.
 
 
Swiss company Firmenich has its aces: as a base-substitute for lilial there is a patented formula founded on Lilflore, the newest, rather persistent lily of the valley odorant; there is Hivernal created by the company and now supposed only for in-house use; and several other rather popular ingredients. I don't think it's a top secret, rather quite a specific knowledge, but you will hardly find elsewhere on the web the structural formula of Hivernal. They have also a captive aldehyde called Mimosal.
 
 
Givaudan has a perfect replacer for lyral — their captive material called Mahonial. Mahonia is a genus of about 70 species of evergreen shrubs in the family Berberidaceae with yellow flowers featuring a subtle smell; sometimes Mahonia is called the lily of the valley bush. Having a look at the formula we see that Mahonial is very close in structure to lyral. This is an example of so called seco-structure [from lat. secare — to cut, seco — part, half; the word sex also comes from here]. With its smell, Mahonial is close to lyral, but according to those who know it, it's even more persistent. And, of course, it's free of all the negative features of lyral. Another Givaudan material that we have to name is Dupical. I'd say it is the most intense and persistent of all known lily of the valley odorants today. Above you can see the results for lily of the valley odorants γ-unsaturated aldehydes (it's patented but doesn't seem to be in mass production). There are also a captive "classical" lily of the valley aldehyde at Givaudan called Nympheal.
 
 
As far as lily of the valley odorants are required not only in fine perfumery but to produce a bulk of flavours, home care manufacturers joined the search for new persistent and safe materials. Henkel, for example, patented oxazolidine derivatives with a structure shown above as a substitute for lilial. They say that this material features the smell close to lilial but is much more persistent. Attentive readers should know that this is a rather heavy component (with Da equal to 500, meaning it should not have any smell at all). Likely, silvialoxazolidine's smell is determined by silvial, appearing during gradual decomposition of this combination; from here comes also its persistence (there is also cyclamenoxazolidine, a cyclamen aldehyde derivant).
 
 
As we can see, there was a lot of work undertaken in recent years — lyral and lilial don't appear in practically any modern fragrances; instead, a large number of new unique materials have been created. I'm sure that soon we can expect a certain interest in the lily of the valley theme. Perhaps before the ban we will see the last of the old-school lily of the valley scents, and soon we will see fragrances with the new lily of the valley materials — light, transparent and persistent. Will lily of the valley be the 2017 trend? Well, time will tell.
  
 
By Mat Yudov
