Recently, when I was sorting through spices in my kitchen, I suddenly thought that American singer Suzanne Vega often mentions different scents in her songs, including spices. This prompted me toward the topic of today's conversation about cinnamon.
 
 
Cinnamon, or Cinnamomum verum is a plant from the genus Cinnamomum, its dried bark has been used as spice since ancient times. It is found in the wild in Sri Lanka and South India but cultivated everywhere in tropical climates.
 
 
 
 
 
Cinnamon bark oil (yield up to 1%) is steam distilled or water-distilled from the dried and crushed bark. About 50-75% of the essential oil consists of cinnamaldehyde (cinnamal), followed by eugenol (4-10%), cinnamyl acetate, ortho-methoxycinnamaldehyde, β-caryophyllene, linalool, phellandrene, methyl chavicol, limonene, α-terpineol, and benzyl benzoate. Sometimes the oil contains camphor, camphene, and other substances. The distillation of cinnamon essential oil requires a certain dexterity since cinnamaldehyde is easily oxidized and turns into cinnamic acid, which is practically odorless.
 
 
 
 
 
 
Cinnamon bark oil is quite expensive, so it is often replaced by cinnamon leaf oil which is distilled from leaves of the same plant. It mainly consists of eugenol. However, if the amount of eugenol in the oil exceeds 10%, it is a reason to question its authenticity. They often trick you by adding synthetic cinnamal, but it can be easily discerned by chromatographic analysis, since synthetic cinnamal always contains noticeable traces of cinnamic acid.
 
The tricks with cinnamon do not stop here. When we talk about cinnamon from the spice cabinet in our kitchen – it is a completely different plant!
 
 
In the picture above, there is true cinnamon we were talking about on the left. On the right is what we usually have in the kitchen – cassia, or Chinese cinnamon (Cinnamomum aromaticum). You can easily tell them apart: cassia is a single rolled-up bark, while Ceylon cinnamon bark is several of them rolled-up together – when cut, it looks a bit like a cigar. The bark of cassia is thick, up to 3mm, and if you try and grind it in a coffee grinder, it usually means you can count on buying a new coffee grinder.
 
 
Essential oil is also produced from cassia. It is distilled from leaves and bark, the composition of the essential oil from them is practically the same.
 
With a yield of up to 0.7%, the essential oil consists of approximately 80% cinnamaldehyde (this is significantly higher than that of Ceylon cinnamon). A characteristic feature of this essential oil is the rather high content of coumarin (above one percent), as well as a high content of ortho-methoxycinnamaldehyde (up to 10%) and benzaldehyde.
 
 
Although the content of cinnamaldehyde in Chinese cinnamon is higher than in Ceylon, the aromatic qualities of the latter are higher, and it is more valuable and expensive.
 
It is worth mentioning another important cinnamon relative, Camphor cinnamon, or Camphor tree (camphor laurel, Latin: Cinnamomum camphora Sieb.) The composition of its essential oil is dominated by 1,8-cineole, camphor, cymene, pinene, limonene, and myrcene, it has a characteristic camphor smell, almost devoid of cinnamon. One of the names of this oil is Ravensara for the product coming from Madagascar and India.
 
One of the varieties of this tree growing in India is the Ho tree. The essential oil from its wood is rich in linalool (up to 94% for some of its species), and it is often used to extract natural linalool.
 
 
Perfumers do not have that many cinnamon materials at their disposal, in addition to natural materials we mentioned above (by the way, natural essential cinnamon oil is used in Guerlain Shalimar and Gres Cabochard) and synthetic cinnamal, there is also cinnamon alcohol (which is more balsamic and hyacinth-powdery, it is used in Chanel №5, Lanvin Arpege), and cinnamic acid esters, which, among others, play an important role in some fruity fragrances.
 
 
Related to cinnamal, amyl cinnamic and hexyl cinnamic aldehydes can be regarded as precursors of hedione. Their smell doesn't have much in common with the smell of cinnamon, however, but at the time they played an extremely important role in the formation of floral accords, especially jasmine.
 
 
 
 
 
Returning to Suzanne Vega, my next story will be about the spice important enough to be featured in one of her song's names. You may have guessed which one. At the same time, we will talk about the biological classification of plants and why a lot has changed in it recently.
 

 by Mat Yudov
