"If you can't find springtime, you create it." Thus wrote Greek poet Odysseus Elytis, in one of his many verses. And no time could be more applicable than today. In the graying uncertainty of the unknown, spring has not ceased to blossom around us, to remind us that the eternal cycle of birth, death, and rebirth is our constant compass.
 
As I sit and watch the sellers of flowers, and specifically season-appropriate hyacinths, retreat from their usual places in the market due to the curfew, I contemplate in this parting how much flowers are laden with subtext. Most of it, like with Iris (the goddess of the rainbow), derives from Greek mythology. Hyacinth (υάκινθος) means “rain blossom” and “In memory of Hyacinthus.” Ovid in his Metamorphoses recounts the story of the beautiful Spartan youth Hyacinthus, who was beloved by the sun god Apollo.
 
When throwing the discus, Apollo competed with him, sending the disc to the clouds, but while the youth ran to catch the disc it hit him with such force on the head that it killed him. Apollo, desperate that even his skill with medicine was not enough to keep him alive, made a flower spring from Hyacinthus's blood. Although one would think it's the eponymous flower, ancient sources seem to pinpoint to rather the larkspur than the modern hyacinth bulb cultivars. On its petals one could read the word of "αϊ" (aye aye) that symbolized Apollo's mourning. Therefore hyacinth came to symbolize dying and the resurrecting beauty of Nature.
 

 Painting by Jean Broc, Dying Hyacinthus and Apollo, 1801
 

 The Spartan celebration Hyacinthia, a spring rite, comprised mourning rites for the youth’s death, then celebration of his rebirth as a flower. We can clearly see the connection with the god Adonis, lover of Aphrodite, as well as the eastern Attis. Three male versions of Persephone, youths who die in order to ensure the earth’s fertility. Hyacinth therefore remains a powerful symbol of the earth's rebirth as it blooms earliest in spring.
 
As Olga Ivanova wrote for Fragrantica, "There are hundreds of hyacinth varieties grown everywhere. Their ancestors are Hyacinthus Orientalis, native to Greece, Dalmatia and Anatolia. The flower is one of the first spring guests coming from underground. They complement yellow daffodils bringing so many colours while winter is still fighting with spring: white, yellow, even orange, different pinks (from hot-hot to pure light), blue, burgundy, purple and almost black. Each color has its own fragrance. When a hyacinth just starts blooming, it produces a light floral aroma, but when the flower stem opens up the scent becomes deeper, I would say darker…"
 
It's this strange synergy between bright, green and darkly oily which I find most fascinating about hyacinths and their aroma. Τhere are compelling facets of jasmine-like, indolic freshness, that green and medicinal quality that we sometimes catch in white flowers, but also an animalic undercurrent, a chocolate-y dark depth that engulfs the freshness in earthy remnants reminiscent of the bulbous nature of the plant itself, and of organic matter decaying.
 
It was a limited edition by Christian Dior which first convinced me that I adore not only the flowers themselves, but the fragrant interpretation in perfumery as well.Remember Me(evoking the "forget-me-not" blossoms indirectly due to the name) launched in 2000 and vanished just as easily as it came. I could cite the comparably elusive Jacinthe des Bois by L'Artisan Parfumeur, a woody coup de foudre, green like spring sap, daring like tender young shoots piercing through ground and snow, refreshing like green moss suddenly sparkling with new colorful dots; but I'd be devastated if I had to recount how I finished it before replenishing...
 
 A bottle of in a pale gray-green color, the one we call celadon in Chinese pottery was chosen for Remember Me. One fateful Easter Week in 2000, I saw it with spring buzzing around inviting me to reap its fragrant fruits and laying my hand on the bottle ...like I did. A fragrant, clear and crystalline green, like a windmill playing just for your ears. A flutist who invites you across the countryside with hugs of wild hyacinths, promising lilacs full of promise, and stunning lilies of the fields. A fragrance that disappears soon, like the smile of a loved one who now sees you only as a friend. Remember Me, it shouts within you, as if a Victorian hero holding a starving bouquet, dried among the yellowing pages of a poetry book. No need...Radiohead said it all in just three lines: 
 "You're like an angel, your skin makes me cry,
 you fly like a feather in a beautiful world.
 I wish I was special, you are so special. "
 

 During the 1990s I also loved another green floral with prominent hyacinth, though I didn't quite pinpoint it as such at the time; the marrying of the green hyacinth with the bubblegum quality of tuberose gave an intense, piercingly green and sweet, floral scent in Madame Gres' Cabotine. 
 
 
 
 
There is very little of any mourning or melancholy spirit in Cabotine. Its diminutive name from Cabochard, a scent that my own dear mother used to love and wear, gave me the impression that it is meant for youthful exuberance. And indeed it evoked for me the beauty of the world in bloom. Ad the flowers proudly soak the dewiness of the early morning, as they unfold to offer their rich, optimistic bouquet. The sun high in the sky caressing my skin as I taste, as I savour, the love that is all around, the joy of living itself.
 
Among the fragrances which express hyacinth in such an exalted, bright, wonderfully vivid way is the resurrection of Jean Patou's Vacances, as overseen by Jean Kerleo in 1980 for Ma Collection. I came to experience it later on, but it marked me as the true lushness of that flower.
 
 
The green oiliness of hyacinth is mingled in Vacances with fresh and somewhat peppery lilacs (another favorite flower of mine), still retaining dew on their little petals, from a spring shower. When Henri Almeras originally made the scent in 1936, the composition celebrated the first paid vacation for employees in France. It was cause for going out in the countryside, more of a commoner's pastime than a rich woman's yachting à la Chanel who championed sunny vacations by the sea. Henceforth, the fragrance smelled of the meadows and the gardens of the French countryside, rather than the flora of Cap d'Antibes, or Capri, where citrus groves sing by the morning sun and jasmine vines greet you by the sunset. The fragrance is reissued in the Heritage Collection by Jean Patou.
 
Hyacinth has this strange quality, of evoking something pastoral, something bucolic, which encompasses even the fauna, apart from the flora. One can sense a tinge of animalic nuance underneath. It's sweet, with a fresh component, nutty overtones, honeyed clover, like something insects would use for mating and cross-pollination...
 
As our chemist Mat describes the olfactory composition of the oil, "the unique aspect of the hyacinth aroma is determined by phenylacetaldehyde, but other important ingredients that form the smell are benzyl acetate (one of the key jasmine odorants), phenylethyl alcohol with its rose smell and its esters, balmy benzyl benzoate, indole, "mushroom alcohol" 1-octen-3-ol, terpenes (among them farnesene and ocimene, which bring a certain citrusy petitgrain-like accent) and trimetoxybenzene, with a rather strange, medicinal animalic smell."
 
 
A fragrance that made hyacinth popular for many is deservedly of cult status. As reviewer Fleurine wrote, "what makes Laura Ashley No. 1 so different from today's fragrances geared for young people is that No. 1 is completely void of some of the most common notes in this genre currently. There are no ozonic or aquatic notes, there is no vanilla, no amber, no patchouli, and very little musk.'' It's the embodiment of an English garden, a side of rose alongside the greenery of daffodils and hyacinths, a little bit of spiciness in the air, but prim and pure all the same. It recalls the dresses that Diane Spencer, later on Lady D, wore during her early days as a young woman blossoming into her princely role. And although the fragrance evokes an English garden it's never chilly, but warmly green and soapy. Unfortunately, the reissued version of the classic left our reviewers severely disappointed...
 
 
A personal memento of Annick Goutal's founder was bottled inGrand Amour. It spoke of a lovers' story, more conventionally normative than Apollo's coupling, but still eerily romantic. Annick Goutal's husband Alain used to offer her flowers at her piano recitals, and those flowers included prominent stems of hyacinths, honeysuckle branches and glorious big lilies. When I discovered Grand Amour during the 1990s, I knew not of the background story which was revealed to me later on by a knowing niche boutique owner in hushed tones upon imparting the sad news of Annick's passing. The brand has since rebranded as Goutal Paris, and some of this romantic personal element has been sidetracked. It's a pity.
 
But to revert to my personal grasp of the scent, I only knew back then that the hyacinth and lily were very prominent to the point of heady. It involved snippets of a romance that was budding for me as well and it still retains wonderful memories of early beautiful times. If you like green florals with a tiny vanillic undercurrent, it's an unmissable creation.
 
 
Next come two classics of feminine perfumery. One by Guy Laroche, the other by Guerlain. I started this narration of hyacinth love with the purest scents and progressing to the warmer, and am going to trickle down to the cool and chilly, so we're in the intermediary phase right now.
 
Josephine Catapano, one of the few women perfumers in the older days, made Fidji in 1966. Fidji stands for the name of the islands located in southern part of the Pacific. Just there, on one of the Fidji islands, where the LaRoche managers were on vacation, the idea of the first Guy Laroche perfume was born. Fidji has become incredibly popular, because it is the perfume advertised first with pictures from sunny islands, far and attractive. The fresh wave of bitterish galbanum, hyacinth, lemon, bergamot is not particularly tropical and it meets the floral heart of rose, jasmine, violet, and ylang-ylang, underscored by a spicy floral nuance, interpreted as carnation, which makes the composition more headstrong and distinctive. It's a scent poised between warm and cool, between sunny and dusky, warmer in extrait de parfum, and very evocative of warmer days. 
 
 
In Guerlain's Chamade, the harsh greenness of hyacinth is mingled with the grayish, green dryness of scathing galbanum. A well known critic, Turin L, wrote a long time ago that it was the perfume with the lengthiest introduction to the main theme. The 1969 fragrance was inspired by then it-girl Francoise Sagan's novel “La Chamade”. In the time of Napoleon, ‘la chamade’ was a very fast drumbeat that called to retreat. This perfume is meant to emulate the heavy heartbeat of a person in love and its fragrance is based on hyacinth, heavy and green, and blackcurrant that can be sensed through the oily hyacinth richness. In fact it is seminal in that it incorporated blackcurrant buds for the first time (Van Cleef also claims this innovation in their First)
 
The fragrant love story of Chamade is rounded by a pretty bottle in the shape of an upside down turned heart, pierced by an arrow – a symbol of surrender to love. The clove, amber and sandalwood embrace you with warmth, tilting the scent into the floriental category (hmmm, not quite), at any rate warming up the garden into an embrace by a loved one. It's not a prim fragrance, it has the soap, the powder, the flower, but it's heaving with lust and love.
 
 
As we progress in the cooler end of the spectrum for hyacinth, I realize how much galbanum is offering to the drier bits of the flower, making it gain nuance and chill. In Ombre de Hyacinth from Tom Ford's private line of Eaux de Parfum, we find the oleoresin of galbanum boosting the green facets, but the composition by Calice Becker is more distinctive for its aqueous facets, which simultaneously bring it closer to and further apart from well-known galbanum and violet leaf classics such as Grey Flannel and Chanel No.19. I could easily lose myself to the peppery hyacinth chord of the beginning as I spray it on skin; this full-bodied green halo is impressive like a an absinthe fairy rising from a malevolent cocktail of yesteryear. The herbal and earthy facets recall something vaguely masculine, and at the same time the odd aloofness that cool scents naturally emote. It does not pretend to say "come hither" and yet it does so in an inconspicuous, unaffected manner of aristocratic pedigree.
 
In the fragrance of Anaïs Anaïs L'Original Eau de Toilette on the other hand we find a cool lily-hyacinth bouquet in which we find said flowers, but also the surrounding environment of springtime; pollen, leaves, and wet stems with soil bits stuck to them still. The soapy element of a retro fragrance, beautifully reflected in the advertising with Sarah Moon's young girls in budding sexuality, is heard from the base - this feeling is created here with moss, jasmine, vetiver, and ylang notes. The L'Original version is as good as could be expected and not to be missed by anyone loving lilies and hyacinths, it's also quite youngish in demeanor, thanks to the pervading advertising of hippie-girls with blossomy garlands in their hair.
 
 
 
Last but not least, the coolest in the bunch and possibly the most disturbing in a quiet way, is a Lutensian creation of the good old days. In Bas de Soie by Serge Lutens (silk stocking), maybe I come upon the purest chill that hyacinth can give.
 
It's an oddly cool scent, with a perverse undercurrent of suppressed erotic energy; something Belle de Jour would wear to escape into one of her fantasies before heading to the afternoon brothel. The Lutensian paradox of lacing warmth with cool, pale, grayish pallor is translated through the bitterness of galbanum, the cloudy, leaden note of musk, almost watery and soapy if you look at it a certain way, and a powdery iris that recalls overcast Parisian skies in early spring. It's a magnificently melancholic fragrance and it makes my heart ache.
 
Hyacinth stands for death, but also for rebirth.
 
By Elena Vosnaki
