 Strangers Parfumerie Cigar Rum Intense

I have never enjoyed the feeling of drunkenness, but I have always been keen on fragrances that smelled like quality liquor. In the past, when ever I happened to enjoy some good aged whiskey with its particular smoky-woody fragrance, or some gin with its delightful juniper sharpness, or even absinthe redolent with bitter greens, I regretted not owning fragrances featuring such notes.
 
Now I know that there are so many fragrances with alcoholic notes that it is utterly impossible to describe them all in one review. This is why I have chosen just the ones that seem the most interesting to me personally.
 
Let me start with the ones that are more like hard liquor..
 
 
Fumidus by Profumum Roma: to me, this is the scent of whiskey and in an absolutely delightful way. It is first and foremost the fragrance of smoke, true, but it is some kind of extremely delicious smoke. The smokiness here is stronger than that of whiskey, but the fragrance of whiskey is present at the same time. There is also the scent of leather, which could be described with 'yummy' as well. And then there is vetiver, green and smoky, too.
 
 
Fumidus shocks you first, but then it envelops you, creating a cozy cocoon. It is utterly wonderful in its trail (sillage), you simply want to follow a person wearing Fumidus inhaling it greedily. It is even nicer to wear it yourself.
 
It does not matter that it's aimed at men. The main thing here is that it smells beautiful.
 
 
According to the perfumer's idea,Voyeurisme by Nimere Parfums is supposed to provoke associations with sinful passions and fetishes, but I associate it with a cozy fireside evening, when you have come back from the cold outside, enjoyed some tasty dinner, and set yourself up comfortably with a book somewhere in the warmth of your home, slowly taking sips of whiskey from a heavy old-fashioned glass. You do not need to rush anywhere
 
The fragrance is intense like all Nimere Parfumscreations and it is beautiful. The scent of slightly cured prunes and good cherry-flavored tobacco, the scent of leather book binding and the soft nappa leather used for gloves, the somewhat sweet aroma of warmed wood; all of the above is shrouded in the fragrance of aged, noble and strong whiskey.
 
 
In my opinion, Voyeurisme is the perfect Fall-Winter fragrance for calming down your nerves, for creating a protective shield: I feel calm when wearing it. It reminds me of the fact that a cozy evening with a book will definitely come some day. An evening to reread The Hound of the Baskervilles, or some other classic mystery, making the pleasure from wearing the fragrance complete and utterly perfect.
 
 
A*Men Pure Malt by Muglercontinues the theme of the scent of whiskey in perfumery. I love fragrances by Mugler, I enjoy their limited editions for men and I like this one, too, despite the many negative reviews.
 
In my opinion, A*Men Pure Malt is the aroma of whiskey poured into a glass (however, I imagine blended whiskey here, not the single malt kind as the fragrance name suggests) and some whiskey spilled onto an oak table that has over the years absorbed many a droplet of this hard liquor redolent with smoke…
 
 
Gradually, A*Men Pure Malt becomes sweeter and turns into a whiskey used for soaking dried fruit in, possibly for some pudding. I have no idea why you would use whiskey rather than something else for the purpose, but the combination of whiskey and dried fruit is pure pleasure to me.
 
 
 
***
 
 
 
I have encountered good gin in perfumery only once, and it was in a fragrance where I had not been promised any.
 
 
I searched for gin in L'Humaniste by Frapin, but instead found fresh air, juniper shrub heated by the sun, and an orchard with ripe lemon trees instead.
 
I looked for it in Much Ado About The Duke by Penhaligon's, but instead it offered me velvety roses, fresh peonies and bitter tonic with lemon, possibly without any gin whatsoever.
 
The real aroma of gin, however, was found in White Plumage by Francesca dell'Oro. This woody-musky fragrance smells like juniper berry, an old put-out fire, spilled gin, and the bitter juice of aloe vera.
 
 
Despite the fact that the notes are not particularly loud by themselves, the fragrance creator was able to intensify them to the max. It is like a whisper turning to a piercing whistle through an amp. Despite the loudness and intensity, White Plumage does not kill you. It is more of a prominent whisper than a loud screech. Little by little, all the notes fade away, but the juniper-like, clean and clear aroma of gin stays; I like it.
 
 
 
***
 
 
 
On the contrary, there are so many good fragrances starring rum that it is difficult to select only a couple of the most interesting ones. Some of them will be featured in our review of sweet alcoholic fragrances, whereas the more macho versions are suggested below. It is most likely that you have already tried all of them, but then again, it is a good excuse for some discussion.
 
 
 
First, there isIdole by Lubin, the one in the EDT concentration, which is very different from the EDP that was launched much later. In my book, it is the only true Idole by Lubin, a lost masterpiece.
 
 
This fragrance should be gifted to two people, a couple in love, and not just to a man or a woman separately. They should use it in special moments in their lives, when they are united by either a desire for passion or an urge for adventure. I say this because Idole is a magical fragrance.
 
In everyday use, Idole smells like noble, beautifully crafted leather and golden vanilla rum. It also smells like smoke, saffron, cumin, and cloves. When it is hot outside, more sweetness comes through, as well as some candied oranges.
 
But Idole develops more beautifully either on very hot skin, heated by a source or between the sheets, or the opposite, when you are in some wide open space, bashed by cold wind. It develops differently though, like two completely separate fragrances.
 
 
In the first scenario it smells like a magic potion.
 
In the second one, the sharp leather note, the delicious rum note, a halo of freshly ground spices, all so spicy and so aromatic, make the fragrance smell like wild nature, intensifying the rage of the wind and bringing a sensation of fresh air.
 
That said, the fragrance, which is most often the case with genius Olivia Giacobetti's creations, is 'no louder than a whisper.' In order to feel all of its beauty, one needs to come in close contact with the skin redolent with Idole, or wear it.
 
The transparent, ever-changing Idole is nevertheless a very long lasting fragrance. It is capable of not only holding your soul captive, but also quietly and almost imperceptibly saturate everything around you: all of your clothes, everything in your home will smell like Idole… It will still remain quiet and smooth, regardless.
 
 
 
Abolition by Parfums et Senteurs du Pays Basque is a fragrance that, according to its creators, is made on a rum base, not on a traditional alcohol base. Its smell makes you believe it. It is deep and long lasting, more long lasting than other creations from Parfums et Senteurs du Pays Basque that I have come across.
 
Abolition is not as mysterious and noble as Idole,  but it is more intense. The aroma of rum here is amplified to the max and augmented with the floral-syrupy scent of jasmine and some hot spices. It also has something exotic to it, like a distant whiff of a tropical forest brought over by wind.
 
 
Abolition is a velvety, caressing fragrance. It is almost sweet, yet it remains on the brink there, stopping at 'almost.'
 
I do not associate Abolition with either the fanatic and passionate French revolutionaries, or the fanatic and austere American abolitionists who fought to end slavery. They were undoubtedly worthy people, especially the abolitionists, but they were too righteous for such a luscious fragrance.
 
I rather associate Abolition with the freedom to do whatever, the freedom to sin, the freedom to love and enjoy things. It is the freedom of having money and not being encumbered by certain moral principles… I imagine more of an abolitionists' worst enemy: a gorgeous plantation owner dwelling in the lap of luxury. It could also be a libertine, a person who is pro-libertinage - freedom of religion and freedom of love, quite popular in France just before the Revolution.
 
 
Such was Vicomte de Valmont, the protagonist of 'Dangerous Liaisons,' who was very prone to vice: in this case, he has lost some of his luster yet gained a certain daring. A Vicomte de Valmonte who does not die in a duel, but possibly flees to America and maybce even becomes a pirate. Something like that, or maybe even a plantation owner. Anyway, that person has taken a liking to some high quality rum.
 
 
 

 ***
 
 
A Taste of Heaven by Kilian is a vivid and truthful fragrance of absinthe.
 
Truth be told, Kilian is not 'my' kind of brand; for some reason I haven't established a great relationship with any of their perfumes, all of which seem uninteresting and overly simple to me. However, A Taste of Heaven is an exception. It is not a masterpiece by any means, but it is pleasant.
 
Absinthe, especially good quality absinthe, has a wonderful fagrance. The same is true for apothecary sagebrush infusion. Modern-day absinthe is basically devoid of thujone, i.e. the Green Fairy that gave visions to artists and poets of the end of XIXth century, often stealing their right mind and their soul in return… In any case, it smells enchanting. Kilian has been successful in capturing some of that charm.
 
 
A Taste of Heaven smells like absinthe: the bitter green scent of sagebrush, the sweetish spicy aroma of hay. There is luckily very little star anise there, because it usually ruins all absinthe fragrances with its sharpness (and there are many absinthe fragrances out there!).
 
I test all absinthe fragances that I happen to meet. When I first tried the absinthe in A Taste of Heaven, I tought that it was the best and only true absinthe in fragrance that smells like the drink alone. I no longer think so today. The note of sagebrush, namely of absinthe sagebrush, or 'the scent of the Green Fairy' is, in my current opinion, more vivid and evident in such fragrances as Angélique Noire by Guerlain, Memoir Man by Amouage, and Memoir Woman by Amouage.
 
A Taste of Heaven is simply absinthe, but that is exactly what makes it attractive: its purity and clarity, or even the certain harshness of the fragrance. It is the real deal. I am tempted to say 'honest,' but absinthe is always deceitful..
 
 
The real Green Fairy of absinthe, however, and her magic, can be found in the sweet and delicate Angélique Noire by Guerlain.
 
It is one of the fairy-like perfumes that I associate with magic. In this case it is an odious fairy. The Green Fairy of Absinthe, enchanting, inspiring and stealing your mind, must smell something like this; the smell of somewhat bitter greens, enveloped in the sweet spell of innocuous and noble vanilla.
 
Angélique Noire is built on the contrast between the somewhat bitter, tart, spicy green note - it is not even Norwegian angelica here, but a bouquet of sedges and nettle! - and the soft, fluffy and enveloping vanilla. The latter is so beautiful and sounds so noble, not overly sweet, delicately combined with almond-powdery heliotrope and tonka bean; a vanilla only Guerlain can offer. The last statement is mainly true for the vintages though.
 
Here, I do sense absinthe poured over a sugar cube. It is an absinthe that has become cloudy from the sugar, it is bittersweet, and yet it is rather more bitter than sweet.
 
 
Greens and vanilla, bitterness and sweetness, spiciness and delicateness, freshness and warmth are all balanced in the most careful way here. The green and the vanilla go hand in hand from the beginning to the very end. Depending on the weather, there are either two notes performing simultaneously, or the vanilla floats right into the greens and then comes back, intermittently. The fragrance is quite modern yet sophisticated.
 
Angélique Noire is nearly devoid of trail (sillage). It envelops you like a cocoon and can be sensed when one comes closer, but I still cannot say that it is merely a close-to-the-skin kind of scent. It is more of an arms-length kind of fragrance, whereas its longevity is in the medium range.
 
Right after application, Angélique Noire seems rather odd - sedges and vanilla, or almond milk; how does one wear it? But take heart and let it warm up on your skin just a little, allowing yourself to adjust to it, it is quite possible that you will discover its beauty, too.
 
 
It is also quite suitable for the male audience. After all, it is men, or creative male individuals to be exact, who fell victims to the Green Fairy of Absinthe more often than women.
 
 
 
Baraonda is my favorite fragrance from Nasomatto.
 
It is sweet, hot and boozy. We have whiskey, gin, rum, creamy liqueur and cognac here. From the whiskey it takes its strong smoky note, from the rum it gets the aroma of cane sugar, from the creamy liqueur comes the milky sweetness, gin gives it the aroma of juniper berry, and vodka is simply felt throughout the fragrance.
 
It is hard to recreate the aroma of vodka, I know only two fragrances with the true aroma of vodka, one of which is Baraonda and the other being Ambre Russe by Parfum d`Empire.
 
 
Also in Baraonda, you can sense the tart pomegranate juice and the aroma of mainly exotic dried fruit soaked in rum. There is also some coffee with cognac and hot chocolate with amber, as in the recipe used in the XVIIIth century. There is wood as well, drenched in alcohol. And, what is most surprising, there is a fragrance of a nighttime garden with the sweet redolence of jasmine and a very sweet, near honey-like rose.
 
Baraonda is a sweet fragrance but it would be wrong to classify it as a gourmand. It is mostly intoxicating. Other than that, it is seductive. It is a decidedly sultry fragrance. The alcohol here is so strong that I could not bring myself to put it on the same list with fragrances of sweet liqueurs and cocktails. Baraonda is for those 'who like it stronger/harder.'
 
 
 
The aroma of vodka in Ambre Russe by Parfum d`Empire is pure, almost crystal clear and surprisingly beautiful: up to the day I discovered it, I had never found the smell of vodka attractive. However this fragrance makes it into a romantic prelude to the fragrance's main perfumery melody, composed à la russe. Honestly, the scent of vodka is quite fleeting in Ambre Russe and you cannot sense it for long.
 
 
The fragrance warms up on the skin right away and then there is an utter burst of hot and spicy, smoky and resinous notes, namely cinnamon, coriander and cloves, as well as pine and juniper, delicate linden blossom, honey and strong black tea… All of the above perform on the backdrop of leather. The leather in Ambre Russe is totally of the fetish kind, i.e. rough, full of smoke and tar. There is also a whiff coming from the traditional samovar, where water is brought to a boil with pinecones: a more pronounced scent of that can only be found in Russian Tea by Masque Milano.
 
The perfume is quite persistent, it lasts on the skin for many hours, retaining all of its rich bouquet, balancing on the edge of sweetness characteristic of mead, the spicy aroma of sbiten (a spicy type of Russian mead served hot), the aromatic redolence of a coniferous forest, and the animalic sensuality of leather.
 
It is surprising, but I also sense some champagne in Ambre Russe; a certain sweet-and-sour note with that special prickling of sparkling wine.
 
Ambre Russe sounds best when worn on the skin, it needs warmth to truly develop. It is not simply appetite-provoking, it has a particular kind of elegance, like a designer fur coat made of wolf. It is truly the epitome of the àla russe trend in the European fashion of the ХХth century.
 
 
Apple Brandy By Kilian is a very honest fragrance which delivers exactly what it promises: the aroma of alcohol from an oak barrel, strong and spicy, a scent that turns into calvados and sweet overripe apples. It becomes sweeter and sweeter over time, and in the end it turns into a dish made of baked apples with caramel, generously dressed with brandy that are later set on fire in order to make the caramel taste a bit more bitter. It is boozy, spicy and yummy.
 
Another perfume with the fragrance of calvados is Cracheuse de Flammes by Serge Lutens. At first I could not understand it and thus had a hard time making friends with it. I was even disgusted and repelled when I first tried it. However, by trial and error, I found out that the beauty of Cracheuse de Flammes  opens up when it's hot outside, then the fragrance smells shocking and daring. The alcoholic notes are very vivid here: drivers should probably stay away from this fragrance. This calvados is very sweet, it has a very sharp sour note as well, and it also seems that someone drowned a whole basket of luxurious and sweet roses in it. It is a dizzying fragrance that can make you drunk.
 
 
 
***
 
 
 
 
And finally, let us turn to cognac.
 
Laudano Nero by Tiziana Terenzi: Here we have the sharp green note of absinthe, then the somewhat sweet incense, so at first nothing suggests that once the fragrance is warmed up on the skin, it will turn into an Oriental, sweet, honey-oud temptation of a scent with generous doses of amber and sandalwood, underscored by spicy herbs. There is some burnt wood, some caramel icing, much like expensive pastries have. There is also a bit of tobacco. But there is lots and lots of honey and oud.
 
I find lots of good cognac in it, too. This is expensive, aromatic cognac that leaves a light oily aftertaste, as if it caresses your throat with every sip.
 
Both cognac and absinthe are worthy choices for alcohol bottled into a flacon of some very sweet and hot Oriental fragrance!
 
 
There is only one obvious drawback to this fragrance: It is very similar to Tom Ford's Sahara Noir. Moreover, Sahara Noir is more austere and laconic, which is why it kind of wins the duel in terms of beauty. However, Laudano Nero is more seductive; it envelops and enchants you, and, despite all the richness of its bouquet, it is easier to understand than Sahara Noir.
 
Laudano Nero offers an abundance of simple yet likable pleasures: good quality liquor, exquisite desserts, and a night of love. It was created to remind people of those pleasures and to coerce them into pursuing them.
 
 
 
Passion Boisee by Frapinis a soft and warming cognac that is combined with citruses - sweet tangerines and slightly sour Sicilian oranges. There are spices here too, with nutmeg playing the lead. It is a pleasant scent of wood, sweetish and warm. There are dry aromatic herbs as well, plus the scent of delicate suede.
 
Passion Boisee combines freshness and warmth. Much like many of the other fragrances in this review, it balances on the edge of sweetness, never becoming truly sweet. It is beautiful and refined but cosy at the same time, similar to pure cashmere.
 
 
I do not know what else to compare it to. All of the associations that come to mind are quite banal: a chalet in the mountains where one has everything for a hedonistic kind of leisure time, but still a place where nothing is excessive. It is a place where one can isolate oneself from the rest of the world without sacrificing any of life's comforts. A glass of cognac, warmed by the hands, a slice of lemon, some tea peppered with spices. There is likely a rocking chair on an open deck overlooking the wooded mountains. It's a place that takes you into its sleepy embrace, where you are lulled into sleep by the caress of a plush throw.
 
Passion Boisee is relaxing, beautiful and affectionate.
 
Passion Boisee is like a cat among the other perfumes, a fluffy and purring cat.
 When the weather is wet and gloomy, it is a great remedy against the cool and the melancholy.
 
 
There is just one flaw when it comes to Passion Boisee: in warm weather, a soapy musk note tends to reveal itself. But there is an easy way to deal with it: one should simply not use it when it's warm outside!
 
 
 
By Elena Prokofeva
