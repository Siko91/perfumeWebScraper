 Honestly, I am not quite sure about many things, but for black pepper I can truthfully say: this is my favorite spice. I am totally addicted to it. I can survive without salt and I am really not picky with food, but the absence of a pepper mill on the table could plunge me into despair.
 
Black pepper is one of the few widely available spices from my childhood. My grandfather loved black pepper very much and my lunch at grandmother's was always generously seasoned with it. Myself, I add pepper to almost everything, even to pastries (although in sweet stuff I add white rather than black, but nevertheless).
 
 
Black pepper has such a complex multifaceted aroma! Its smell plays with lots of nuances, from uplifting citrusy to grassy, evergreen and sweet. It is fresh, dry, warm and piquant. It has character, a very attractive and imperative one, but at the same time it is easy paired with almost everything from a perfumer's palette. It is surprising that perfumes with a distinct black pepper accord are quite rare.
 
 
Black pepper essential oil was used as an accent in spicy oriental perfumes for a long time, there is for instance a lot of black pepper in Caron Parfum Sacré (5%). Starting from the 1980s, black pepper would appear in fougere and citrus colognes for men: Lauder for Men, Cerruti 1881, Annick Goutal Eau d’Hadrien (2%), and among the relatively new I can name Guerlain Homme. For a long time, the record of containing black pepper essential oil was held by Ralph Lauren Extreme Polo Sport (6%).
 
 

 In 2002, L’Artisan Parfumeur launched Poivre Piquant by Bertrand Duchaufour. In its composition the leitmotif of pepper (officially white, along with some interesting facts from the Kama Sutra, passion and joy) was supported by creamy lactonic accords with honey and licorice; it was one of the most unusual pepper interpretations I've ever smelled. A few years later, the magnificent Marc Jacobs Bang and Molton Brown Black Pepper, both black pepper mono-aromas, came out.
 
 
 
___________________________
 
 
 
I have been anticipating the launch of Blackpepper Comme des Garçons since the first uncertain rumors emerged. Any launch from this house I note as a big event, can you imagine with how little patience I was waiting for the launch of their mono-perfume of black pepper?!
 
 

 Nothing about the perfume had leaked to the public before its official presentation, but now we have an opportunity to try it in stores. Tensions have been so high and discussions after its launch on the 16th of November were so passionate, that it has become the number 1 news in my social media space.
 
 

 CdG Blackpepper was created by Antoine Maisondieu of Givaudan. The composition is built around a black pepper accord in a very minimal manner that allows us to observe it from many angles. Officially there are only a few main components declared. Let's dwell upon each of them.
 
1. Black pepper, the hero of the occasion. The first thing you smell is an aroma of freshly ground black pepper, reconstructed with great accuracy. Natural materials rarely smell the same as the plant from which they were sourced (compare narcissus flower with its absolute), and black pepper essential oil is not an exception. The natural oil in Blackpepper is of a remarkable quality (I am almost sure that it has overrun the record from 1998), it was smartly photo-shopped to be supernaturally perfect.
 
2. Cedar. Most of the black pepper essential oil components are extremely volatile. In order to make a full bodied black pepper aroma, you have to "extend" it by something weighty. In this case, the perfumer chose to do it with help of various woods. Coming in second in importance, the note of cedar, dry, coniferous and woody, peeks out soon enough, and supports the savory theme of black pepper recalling cedar nuts, cutting board and juniper wood trivets. Cedar creates the very traditional CdG style.
 
3. Akigalawood®, a unique product of the XXI century invented by Givaudan, a triumph of modern technology. It is obtained from a fraction of patchouli essential oil by processing it with copper-containing oxidase enzymes Laccases. As a result we get a very transparent perfume material, lacking any earthy or camphor nuances characteristic for patchouli. Akigalawood corresponds to patchouli roughly the same way as Iso E Super corresponds to cedar. Akigalawood has spicy nuances which perfectly tie in with the peppery theme of the fragrance, and also produces some oud-like effects.
 
4. Tonka beans. The sweet, milky almond scent of tonka beans serves here a very functional purpose. It binds all parts of the composition together and smooths out the roughness of some of its perky notes. Tonka beans dress the whole thing in a fougere style.
 
5. Musk is a necessary component in almost all modern perfumes. The chemistry of musk odorants is very wide and diverse (there is at least five fundamentally different classes of compounds possessing a musk aroma). In Blackpepper, the musk in the base does not pull up the blanket over it all, its amount is exactly right to wear the fragrance comfortably. To my opinion, the musk accord of Blackpepper has a certain characteristic of nitro musks, compounds used in perfumery in the first half of the 20th century, which add a certain vintage feeling to Blackpepper.
 
6. The sixth component is, as our Eugeniya said, a secret thing. Producers do not rush to disclose all materials, and we have been left to guess and speculate what it might be. Givaudan has a captive material with a black pepper smell, of which they are very proud and very secretive. This is my guess.
 
 
Good luck in your own Blackpepper adventure!
 
By Mat Yudov
