 From the European perfume shows of recent years we learned that green perfume notes have come into fashion and are still gaining popularity. The group of green fragrances is extensive and diverse. At first they were modest and classic, elegantly reticent with such veteran ingredients as galbanum, fig, hyacinth and vetiver, but little by little green scents have started to offer new surprising ways of expressing green freshness of all shades, bringing in new green ingredients as well. This young perfume family shows an intensive and interesting development.
 
 
One of the relatively new and quite exotic notes within the green group is rhubarb, which has become increasingly popular among perfumers, something you've most probably already noticed. Rhubarb's scent is attractive and diffusive; its ability to create surprising contrasting combinations is amazing. Besides that, rhubarb itself offers a number of nuances, revealing its watery, sour, sweet, aromatic, or fruity aspects. Whichever is highlighted, rhubarb always adds a new, fresh, and bright detail into the composition.
 
We have smelled rhubarb in different perfume environments, starting from transparent watery ones all the way to syrupy orientals and gourmand perfumes. The rhubarb accord is one of the main trends in green fragrances and in general in perfumery, one of the new discoveries, although not as mighty as oud has been. We would like to offer you some remarkable rhubarb fragrances to illustrate this bright green perfume division.
 
Valery Mikhalitcyn,
 Fragrantica.ru editor and columnist,
 Founder & perfumer of Mercurio Perfumes
 
Aedes de Venustas — Signature
 
 
My love towards rhubarb started with Signature, the first fragrance by Aedes de Venustas. It was at that same time, and most probably this particular perfume was one of the causes, that I got attracted to and started following Bertrand Duchaufour's works.
 
The rhubarb in Signature has many facets, it has qualities of marijuana, sweet green apple, transparent wood and even lactonic, crispy hazelnut! It is so fresh and possesses certain powers of attraction that we usually attribute to animalic fragrances. It is innocent and transparent, and yet controversial, because of its wet marijuana-green switch. Signature is the perfect morning of the hedonist, who gets out of bed to refresh himself with a small breakfast and a glass of champagne. Rhubarb is the central trick of the composition; it puts accents and creates exotic contrasts, which are so typical for Bertrand Duchaufour. Signature is my number one in the list of rhubarb perfumes.
 
Mercurio Perfumes — Eau de Nonchalance
 
 
Signature made such an impact on my imagination as a perfumer (it may be still my brightest perfume experience), that I dedicated the very first perfume of my own perfume house Mercurio to rhubarb. Creating Eau de Nonchalance, I did not intend to copy the fragrance I liked so much, and so they are not alike. I used rhubarb as the core of the whole structure, and arranged it with tarragon, gardenia, sandal and sweet ripe pear. Bright yellow pear lively opposes the gusty rhubarb, and blends with the delicate gardenia, astringent mate and salty sandal.
 
Eau de Nonchalance is rhubarb from the beginning to the end, braiding with other accords and yielding to their will, but still remaining bright and important. I guess it was not very modest of me to feature my own fragrance as an example of the trend, but I have a small excuse: I created it with Bertrand, or to be more precise, we reformulated the old version of it together from the ground. The final touches are mine, but Bertrand intensely participated in creating its formula.
 
Comme des Garçons — Series 5 Sherbet: Rhubarb
 
This tropical rhubarb–based cocktail was too advanced for its time and has passed by unnoticed by a wide audience. If only it was released a couple of years later than 2003, it might have been a big success.
 
Series 5 Sherbet: Rhubarbis an important perfume for the rhubarb trend. Its dazzling fruity, sour and sweet rhubarb is placed against soft vanilla and lucid flowers (hedion with woody-spicy sparkles). Although simply constructed, it boasts an excellent balance of textures and gustatory sensations, and can easily release the twisting embrace of the summer heat.
 
Series 5 Sherbet: Rhubarb has nothing in common with Signature Aedes de Venustas; its rhubarb accord is surrounded by different conditions and has totally different perfume connections, so these two fragrances could not replace each other. Besides, the perfume by Aedes de Venustas is noticeably more complex.
 
Nomenclature — shi_sõ
 
 
This new fragrance was released in March 2017, and we all loved it from the first sniff. The rhubarb in shi_sõ Nomenclature by Bertrand Duchaufour does not play the main role, nevertheless it is a substantial part of the shiso leaf accord. It participates in its flavorous minty, lactonic and rough green perfume profile.
 
The scent is cool and fresh, slightly watery, but sweet and bright due to the rhubarb, which blends, joined by other elements, with an imposing musk accord responsible for the velvety texture of the fragrance and its airy inside. Rhubarb creates a light, natural, sour effect which it shares with shiso. Its delicacy thins the musky lactonic part of the fragrance, adding a 3D volume to shi_sõ.
 

 Herve Gambs Paris — La Baie des Anges
 
 
It would not be an overstatement, if I call La Baie des Anges the most beautiful among the gourmand floral fragrances with rhubarb that I know. Its rhubarb accord is full and crisp, it demonstrates here some qualities of grapefruit and red berries, which are in general commonly attributed to rhubarb. Although important, it's only a part of the fragrance. The second part is spectacularly floral, based on jasmine grandiflorum. Sadly, it is not always this beautiful in perfumes. The sweet and sour nuances of jasmine perfectly blend with the ones of rhubarb, which is also bitterish juicy, reminiscent of grapefruit. Together they produce a brilliant gleam over the gummy vanilic base of the fragrance.
 
This sweet, smoky, resinous, intense floral, and, on top of it all, juicy rhubarb perfume offers an unforgettable experience. I do count on adding this fragrance to my own fragrance wardrobe some time in the future.
 
 
 
Mat Yudov,
 Fragrantica editor and columnist,
 chemist & perfumer
 

 Mugler — B*Men
 
 
Christine Nagel and Jacques Huclier made this rhubarb perfume way ahead of its time. For the sake of accuracy I wish to mention that there are no natural rhubarb ingredients, but their synthetic analogues have been in use for a long time. You can find them in Nina Ricc L'Air du Temps, Carven Ma Griffe, Miss Dior, and many other classic fragrances from the middle of the 20st century.
 
This fragrance, one of the best flankers of A*Men to my opinion, was poorly accepted by the public in 2004, which did not do justice to its glorious potential. Its central spicy licorice and roasted coffee accord in a violet and vetiver context (men would appreciate it later in Dior Homme and Hermes Vetiver Tonka), along with tart rhubarb – it was all just too strange for that moment in time. If they were to release it today, my guess is it might be much more loved.
 

 La Folie a Plusieurs — Enter the Void
 
 
It is hard to tell how would I feel about this fragrance, if I did not know the whole story behind it, and did not watch the movie of the same name. But with everything put together, I declare this perfume has lived up to its idea! It's a screechy sour rhubarb over dirty musky costus, surrounded by purposely synthetic paint and varnish, leaving you in a state of confusion. Of course, it would not have been Mark Buxton, if he did not put in here a fistfull of poisonous greens, metal and aldehydes. A sudden immortelle brings the situation to a complete absurdity. So if you like rhubarb, and appreciate really crazy perfumes, you need this.
 

 L`Artisan Parfumeur — Mon Numero 9
 
 
Bertrand Duchaufour, for a contrast with the previous deconstructive fragrance, created a rhubarb scent for a sporty person without bad habits. In Numéro Nine, uplifting green lemon and realisticly crispy, juicy rhubarb stems are accentuated by a specific fatty aldehydic shiso note, counterposing the balsamic frankincense and myrrh.
 
Moderately sweet, moderately sour and green, lactonic and musky, the fragrance serves its purpose to be clean and inspirative. Imagine an athletic freshly groomed man rosily flirting with some ladies on a bench in the park, while playing with his golden retriever. Nothing too serious, but it is a deceiving simplicity meticulously made.
 
 
 
Jean Paul Gaultier — Kokorico by Night
 
 
The original Kokorico, created by Olivier Cresp and Annick Menardo in 2011, was a freakish gourmand fougere with fig, cacao and patchouli. Unlike it, Kokorico by Night that was released one year later bursts with bright cool citrus and contrasting rhubarb. It is a good example of rhubarb in commercial perfumery.
 
 
 
Sergey Borisov
 Fragrantica columnist
 
Humiecki & Graef — Eau Radieuse
 
 
Rhubarb to me is an agitated green note, super sour, sticking out from any otherwise calm surroundings; it wakes you up instantly! Therefore I prefer to use perfumes where this note is not as visible, but close to vetiver, green apple, rose, magnolia, or mint. Just like in Eau Radieuse; I do not like too much excitement.
 

 One of Those— [80Hg] Mercury 
 
 
Antoine Lie made another round to the idea of Secretion Magnifique Etat Libre d`Orange, working on a mercury fragrance for the Italian niche brand One Of Those (former nu_be). Rhubarb has a dual role here. From one side, it adds energy and life to this drop of "liquid silver", but from the other, it also makes it green and natural. It never gets to the point of being edible though, but feels quite harmless, especially in the end it's quite safe.
 

 Mark Buxton— Devil in Disguise 
 
 
Mark Buxton has hated rhubarb ever since his childhood, because he was fed this vegetable for vitamins and benefitial nutrients, as he said, but it did not stop him to use this note quite exessively in his creations, especially in his green chypre fragrances, to which it adds a piercing greenness in their animalic trail (Roboris Cale Fragranze, A Rebours Friendly Fur, Emotional Rescue Mark Buxton).
 
Devil in Disguise is just the same: sharp and feminine, beautiful and dangerous, a tender magnolia tree growing in a hemp field. Mark defined it as the smell of approaching danger.
