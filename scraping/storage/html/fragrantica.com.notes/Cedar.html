Group: WOODS AND MOSSES

Cedar definitely deserves the title of one of the first natural odorants used by man for aromatization and perfume creation. In ancient Egyptian mythology (from about 6 thousand years ago) cedar was a symbol of Heaven, Earth, and the Underworld, and its smell was closely associated with everything holy and godlike (Lebanese cedar was the cedar of choice at that time, to be more precise, but we will discuss it later). That led to the emergence of some specific procedural details of ancient rituals: for example, Osiris‘ priests used to chew cedar sap in the practice of worship to forge a better connection with the deity.
 
 
From the botanical point of view, Cedar (lat. Cedrus) is a genus of the Coniferous family (lat. Pinaceae) that consists of four species: Lebanese cedar (lat. Cedrus libani), Cyprus cedar (a subspecies of Lebanese cedar, namely lat. Cedrus libani var. brevifolia), Atlas cedar (lat. Cedrus atlantica), and Himalayan cedar (lat. Cedrus deodara).
 
 
 
Lebanese flag
 

 There are many pines and other coniferous trees of a high perfume value, but today we will focus on what is called a cedar note and how drastically different it can be. By the way, not only natural, true cedars can be perceived as such - there is a huge number of so-called false cedars, that are widely used in perfumery as well.
 
First of all, let's take a look at the above-mentioned Lebanese cedar (lat. Cedrus libani). It is a symbol of Lebanon: you can see it on the flag, and it symbolizes the life eternal thanks to its naturally high resilience to decay. In ancient times it helped make the Phoenicians the “first sea trading nation in the world”: wood of the conifers was known as soft and light enough to facilitate the timber harvesting and withstand water later on, and extensive cedar forests thrived across Mount Lebanon at that time.
 
 
 
Cedrus libani, or Lebanese cedar
 
 
 
Lebanese cedar is one of the most important Christian symbols and appears frequently in the Bible. It was said to be used as a building material for the Temple in Jerusalem (2 Chronicles 2:3,7; 1 Kings 5:20) and to be plentiful as the sycamore-fig in the foothills (1 Kings 10:27, NIV, excerpt). It is also stately and noble as a righteous, vigorous human being, fair and beautiful like the one who has nothing to hide in the face of God (Psalm 92:12). Lebanese cedar essential oil is one of the oldest fragrant materials: it was used to cleanse the participants of religious offerings from impurity together with hyssop (Numbers 19:6).
 
The fragrant oil possesses the yellowish colour and distinctive herbaceous-woody scent, but it is not produced anymore. Steffen Arctander mentioned in his famous book, that "there are today only a few - if any - perfumers who have seen and smelled a true Lebanon cedarwood oil" (p. 109), which is still an honorable and venerated material of distinguishable character nonetheless. Most frequently, Lebanese cedar essential oil is substituted by an essential oil of a different, but related or visually similar plant.
 
 
One of these plants is the Atlas cedar (lat. Cedrus atlantica), which is a cedar native to the Atlas Mountains of Morocco. Essential oil of Atlas cedar is one of the most important cedar materials. It is a viscous and slightly nebulous fluid, the colour of which varies from yellowish tints to the dark cinnamon-orange. Unadulterated essential oil can possess a multifaceted, but slightly appalling scent: medically camphorous aspects together with sweet honey-mimosa undertones are adorned with unmistakable urinal nuances, not pleasing for most of us. The good news is that aromatic compounds (like resinoid and absolute) extracted from the raw material smell almost like natural cedarwood and are deplumed of all the undesirable tinctures.
 
 
 
Cedrus аtlantica, or the Atlas cedar
 
 
 
The scent of Atlas cedar oil varies significantly, mostly because of the dissimilarity of stock batches (not only wood pulp, but also stumps, shavings, and sawdust are used) and the distillation methods (which is sometimes preceded by the so-called chemical pulping, or alkali maceration). Atlas cedar oil is widely used in perfumery as a fixative and a base component - together with labdanum it merges into the base chord of great tenacity, suitable for woody and flowery fragrances. Besides all that, Atlas cedar oil is relatively inexpensive and is almost never falsified.
 
 
 
Cedrus deodarа, or the deodar cedar, or Himalayan cedar, or deodar/devdar/devadar/devadaru
 

 The closest relative of Atlas cedar is Himalayan cedar, or deodar (lat. Cedrus deodara) — a gigantic tree with height of up to 50 meters and trunk diameter of up to 3 meters. The Himalayan cedar lumber is usually not good enough, that is why most of the harvested timbers are used for essential oil extraction (up to 2.5% yield from the mass of the dry matter) with overall production of up to 200 tons per annum. Deodaroil is thick and viscous, with colour ranging from yellow to cinnamon brown and with a characteristic woody-sweet, almost balsamic scent. It starts with the same camphorous cresol-medicinal nuances that one has to wait through, so that it could reach the tarry base with a slightly needle-foliage overtone.
 
 
Ingredients responsible for true cedars' smell are the derivatives of sesquiterpene called bisabolene, terpenes of himachalene family - isomers that differ in location of the double bond. Such essential oil contains 30-50% of β-, 15-20% of α-, and approximately 10% of γ-himachalene.
 
 
 
 
 
But that is not the entire story. You may know that a Virginia cedarwood oil exists. Common names of the plant in question include eastern red cedar, but botanically speaking it is not a cedar at all - it is a juniper, one of the taxonomically extensive Cypress family (lat. Cupressaceae). Its official name is Virginia juniper (or lat. Juniperus virginiana), but it is perceived as the one and the only cedar by many perfumers around the world (maybe you remember the windy path of aldehydes and mimosa?). It comes from Northern America, but since the 17th century it is effectually cultivated in Europe with a plethora of decorative hybrid genus existing.
 
 
 
Majestic wide-branching “Virginia Cedar“ (or Virginia juniper, one of the false cedars) in Moscow Apothecary Garden
 
 
 
Wood material of Virginia juniper is incredibly rot resistant, but it is used mostly for making of pencils (hence its other name, pencil cedar). Stumps, shavings, and needle foliage are used for essential oil production by means of steam distillation (with the process requiring up to 10-12 hours and 3,5% of yield).
 
 
 
Juniperus virginiana, or Virginia juniper
 
 
 
The amount of production reaches 200-300 tons of "cedarwood oil" per annum; oleaginous fluid can undergo subsequent distillation to turn into a clear aromatic substance. It is not as viscous like Atlas or Himalayan cedar oil: it is quite flexible and possesses a pleasant and mild, woody, buttery, and slightly balsamic scent. It grows drier with time, gives up some sweetness, and becomes distinctively woody.
 
 
Essential ingredients of Virginia cedar oil stem from the tryciclic sesquiterpene family: both α-cedrene and thujopsene comprise around 25% each (raw oil can contain up to 60% of α-cedrene), and β-cedrene comprises around 15% of the whole amount of ethereal oil. Eventually cedrene oxidizes to cedrol, sesquiterpene alcohol, also called "cedar camphor": it is regarded as the source of characteristic "pencil shavings" smell of cedar compounds. Cedrol and its derivatives will be addressed later.
 
 

 Virginia cedar oil can be found in woody fragrances for men: for example, classic Givenchy Gentleman together with Roberto Cavalli Black by Olivier Cresp contain around 10% of this essential oil. Not significantly less of it was added to Paco Rabanne Black XS; the fresh and invigorating DKNY Men and Versace Man Eau Fraiche; the sweet licorice-rhubarb Mugler B*Men (I'm sure it would have been extremely popular nowadays were it still produced); and the mournful and sepulchral Serge Lutens Iris Silver Mist.
 
 
 
Juniperus ashei, or mountain cedar
 
 
 
The close relative of Virginia cedar is the so-called Texas cedar, which also is not cedar: it happens to be blueberry, or Ashe juniper (lat. Juniperus mexicana or Juniperus ashei). Essential oil of Ashe cedar had not been produced until a couple decades back, when the production amounts rocketed to more than 100 tons per annum and hasn’t stopped increasing since. Texas cedar oil is a dark-orange, slightly nebulous liquid, with cedrol crystals settling out quite often (it is sometimes the case, when the surrounding temperature decreases - the oil can even harden completely). It possesses a pleasant, woody-sweet, slightly fumous and smoky scent, with tarry and cade nuances, and a characteristic pencil shavings undertone. In due course it becomes sweeter and more balsamic, and this is where the difference between Virginian cedar oil and Texas cedar oil is most obvious. Nowadays Texas cedar carries the garland of top performer on the market: around 1.5 thousand tons of its essential oil is produced per annum, while Virginia cedarwood oil drags behind significantly.
 
 

 For a long time Texas cedar oil could be found in woody fragrances for men (please pay attention to Сartier Declaration, Acqua di Parma Cipresso di Toscana, Ralph Lauren Polo Explorer, Diptyque Tam Dao, and Nasomatto Black Afgano). Legend has it that Serge Lutens traveled across Morocco and visited a small shop of an ebeniste, or a cabinet-maker, who sold wood articles at the back of a Marrakesh souq. There he found a sliver of fragrant cedar wood, with a scent of which he was astounded. He decided to make a fragrance on the base of this magnificent note no matter what it took; he remembered this idea in 1990, when he was first hired by Shiseido.
 
 
And that is how Feminite du Bois was born, created together with Pierre Bourdon and Christopher Sheldrake: built around the most certain Texas cedar oil overdose (8%), it quickly gained public appraisal and jump-started a new comprehensive trend. For example, approximately 6% of Texas cedar oil can be found in Lancôme Magnifique and a whole 9% is awaiting you in Mugler Womanity.
 
 
 
Cupressus funebris, or Chinese weeping cypress
 
 
 
Essential oil of the Chinese cedar is extracted from the weeping cypress (lat. Cupressus funebris) that occurs in the Chinese provinces of Guizhou, Hunan, Chongqing, and some other southern territories of China. It is an evergreen tree of the noble Cypress family and quite a popular odorant with up to 240 tons of essential oil produced per annum. It can be observed in Sonia Rykiel Woman.
 
 
 
Juniperus procera, or African juniper, or East-African cedar
 
 
 
Besides Virginia and Texas false cedars of the Juniper family, essential oil can be extracted from the East-African Cedar (or African juniper, lat. Juniperus procera). 50-100 tons of essential oil are produced in Kenya per annum. It is not everything that one can tell about juniper‘s presence in contemporary perfumery, but essential oils extracted from the berries of prickly juniper (or sharp cedar, or cade juniper, lat. Juniperus oxycedrus) and cade oil, extracted with the help of dry distillation process, are a separate case.
 
 
 
Thuja occidentalis, or Northern white cedar, or arborvitae
 
 
 
There is also an essential oil of cedar needles, that is extracted from the northern white cedar (or thuja, lat. Thuja occidentalis), an evergreen plant of the Cypress family of the Thuja genus. Sometimes a codistillation is performed, when an essential oil of northern white cedar and needles of Virginia juniper is needed.
 
 
 
Thuja plicata, or giant cedar, or shinglewood
 
 
 
One more fragrantly significant thuja is the western red cedar (or giant arborvitae, lat. Thuja plicata), which is good for the extraction of two types of essential oils, of needles and wood.
 
 
 
Chamaecyparis obtusa, also known as Japanese,or hinoki cypress
 
 
 
Lest we forget about essential oils of the Japanese cedars that also belong to the Cypress family. Three exotic essential oils are usually introduced here: hiba oil, extracted from asunaro (or false arborvitae - lat. Thujopsis dolobrata var. dolabrata and Thujopsis dolabrata var. hondai are endemic to Japan); hinoki oil, extracted from needles and roots of hinoki cypress (or the Japan cypress, lat. Chamaecyparis obtusa); and sugi oil, extracted from Japanese cryptomeria (or Japanese sugi pine, or Japanese red cedar, lat. Cryptomeria japonica, formerly belonging to the family Taxodiaceae, Taxodium).
 
 
 
Cupressus nootkatensis, or Alaska cypress, or yellow cedar
 
 
 
Besides from all the above-mentioned species, an essential oil of Nootka cedar existsas well (or Nootka cypress, or yellow cedar, or Alaska cedar, lat. Cupressus nootkatensis) - which turns out to be a cypress again. First of all, it‘s a widely spread decorative plant, and secondly it is a not-so-popular flavor compound - please refer to Penhaligon’s Blasted Heath by Bertrand Duchaufour and to Mizensir Perfect Oud by Alberto Morillas.
 
 
 
Pinus mugo, or creeping pine, or dwarf mountainpine
 
 
 
There is also an essential oil of Swiss mountain pine (or creeping pine, lat. Pinus mugo), which belongs to the Pinus, or Pine, family, but this we can most certainly discuss on another occasion.
 
 
 
 
 
Contemporary perfumery needs cedar materials a lot: these compounds are the most important on par with sandalwood, vetiver, and patchouli. The chord of cedar is emulated not only with the help of natural oils, but through the agency of synthetic components, too. Here we can observe "natural" miraculously merging into "synthetic": "synthetic" cedar compounds derive from the modification of isolates, or entities extracted from the natural raw stock - mostly the key components of juniper essential oils, like α-cedrene and thujopsene.
 
 
One of the most important "half-synthetic" products is the substance acquired after the acylation of terpene fraction in Texas cedar essential oil (the process looks the same whe one produces vetiveryl acetate using vetiver essential oil). This compound first appeared in the early '60s under the trade name methyl cedryl ketone (MCK),or acetyl cedrene (supplied like Vertofix Coeur IFF, Lignofix Symrise, and Cedratone V Givaudan among others): it possesses an intense cedar smell with a deep ambery tone, supported by vetiver, leathery, and musk nuances. It is generally thought, that the entity, characteristic for the MCK scent (the so-called G isomer), comprises approximately 5-10% of the composition and is described as the product of transformation of thujopsene. Perfumers quickly took to the idea of МСК, making it one of the most popular woody fragrant materials by the '70s. The use of MCK is heavily present in classic scents like YSL Opium, Paloma Picasso, and Chopard Casmir, with it being the key component of the composition in Chanel №19 (12%) and Estee Lauder White Linen (20%).
 
 
Dry and sweet α-cedrol is produced with industrial efficiency - it is a pungent and vivid cedryl acetate enriched with powdery nuances. Cedrol methyl ether is even more important, widely known as cedramber (cedryl methyl ether) and extremely popular due to its dry and somewhat dusty ambergris note, and, of course, due to its glimmering essential cedar tone. One more popular product of cedrene modification is ambrocenide - a powerful ambery-woody odorant with agarwood aspects.
 
There are completely synthetic iterations of cedar, like Palisandin and Amberwood F invented by Symrise (both of them are alkoxy-substituted cyclododecanes), but I shall only note them for further investigation. Such is the case of a small square pictogram with a conifer cone in a fragrance pyramid.
 
 
 
Well then, lets sum up:
 
 
 
  • yes, cedar essential oils exist (extracted from botanically true cedars, belonging to the Cedrus family);
      
    •  
  • "cedarwood essential oil" is quite often a Texas or Virginia cedar essential oil, meaning it's a juniper essential oil;
      
    •  
  • there is a huge amount of various exotic essential oils, that are extracted from wood materials of various coniferous plants, only visually resembling cedars (but it is called "cedarwood essential oil" all the same);
      
    •  
  • "synthetic" cedar materials are most certainly isolates, produced from the Texas cedar essential oil, or their direct derivatives. Such materials have been used in perfumery for more than 50 years.
