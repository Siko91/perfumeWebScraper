 Jil Sander Simply Jil Sander Touch of Mandarin

Violets are my favorite flower. I have been fascinated by them since childhood. But after many years of experience, I have started to question this fascination. What exactly do I like? Is it the natural odor of a flower bouquet from my garden? Is it the conceptual or the ideal odor of the violet? Is it the profound visual impact of the flower itself? 
 
Perfumers tend to make abstract versions of their real life experiences. This is why violets, as you can smell them in nature, are not exactly the same as violets in perfumes. These flowers have a very short blooming period. The odor changes from the moment you pick them to up to ten hours later. They also change every year in terms of nuances. Both leaves and flowers contribute to the odor perceived in a garden. When flowers are not in bloom in my garden, the odor of leaves are present many months later – nonadienal and nonadienol mainly – a specific and highly diffusive cucumber note, not really related to “violet leaves absolute.” This odor contributes to the nostalgic odor of late fall when most flowers are gone.
 
 
Among the five “fundamental flowers,” violet and lily-of-the-valley share a unique position. The natural reference shows little “variation,” unlike the multiple nuances of a rose. In fact, we speak about two highly characteristic plants without many botanical versions and without many “echoes” among other botanical families in the northern hemisphere. Most perfume interpretations of these flowers are artistic “definitions” imagined by talented perfumers. They show the transition from “natural odor and reference” to “concept.” This concept is often highly realistic, but it is nothing more than the fruit of imagination. It takes a lot of talent and work to create a flower that smells natural despite the obvious differences when it is compared to the source. This is because in perfumery, the power of imagination and its ability to generate a new “reality” is far greater than the power of memory. In fact, it is nearly impossible to compare violet and lily-of-the-valley to many perfumes based on these themes.
 
 
Bouquet of violets, Edouard Manet (1872)
 
 
 
 
 
These flowers have a very short blooming period. “Perceived realism” is not based on true scent comparison, it is based on our ability to conceptualize a scent. The odor becomes abstract in our memory and this “mental image” is not static. This idea is very important in floral compositions because you do not need, as a perfumer, to pick up all of the molecules shown in the headspace analysis. You do not mimic an odor; you evoke its presence. We memorize a scent via analogies and differences with previous experiences; the “memory” is never accurate, and it is rather vague as the mental image changes with new knowledge. This is why there is always a difference between the “portrait of a flower” under your nose and the recreation of the same flower off season in order to generate a similar “scent realism.”
 
 
Bouquet of Violets in a Vase, Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec (1882)
 
 
 
 
 
Many violet perfumes from the past are “pure concepts.” They are based on the highly characteristic ionone and methyl ionone family with accents from the acetylenic family (Neo Folione and company). But this is nothing more than an exaggerated sketch. When you analyze the natural odor, you will detect multiple nuances, like those related to flowers like cassis, narcissus, or cyclamen.
 
Creating a violet perfume is both very easy and extremely complicated. Most characteristic elements are available since the late 19th century, and their combinations generate an obvious, instantly recognizable profile. But this is not the real violet flower, if you are familiar with a violet bouquet.
 
For many years I have perfected my violet bases. It is much easier to recognize the ionone accord than a true violet accord. A violet flower is extremely easy to simulate, but it is more complicated to reproduce all nuances, its subtlety, the freshness, and the naturalness of a bouquet. It is also much more difficult to formulate a product. This is because ionones and other high impact elements – natural or synthetic – have many variables and their adjustments from one batch to another is not easy. I will not discuss the differences between the many “ionones” you can find on the market.
 
 
Violet Bouquet, Albrecht Durer (c.1502)
 
 
 
 
 
Natural violet flower extracts were almost extinct after WWI. In 1935 Ernest Guenther, then chief research chemist at Fritzsche Brothers NY, wrote that the cultivation of violets had practically disappeared in France. Violet Parma and Violet Victoria (Luxonne) were cultivated in the south of France, mainly Grasse, Hyeres, and Toulouse (cut flowers). Violet flower oil was used in several perfumes from the late 19th century from Pinaud, Delettrez, and Millot. Later, a very small amount was mixed with new ionones in several specialties to give them naturalness.
 
In 1938, Charabot was still making violet flower oil, from both Parma and Victoria flowers, with an astronomical price: eight times the price of regular jasmine absolute. This product was obtained via the fractionation of the Parma violet flower absolute and was the equivalent of contemporary molecular distillation. This is why many vintage perfumes from the 1930s cannot be made today without access to such haute couture natural extracts typical for the era.
 
April Violets (Yardley) was a long time ago and a very good interpretation (perhaps too much accent on the green aspect), but when it was reformulated, perhaps a decade ago, many things went wrong. The EDT, the deodorant, the soap, and powder...after reformulation, everything went entirely wrong, and the lack of balance revealed the “bad aspects” of a classic theme. Vintage versions show the evolution from class to mass.
 
 
Because we are attracted to contrasts, and perhaps the overdose, the violet type as it is known via perfumes (Guerlain, Caron, Berdoues, Penhaligon’s) is far more popular than the natural odor. However, the natural reproduction of the flower represents a major theme in classical perfumery. This odor – subtle, but highly characteristic if you are familiar with the flower – is a theme in several classic perfumes from Molyneux or Balenciaga, from between the 1930s and 1960s.
 
Violet, with any other element from the five fundamental flower themes, is able to generate a very long list of accords. Also, this flower is able to generate even more themes with quite unrelated families, going deeper into the woody and ambery fragrances. I have in mind two exceptional perfumes from the late 1930s, one with a tobacco base, the other with an animalic base. The role of violet in the “leather theme” is quite obvious since many classic complex leather scents from 1930s to 1960s contain the leather isomer of methyl ionone made by Givaudan. It is the case of Jolie Madame and Miss Balmain.
 
 
The violet odor – or more precisely, a very balanced violet base – is very important in the recreation of realistic scents like narcissus, rose, or even jasmine, from 10% down to 0.1% of the formula. The role of the violet in a narcissus theme, a rose theme, or even a jasmine composition is part of classical training. This gradual importance can be experienced in many couturier perfumes made by Roure since the 1950s. In this case, the “violet” is rarely obvious, it is “hidden.” Violet is also important in other themes like “fleur de vigne” (grapeflower), “reseda” / mignonette, “champagne,” camellia, pansy, mimosa, jacée (an old prototype for Iris gris), paulownia, freesia, and fuchsia, but also such woody variations as “Bois de Violette” (an old Robertet base used by Chanel) or Jacaranda.
 
 
Wisteria flowers are also an original interpretation of violet
 
 
 
Today, creating a violet perfume is nearly impossible without knowing the bases created by Givaudan, Firmenich, De Laire, and H&R (now Symrise). This is because most combinations of very well-known materials were created decades ago. Obviously, new materials introduced by Bedoukian enriched this theme. It is also the case of the neo Violettine (Firmenich) family. You cannot reinvent the wheel. Again, there is a major difference between natural odor and what the consumer perceives as “violet.” In this case, “candied violets” (and all similar confectionery products), make-up products, and body products with a violet fragrance shaped the contemporary interpretation, quite different from the natural flower.
 
In fine fragrances, there are several directions and very different interpretations of the flower used in complex contexts. These perfumes are creations of Guerlain, Caron, and Roure; the list is impressive. They show how a delicate flower with a unique odor can be artistically interpreted by perfumers for very different results.
 
Creating a “violet” base starts with the study of the ionone family. Ionones from various suppliers are not the same – not in terms of scent and not in terms of isomers. Among famous violet specialties I mention Violettal 2905 and 129 from Firmenich; Irrozol, Violette Blanche, No 1200, No 3505, and No 9463 from Givaudan; Violette Blanche, Violette Bleue, Violette 35, Paulownia, Violette N, and Ionarol from De Laire; and Violette Invar from Roure. There are also several violet bases from H&R and Dragoco which are very useful and available in the Symrise archive.
 
 
The use of "conceptual" violet bases is typical for the perfumes created before the 1940s. In the 1950s, new bases created around the flower and not around accords of ionone alfa or beta made their debut. In this case, the violet odor is much subtler and extremely well integrated into a complex bouquet. The odor cannot be easily identified unless you are familiar with the flower. It is not the “hardcore” ionone violet from the 1910s (Roger & Gallet or Guerlain), and it is not the straightforward Parmantheme (Firmenich, based on Ruzicka research). This new “violet” created by Firmenich, H&R and Dragoco is a “true to nature” scent based on the study of isomers found in the flower and quite different from commercial raw materials with molecules from the dihydro beta ionone family and C9 natural elements plus those molecules responsible for the “airy scent” of the bouquet.
 
 
A natural violet bouquet has an unmistakable “ozonic” or “marine” aspect, as in the original Fahrenheit (Dior). For this reason, a subtle violet theme is always present in creations with a watery or airy theme. Modern violets created after the 1950s blend extremely well with most floral notes. Perhaps this is the reason why the violet theme was somehow hidden. The original J’adore (Dior) has a subtle violet nuance. This bouquet was entirely modern when it was launched, but the aesthetic behind it is reminiscent of the 1960s style. Unity and variety, complexity, subtlety, perceived naturalness, precision in detail as in Flemish paintings, return to nature.
 
For a basic violet fragrance, you need ionone a, ionone b, methyl ionone G (iralia), dihydrobeta ionone, orris concrete / abs, nonadienol and / or nonadienal, violet leaf absolute, cassis absolute, neofolione or violettine family, costus (replacement), and a teinture ambre gris (natural or reproduction), plus ginger or galangal or laurel leaf extracts. It is important to detect all nuances of the flower extremely well before adding nuances that are not typical. For instance, natural flowers evoke for me a subtle nuance of Musk Tonkin, but not the musk theme found in many contemporary perfumes.
 
The green and sharp aspect found in old perfumes from Roger & Gallet, Pinaud, and Guerlain was simulated with another rare natural – reseda flower infusion. After 1910 this was replaced with Reseda base (Mignonette). Strong and complex violet themes are found in Violette Precieuse and N’aimez que moi from Caron.
 
 
Classic perfumes from Roger & Gallet (Vera Violeta and Violette de Parme) are far from accurate reproductions, despite their richness. It is hard to evaluate if this discrepancy is due to specific perception (and the aesthetic vision of the era) or to an inadequate study. During perfume creation, it is very tempting to substitute harmonious accords generated by available RM to the original perception because these flowers have a very short blooming period. Violet themes blend very well with many complex themes, but they also tend to “reorganize” themselves within any given formulation. This is why, when a complex perfume contains too many elements from this family, it is better to group them and study the resulting scent in contrast with all the rest.
 
 
Violet flower themes are important in rose bouquets (Paris YSL), but also in several unexpected themes like the original interpretation of the tobacco-woody theme as seen in Molyneux and Snuff Schiaparelli or even Fahrenheit (Dior), where there is a complex dry floral theme, much more interesting than the woody-ambery aspect.
 
In other cases, a violet-orris theme is used to emphasize a rose / jasmine / lily-of-the-valley theme as seen in many 1960s creations. The typical natural violet flower scent surrounds many classic rose perfumes and serves as a starting point for the delicate powder facet which characterizes make-up products. In a realistic reproduction of the floral scent, the creamy powdery aspect and the “watery” and “dewy” aspects are very important. The “sugary” aspect (bon-bon), should be under control.
 
When analyzing a violet bouquet after 12 hours, 24 hours, and 48 hours, many directions emerge. The true, natural odor fades after several hours. This is a crucial moment for correct analysis of the flower. These flowers do not last very long after they are picked. The scent is elusive and very delicate.
 
 
By Octavian - Sever Coifan
