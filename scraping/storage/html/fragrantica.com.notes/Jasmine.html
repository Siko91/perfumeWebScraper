Of strawberries and of bananas
 
The creamy scent of jasmine speaks.
 
Igor Severyanin
 
 
 
"There are no perfumes without jasmine." That old perfume-related saying is actually quite close to the reality of the fragrance world. About 80% of fragrances created in the first half of the last century contained natural jasmine raw materials. That statement is partly confirmed in an interview with Jacques Polge, who sets jasmine notes apart from the rest, calling them a vital part of any Chanel fragrance:
 
 
 
 
 
Jasmine (Lat. Jasminum, from Persian یاسمین‎, which means "a gift from God") is a genus of evergreen shrubs from the olive family (Lat. Oleaceae) comprising about 200 species; jasmine's close relatives are lilacs, osmanthus, the olive tree, and the ash tree.
 
 
Jasminum officinale
 
 
 
The most important species of jasmine in perfumery is white jasmine, also called common jasmine (Lat. Jasminum officinale). In the wild, the plant can be found in the Himalayas (white jasmine is the national flower of Pakistan), but it is easy to cultivate, which was the reason for its widespread distribution across the Mediterranean in the 17th century.
 
A variety of white jasmine, called royal jasmine (Lat.Jasminum officinale L. var. grandiflorum), also known as Spanish jasmine, Catalan jasmine, and jasmine odorant, is widely cultivated with the aim of obtaining extracts for perfumery. It can be found in France, China, India, Egypt, and other countries.
 
 
 
 
Jasminum officinale L. var. grandiflorum
 
 
 
Eight million hand-picked jasmine flowers (which is about 1000 kg) are required to make 2.3 kilos of concrete, which in turn yields just 1 kilogram of jasmine absolute.
 
It is estimated that 5-6 tons of royal jasmine absolute are produced annually. The price per kilo is about $15,000 for absolutes produced in France. Absolutes produced in Italy, Egypt, and Morocco cost somewhat less.
 
 
The high cost of perfumery jasmine materials and their exceptional demand in the respective industry have pushed chemists to research jasmine's scent composition.
 
The key components of jasmine absolute were already known more than 100 years ago. From the absolute, the Germans Albert Hesse and Friedrich Muller extracted benzyl alcohol (5%) and its esters, mainly benzyl acetate (34%) and benzyl benzoate (24%); linalool (8%), cis-jasmone (3%) and indole (2.5%), comprise about three-fourths of the entire mass of jasmine absolute in total.
 
In the first half of the 20th century, people discovered para-cresol, geraniol, farnesol, nerolidol, and α-terpineol. However, the then-known and aforementioned components did not add up to a full reconstruction of the jasmine aroma: The most important part responsible for its wonderful characteristic "radiance" and its deep, somewhat sweet floral personality remained unknown.
 
In the second half of the 1950's, a team led by Edouard Demole received 5 kilos of Egyptian jasmine concrete for scientific purposes and began to research its minor components in detail. In October 1957, these scientists extracted a substance they named methyl jasmonate (which typically comprises about 0.8% of jasmine absolute). The team needed time to determine the exact structure of the substance, which in turn was verified by synthesis of a simpler, more saturated methyl jasmonate derivative that the scientists called hedione.
 
The discovery of hedione radically changed the situation in the modern fragrance industry. At first perfumers used it only in micro-quantities when making jasmine accords, but today its percentage in fragrances is often a double-digit number. Hedione is one of the most important materials and is quite worthy of a separate, detailed review. Here, let me just note that, contrary to popular belief, hedione can be found in nature as well, where its micro-quantities are present, for instance, in the aroma of tea and the sweet lima orange.
 
 
Methyl jasmonate plays an important role in the life of plants: It is a hormone regulating their growth and development. Methyl jasmonate is formed from linolenic acid and the structure of the compound is rather close to that of prostaglandins, hormone-like, physiologically active substances that play a vital role in forming pain sensation in mammals.
 
Non-steroid, anti-inflammatory medications like aspirin block a specific enzyme by lowering the production of prostaglandins, thus inhibiting the inflammation process and lowering pain.
 
 
Rather recently, the monopoly of royal jasmine was affected by another species of jasmine called jasmine sambac (Lat. Jasminum sambac) or Arabian jasmine. (In reality, it comes from Southern Asia.)
 
An important difference of the absolute extracted from jasmine sambac is the much lower indole content and, on the contrary, a higher content of farnesene, as well as the presence of cis-3-hexenyl acetate (leaf alcohol), which imparts a green note, and methyl anthranylate, the presence of which partially relates sambac to orange blossom and tuberose.
 
 
It is jasmine sambac that is normally used to perfume green tea, and a well-known Chinese folk song, "Mo Li Hua," is devoted to that very variety.
 
Royal jasmine absolute production supersedes that of jasmine sambac in terms of production volume. (The former is produced in India, China, and Morocco.) In Steffen Arctander's book Perfume and Flavor Materials of Natural Origin, written more than five decades ago, it is not mentioned at all, but many large companies (e.g. Firmenich, Mane, Biolandes, and Robertet) offer the product nowadays.
 
In addition to "classic" jasmine absolutes, the modern market also offers its CO2-extracts (SFE), but, alas, jasmine essential oil cannot be extracted.
 
 
 
 
Jasminum sambac
 
 
 
Even before the discovery of methyl jasmonate, perfumers had noticed that adding amyl cinnamic or hexyl cinnamic aldehydes to a jasmine accord significantly improves its quality, making the latter more realistic, too.
 
A modern alternative to these is gelson (IFF), a strong, substantive odorant that is sheer and fresh at the same time. It is somewhere halfway between hexyl cinnamic aldehyde and hedione.
 
 
Among synthetic odorants with a jasmine note we often come across lactones: These are close in structure to the natural jasmolactone and similar ketones, e.g. fleuramone (IFF).
 
Quite recently, a synthetic methyl jasmonate has become available (many agree that it smells nicer and more interesting than hedione): In the year of lavish celebrations in honor of the 50th anniversary since the discovery of hedione, Firmenich made it public under the brand Splendione. There are alternatives to that one, too, such as Jasmoneige and Zeppin from the Japanese company Zeon.
 
Another class of jasmine odorants similar in structure are the derivatives of pyran, e.g. jasmonyl (Givaudan) or jasmelia (IFF). They are somewhat reminiscent of cis-jasmone and possess a jasmine-lactone scent with a celery-mushroom undertone, which allows one to successfully combine them with lavender notes.
 
 
As in many other cases (let us remember cedar and mimosa here), there was a certain degree of confusion with the name of jasmine. In certain territories of Russia, for example, it is a bit too cold for jasmine, but there, philadelphus or mock-orange (Lat. Philadelphus) is often called garden jasmine, eventhough it is actually a plant from the hydrangea family that has nothing in common with jasmine. Most likely mock-orange is called jasmine due to the similarity of both plants' flowers and the pleasant scent that is reminiscent of jasmine yet still decidedly different. In English the plant is commonly called mock-orange, i.e. "fake orange," again, probably due to the flowers' similarity.
 
There is a great number of cultivated varieties of mock-orange, and many of them were bred as early as the mid-19th century by Victor Lemoine. In Russia, the most popular varieties are those bred in the first half of the last century by Nikolay Kuzmich Vekhov, a breeder from the town of Lipetsk. One of the most popular varieties of mock-orange bears his name, "In Vekhov's Memory."
 
In addition to mock-orange, other plants are also called by the name jasmine:
 
 
 
  • Mandevilla or Didadenia (Lat. Mandevilla sanderi)– "Brazilian jasmine"
    •  
  • certain varieties of gardenia – "South African Jasmine"
    •  
  • Gelsemium (Lat. Gelsemium) – "yellow jasmine, evergreen jasmine"
    •  
  • another type of mandevilla, Mandevilla laxa – or "Chilean jasmine"
    •  
  • Stephanotis (Lat. Stephanotis floribunda)– "Madagascar jasmine"
    •  
  • Parsonsia capsularis – "New Zealand Jasmine"
    •  
  • Nocturnal sestrum (Lat. Cestrum nocturnum) or Nyctanthes arbor-tristis– "night-blooming jasmine"
    •  
  • Red plumeria (Lat. Plumeria rubra) – "red jasmine"
    •  
  • Trachelospermum (Lat. Trachelospermum) –  "star jasmine"
    •  
  • Millingtonia (Lat. Millingtonia hortensis) or Indian cork tree – "tree jasmine"
 
 
 

 It would be pointless to list even the most significant compositions featuring a jasmine note here. Therefore I suggest that you simply tell us your personal favorite fragrances inspired by this amazing flower in the comments section below.
