When we decided to make an article about Mimosa in perfumery, the first question I asked myself was "Which mimosa are we talking about?". Sometimes we don't call things by their proper names: for example Philadelphus is called by nearly everybody Jasmine, etc. On the one hand its a perfect occasion for a "know-it-all" attitude, but on the other — it's a habit already, and everyone understands what is meant. But with Mimosa, it's hard!
 
 
So, the plant with the small spherical yellow flowers that grows, among other places, by the Black Sea and is traditionally present for International Women's Day — is the one that is usually called "Mimosa", but actually it's Acacia Dealbata.
 
 

 This plant, originally from Australia, belongs to the Genus Acacia, a subfamily of the Mimosoideae and family of the Fabaceae. Its flowers, as we know, have a very characteristic, nice smell. Using petroleum ether extraction with 0.1-0.75%, you can get a concrete, wax-like material with a specific smell that isn't very similar to the real flowers' smell — with bee-wax notes, violet and sometimes phenolic nuances (that's why sometimes it recalls ylang-ylang). But most often, they use another plant as a source for raw materials with the scent of mimosa.
 
 
When we mention mimosa absolute, it means that we are talking about the material derived from the Acacia Decurrens(black wattle or early green wattle), which is actually not mimosa either. This plant grows mainly in Australia (one of its names is Sidney Black Wattle), and in India, Africa and South America. Initially it was used as a source of tanning materials and as a decorative plant. The flowers (or the entire flowering heads) are a source of concrete; using alcohol extraction of the concrete we get an absolute (20-25%) with a smell that is much closer to real flowers than to concrete. In Europe, mimosa absolute is produced nearly exclusively in the South of France. Once upon a time in the beginning of 1956, a serious frost killed the Acacia Decurrens plantations — after that, mimosa absolute was unavailable for the next two years; the key fragrance bases with the scent of mimosa were created in these years.
 
As to their cost (they are quite expensive), mimosa concretes and absolutes are still rather economical materials: even less than 1% gives a noticeable effect — a quantity of more than 0,5% is rare in perfumes. The absolute works amazingly in lilac, violet or lily-of-the-valley accords and in different coumarin-based complexes or accords, making the composition smoother and turning up its diffuseness. The yearly total amount of produced concrete and absolute (of course depending on the harvest and other factors) is 200-1500 kg of concrete and 100-300 kg of absolute. The main components of concrete are methyl salicylate, ethyl linoleate, benzyl alcohol, phenyl acetate, para-anisaldehyde, chavicol, β-ionone, nonadienal, and benzaldehyde.
 
 

 Another related plant, used for sourcing of its flowers absolute, is Acacia Farnesiana (commonly known as sweet acacia, huisache or needle bush). The plant is cultivated in France, Spain, Egypt, Algeria, India and Morocco. The blooming plant's scent is often described as a violet and raspberry smell. The concrete's conversion is 0.2-0.7%, and the absolute derived from it has a conversion of 35%. This material in French is called absolue de cassie, often translated as "Cassia absolute". It contains methyl salicylate (18.5%), geraniol (11.8%), farnesol (13.5%), benzyl alcohol (20.5%), anisaldehyde, benzaldehyde, α- and β-ionones, coumarin, nerolidol and some other components. Specific for Cassia Absolute are certain materials with 11 carbons: cis-3-methyl-3-decen-1-ol, cis-3-methyl-3-decenoic acid, cis-3-methyl-4-decenoic acid and others.
 
 

 Cassia absolute has a complex, very warm, powdery-spicy smell with clear herbal and flowery aspects and a deep balmy-cinnamon undertone. Cassia absolute goes very well with ionones and violet-iris materials, heliotropine, incense, and ylang-ylang; it's beautiful in various oriental compositions. Most often cassia absolute is used in "flower bouquets" as a light nuance, and it works best in minimal concentrations (of only 0.04-0.08%). But there are cases where you can experience a cassia soliflore, — Une Fleur de Cassie, for example, created by Dominique Ropion for Edition de Parfums Frederic Malle in 2000. The production of Cassia absolute is no more than 100 kg per year.
 
By now, you may wonder: "What about a the REAL mimosa"? So, here it is (that is: the most popular of its species called Mimosa Pudica, but also going by sensitive plant, sleepy plant, Dormilones, touch-me-not, or shy plant).
 
 

 The plant is from the Fabaceae family, though there was a separate family Mimosae before. It grows in Brasil, where it's sometimes used as decorative plant. It has no value for perfumery.
 
To conclude, I'd like to mention two more plants that can cause confusion in novice perfume lovers. Both have absolutely nothing to do with mimosa, but their names can be quite confusing.
 
 

 Besides cassie or cassia absolute there is cassia essential oil, cassia bark oil derived from Cinnamomum cassia bark or (rarely) leaves, a close relative to cinnamon. For some reason Cinnamomum cassia sometimes is called just cassia. The essential oil of Cinnamomum cassia consists of more than 80% cinnamon aldehyde, which smells — exactly! — like cinnamon.
 
Another name that can be puzzling in this area is сassis absolute; сassis bud absolute is an absolute derived from black currant buds. It has the characteristic black currant smell.
 
 

 For mimosa-based fragrant compositions they use para-methyl acetophenone — a material with a sweet cherry-coumarin smell and a close relative of acetophenone. It's usually accompanied by heliotropin or its bases-substitutes, vanillin and benzaldehyde — the material that determines the smell of bitter almond. The flowery aspect is created with the help of different lily-of-the-valley odorants, terpineol brings out the characteristic lilac tone; nearly always the mimosa accord has ionones with a violet smell and rose alcohols. The green complex often features watermelon/violet nonadienal and folion or other materials with a violet leaf aspect. Adding natural components (such as bergamot oil, lemon, ylang-ylang, rose, jasmine and narcissus absolutes) brings depth and roundness to the composition.
 
Among the new materials that can be useful in the creation of a mimosa smell, I can name 4-methyl-α-amylcinnamon aldehyde or mimosa heptanal (Acalea, Acalea TBHQ — IFF trade marks) — it has a flowery-powdery smell recalling heliotrope and mimosa with green aspects and fruity accents of berries and tropical fruits. And, of course, we can't forget about Mimosal, synthesized in 2008 by Robert Moretti, a chemist of Firmenich. This compound has a structure close to the classic lily-of-the-valley aldehydes: Mimosal has a bright lily-of-the-valley smell, both green and powdery, with an anise accent and watery effect; it can serve as the starting point for mimosa-lily-of-the-valley compositions.
 
In our culture, the mimosa note is appreciated first of all thanks to its resemblance to spring time flower bouquets, with the smell of Acacia Dealbata. That's why many Russian fragrances (Sweet Mimosa Pokrovka Trading House, Mimoza Dilis Parfum for women and the recent Brocard Mechta, as well as the quite similar Yves Rocher Pur Desir de Mimosa) recreate this very scent: cold, watery-cucumber, green, a bit of honey and just slightly dusty-powdery. I personally find this powdery aspect very pleasant and I'm happy to encounter it in fragrances, for example in Givenchy Amarige, Sisley Soir de Lune, Rochas Byzantine or Rochas Alchimie.
 
That's why, in order to contribute to this article, I had to dive into my perfume collection. The first mimosa-scent I noticed was Brocard's Mechta, mentioned above by Mat. And even though it's described as a combination of mimosa and linden, there is much more green-powdery mimosa in here than honey-like linden. Step by step Mechta becomes more matte than powdery, dusty like the dusty-green color of the mimosa leaves, but it sounds clear, though softer towards the end than at the beginning, for 3-4 hours minimum.
 
 
 
By Mat Yudov
