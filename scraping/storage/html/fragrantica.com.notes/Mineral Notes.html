 Histoires de Parfums This is Not A Blue Bottle 1.5

 Halloween Halloween Man X

 
 
male 2019
 
 
 
 
The world around us is full of all kinds of smells. We do not notice many of them, but if we start paying attention it turns out that not only flowers and smoked fish smell, but everything has at least some faint smell: plastic bags, old magazines and new magazines, sharpies, new clothes in stores, fresh laundry, glass cups, etc.
 
Once we talked about the smell of metal, and today I would like to discuss with you mineral "non-organic" smells. I put the word in quotes, because almost all non-organic substances do not have a smell, but the presence of volatile organic compounds, even in microquantities, make them smell. These organic compounds are the source of what we sense as "non-organic" smells. We know specific smells of kitchen salt, sand, concrete, rocks...
 
If you approach the question a little more carefully, you can learn to identify these nuances. One of my acquaintances was able to detect the smell of diluted solutions of non-organic salts by their smell to a great excitement of teachers of analytical chemistry.
 
 
Mineral notes in perfumery had not been considered as an expressive tool until recently. Really, who would like to smell like, say, rubble, when there are violets and Peru balsam?! But as it turned out many curious customers actually wanted that. A tremendous success of Comme des Garcons Odeur 53 from 1998, which was a blend of sand, metal, oxygen, hot rocks and who knows what else, if we trust the official press info, showed that the time to explore new perfume horizons had come.
 
In the debute perfume by Eau d'Italie (2000) created by Bertrand Duchaufour, we smell a wet clay accord, which was actually a part isolated from a complex smell of tuberose. Green floral and fruity tendrils adorn the central earthy and mineral terra cotta accord of Eau d'Italie.
 
 
Gradually, mineral notes began to appear in mass market perfumery. Who knows, maybe the addition of a specific mineral nuance (as they oficially put it as flintstone in the pyramid) was the cause of the colossal popularity of Jean-Claude Ellena's Terre d'Hermès. His daughter Celine Ellena has picked up the idea and created at least two remarkable things: The Different Company Sel de Vetiver where vetiver was placed in a surprising vegetal salty ambience, in which, by the way, a great part belongs to exotic essential oil of lovage, and L'Artisan Parfumeur Côte d'Amour, unfortunately discontinued, which very accurately recalled the smell of a drying beach towel on the hotel balcony.
 
Olivia Giacobetti created for her own brand Honore de Pres an extraordinary perfume Nu Green: striking green tarragon and mint notes are blended with a concrete dust of cedar and ambretta. It's interesting that the latter two fragrances I mentioned are completely natural and even Ecocert certified.
 
In the same style another work was done, it is Aura Maris where Lorenzo Villoresi is playing on the popular Italian subject of the sea and citruses. Another great mineral aquatic would be Wandervogel (former Wanderlust) from the niche brand 27 87, which I described in a separate overview of the brand.
 
 
The original version of Blood Concept AB from 2011 (sadly reformulated) was a rather abstract aldehydic-mineral-metalic composition, very much in agreement with its name and concept. Mineral notes can also be found in deliberately experimental works Nebulae Series by Oliver & Co., Aether, PMP Perfumes, and other brands open to risky ventures.
 
 
After the recent Pitti Fragranze we concluded that a mineral theme in perfumes was the most obvious among perfume adventurers. One of the main fragrances there, A Lab On Fire California Snow, was the best example. The debut work of Mackenzie Reilly, a rising star of IFF, impressed many Pitti attendees, who looked into the Unscent section at the show. Creators themselves define their work as "strange rose" among woody floral fragrances, but it actually does not give any idea at all of this very weird and pathologically attractive composition.
 
Its mineral carcass is clothed with natural material accords: sage, chamomile, rose, vetiver, cedar. All parts are held together by aldehydes, a salicilate block and a transparent musk accord, and exhibited over a big greasy blot of geosmin (fertile humus soil), which, as I thought, only Etat Libre d`Orange had succeded to tame in their Hermann A Mes Cotes Me Paraissait Une Ombre. In California Snow this ingredient was elegantly and smartly incorporated. As a result we got more than a perfume. We feel the fragrance in other ways, because our trigeminal nerve gets engaged providing tactile and temperature sensivity. When I smell it, I can feel a touch of snowflakes on my skin and a tickling in my nose, which very much resembles a fine powder in the nostrils.
 
 
The Italian brand Extrait d'Atelier showed their new Maitre Ceramiste fragrance to be launched next year. They put an accord of wet clay in the center of the composition. It is surrounded by green notes of galbanum, shiso and mint, as well as pink pepper, prickly aldehydes and metal notes. Incense and iris soften its unnatural mineral dullness. There is also an interesting mossy woody accord which advocates for it to be classified as a chypre. Chypre fragrances celebrate their 100th aniversary this year.
 
 
Aether already had one fragrance with a pronounced mineral character, it was musky ambery Muskethanol. In Florence they introduced another one named Celluloid, inspired by the smell of a film. Ethyl acetate in the top adds a special fruity sweetness, reminiscent of nail polish remover, and supports an ionone/iron complex. Iso E Super and Ambroxan hold it all up in the base. Cashmeran in a certain dosage could also produce a kind of mineral smell of wet concrete.
 
 
Parco 1923 is a new brand, created as a support to the national park Abruzzo. In their debut fragrance created by Luca Maffei, you can distinguish notes of exotic flowers and trees from this beautiful place. It is a delightful woody floral powdery perfume with a light mineral nuance. If you like juniper and angelica, you have to smell it.
 
 
Nomenclature is yet another promoter of synthetic smells, it came up with new Holy_wood, built on the newest Firmenich invention Clearwood. It is a transparent woody aroma with a patchouli character. I smelled it earlier at Esxence in Milan, but the final version is better balanced and logical. Earthy patchouli is enhanced by flowers and a suede accord. There is omnipresent pink pepper in its top, which communicating with the base gives a characteristic dusty minerality.
 
 
We can't skip a very much anticipated novelty Concrete by the trendsetters Comme des Garçons. It has already made its way to some stores.
 
 
The idea of the fragrance came to the creative director of CdG Christian Astuguevieille in the streets of the New York City. Although I doubt there will be any radical mineral turns and twists in it, which otherwise could be easily gathered from the streets of the Big Apple, but it seems that CdG has left its major experiments in the past. You can still enjoy them in Olfactory Library of re-launched CdG classics.
 
by Mat Yudov
